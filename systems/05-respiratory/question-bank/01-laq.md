### Q1. COPD — aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management (15m)
<!-- id: sys05-laq-q1 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management of chronic
obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), including acute exacerbation.

**Definition (2m)**
- COPD = a common, preventable, treatable disease characterised by **persistent respiratory symptoms and
  airflow limitation that is not fully reversible**, due to airway and/or alveolar abnormalities from
  significant exposure to noxious particles/gases. (1m)
- Comprises **chronic bronchitis** (clinical — productive cough ≥3 months/year for ≥2 consecutive years)
  and **emphysema** (pathological — permanent enlargement of airspaces distal to terminal bronchioles with
  alveolar wall destruction). (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (3m)**
- **Tobacco smoking** — the dominant cause; in India also **biomass-fuel smoke** (women) and occupational
  dust/fumes; **α1-antitrypsin deficiency** in young/non-smokers. (1m)
- Noxious exposure → **chronic airway inflammation** (neutrophils, macrophages, CD8 T-cells) → mucous-gland
  hyperplasia, small-airway fibrosis/narrowing and **protease–antiprotease imbalance** destroying alveolar
  walls. (1m)
- Result: **loss of elastic recoil + airway collapse on expiration → air-trapping and hyperinflation**;
  V/Q mismatch → hypoxaemia, later hypercapnia and cor pulmonale. (1m)

**Clinical features (3m)**
- **Chronic progressive dyspnoea**, productive cough, wheeze; history of smoking/biomass exposure. (1m)
- **Signs:** prolonged expiration, barrel chest, hyperresonance, reduced breath sounds, rhonchi; use of
  accessory muscles, pursed-lip breathing. (1m)
- **Advanced:** cyanosis, cor pulmonale (raised JVP, pedal oedema), weight loss; classically described as
  **"pink puffer"** (emphysema-predominant) vs **"blue bloater"** (bronchitis-predominant). (1m)

**Investigations (3m)**
- **Spirometry — confirms diagnosis: post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC < 0.70** (fixed airflow limitation);
  severity graded by **FEV1 % predicted (GOLD 1–4)**. (1m)
- **Chest X-ray** — hyperinflation (>6 anterior ribs, flat diaphragm), bullae, narrow cardiac shadow;
  excludes other causes. (1m)
- **Others:** ABG (type II respiratory failure in advanced disease), FBC (secondary polycythaemia), ECG/
  echo (cor pulmonale), α1-antitrypsin if young, sputum in exacerbation. (1m)

**Management (4m)**
- **Stable disease (the mark-magnet) (2m):** **smoking cessation** (single most effective — slows decline)
  + vaccination (influenza, pneumococcal); **inhaled bronchodilators** — **LAMA** (tiotropium) and/or
  **LABA** (formoterol/salmeterol); add **inhaled corticosteroid** for frequent exacerbations/eosinophilia;
  pulmonary rehabilitation; **long-term oxygen therapy (LTOT) if PaO2 ≤ 55 mmHg** (≥15 h/day improves
  survival). (2m)
- **Acute exacerbation (1½m):** controlled oxygen targeting **SpO2 88–92%** (avoid CO2 narcosis); nebulised
  **salbutamol + ipratropium**; **systemic corticosteroid — prednisolone 40 mg OD × 5 days**; **antibiotics**
  if increased sputum purulence/volume (e.g. amoxicillin-clavulanate or doxycycline); **NIV (BiPAP)** for
  persistent hypercapnic respiratory acidosis (pH < 7.35). (1½m)
- **Surgical/advanced (½m):** lung-volume-reduction surgery / bullectomy in selected patients;
  transplantation in end-stage disease. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — chronic bronchitis vs emphysema** ("blue bloater" vs "pink puffer": pathology, ABG, build) — ~1m
- [ ] **Flowchart — acute exacerbation pathway** (controlled O2 88–92% → nebs → steroid ± antibiotic → NIV) — ~1m

**Learn it (references — read, do not copy):**
- Harrison's — *Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease*. · Davidson's — *Respiratory → COPD*. · API Textbook
  of Medicine — *Respiratory → COPD*; GOLD report for current grading. [chapter refs — verify current edition]

### Q2. Bronchial asthma including acute severe asthma — aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management (15m)
<!-- id: sys05-laq-q2 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management of bronchial
asthma, including the management of acute severe asthma.

**Definition (2m)**
- Asthma = a chronic **inflammatory** disorder of the airways with **reversible airflow obstruction** and
  **bronchial hyper-responsiveness** to various stimuli. (1m)
- Characterised clinically by recurrent, **variable** episodes of wheeze, breathlessness, chest tightness
  and cough (worse at night/early morning), with largely reversible airflow limitation. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (3m)**
- **Atopy** is the strongest risk factor (personal/family history of allergic rhinitis, eczema); triggers —
  allergens (dust mite, pollen, pets), respiratory infections, cold air, exercise, smoke, NSAIDs,
  β-blockers. (1m)
- **Type-1 hypersensitivity:** allergen → IgE on mast cells → degranulation (histamine, leukotrienes,
  prostaglandins) → early bronchoconstriction; **late phase** — eosinophil/Th2-driven airway inflammation. (1m)
- Chronic inflammation → smooth-muscle hypertrophy, mucus hypersecretion, basement-membrane thickening
  (**airway remodelling**) → bronchial hyper-responsiveness and reversible obstruction. (1m)

**Clinical features (3m)**
- **Episodic** wheeze, dyspnoea, chest tightness and cough with **diurnal variation** (worse at night/early
  morning) and identifiable triggers; symptom-free between attacks. (1½m)
- **Examination (acute):** tachypnoea, prolonged expiration, widespread polyphonic **rhonchi/wheeze**;
  **silent chest, cyanosis, exhaustion and bradycardia are signs of life-threatening attack**. (1½m)

**Investigations (3m)**
- **Spirometry** — obstructive pattern (FEV1/FVC reduced) with **reversibility: ≥12% and ≥200 mL rise in
  FEV1** after bronchodilator. (1m)
- **PEFR** — diurnal/serial variability supports diagnosis and monitors control/severity. (1m)
- **Supportive:** raised FeNO, blood/sputum eosinophilia, total IgE, allergen skin tests; CXR usually
  normal (excludes complications/other diagnoses). (1m)

**Management (4m)**
- **Chronic — stepwise control (the mark-magnet) (2m):** **inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) is the cornerstone**
  controller; current GINA prefers **ICS-formoterol** as reliever (anti-inflammatory reliever) rather than
  SABA alone. Step up: low-dose ICS → ICS-LABA → medium/high-dose ICS-LABA → add-on LAMA/biologics
  (anti-IgE omalizumab, anti-IL5) + short oral steroid; **inhaler technique, adherence and trigger
  avoidance** at every step. (2m)
- **Acute severe asthma — emergency (1½m):** high-flow **oxygen (target SpO2 94–98%)**; **nebulised
  salbutamol** (continuous/back-to-back) **+ ipratropium bromide**; **systemic steroid — prednisolone
  40–50 mg PO or IV hydrocortisone 100 mg**; **IV magnesium sulphate 1.2–2 g over 20 min** in life-
  threatening/poor-response cases; consider IV aminophylline; reassess with PEFR/ABG; **ICU/ventilation** if
  deteriorating (rising CO2). (1½m)
- **⚠ Danger sign (½m):** in acute asthma a **normal or rising PaCO2 = exhaustion/impending respiratory
  failure** → senior/ICU input immediately. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — acute asthma severity grading** (moderate / severe / life-threatening: PEFR, SpO2, speech,
  silent chest, CO2) — ~1m
- [ ] **Flowchart — stepwise chronic control** (ICS → ICS-LABA → add-on/biologic) — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Asthma*. · Davidson's — *Respiratory → Asthma*. · API — *Respiratory → Bronchial
asthma*; GINA report for current step-up scheme. [verify current edition]

### Q3. Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) — aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management (10m)
<!-- id: sys05-laq-q3 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management of acute
respiratory distress syndrome.

**Definition (2m)**
- ARDS = an acute, diffuse **inflammatory lung injury** causing **non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema**,
  refractory hypoxaemia and bilateral infiltrates. (1m)
- **Berlin definition:** onset **within 1 week** of a known insult; **bilateral opacities** on imaging not
  fully explained by effusion/collapse; respiratory failure not fully explained by cardiac failure/fluid
  overload; **hypoxaemia graded by PaO2/FiO2** (mild 200–300, moderate 100–200, severe ≤100, on PEEP ≥5). (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (2m)**
- **Causes — direct lung injury:** pneumonia, aspiration, near-drowning, inhalation injury; **indirect:**
  sepsis (commonest), severe trauma, pancreatitis, massive transfusion, shock. (1m)
- **Mechanism:** insult → diffuse alveolar–capillary membrane damage → increased permeability →
  protein-rich **exudative oedema**, hyaline membranes and surfactant loss → reduced compliance, V/Q
  mismatch and severe hypoxaemia (exudative → proliferative → fibrotic phases). (1m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- **Acute severe dyspnoea, tachypnoea and refractory hypoxaemia** developing over hours–days after the
  precipitant; cyanosis, bilateral crepitations. (1m)
- **No clinical features of heart failure** (no raised JVP/gallop attributable to LV failure); the patient
  is typically critically ill with the underlying cause (e.g. sepsis). (1m)

**Investigations (2m)**
- **Chest X-ray / CT — bilateral diffuse infiltrates**; **ABG — severe hypoxaemia with low PaO2/FiO2**. (1m)
- **Echocardiography** to exclude cardiogenic oedema (normal LV function/no raised filling pressures);
  work-up for the precipitating cause (cultures, lipase, etc.). (1m)

**Management (2m)**
- **Treat the underlying cause** (e.g. antibiotics for sepsis) + supportive ICU care. (½m)
- **Lung-protective ventilation (the mark-magnet):** **low tidal volume 6 mL/kg predicted body weight**,
  **plateau pressure < 30 cmH2O**, adequate **PEEP**, permissive hypercapnia; **prone positioning** for
  severe ARDS (PaO2/FiO2 < 150); conservative fluid strategy; **neuromuscular blockade / ECMO** in refractory
  cases. (1½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — Berlin severity grading** (PaO2/FiO2 mild/moderate/severe) — ~1m
- [ ] **Table — ARDS vs cardiogenic pulmonary oedema** (mechanism, JVP, echo, wedge pressure) — ~½m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome*. · Davidson's — *Respiratory failure &
ARDS*. · API — *Critical Care → ARDS*. [verify current edition]

### Q4. Pulmonary tuberculosis — diagnosis and treatment (NTEP) (15m)
<!-- id: sys05-laq-q4 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the diagnosis and treatment of pulmonary tuberculosis under the National Tuberculosis
Elimination Programme (NTEP, formerly RNTCP).

**Definition (2m)**
- Pulmonary TB = chronic granulomatous infection of the lung parenchyma by ***Mycobacterium tuberculosis***,
  spread by **airborne droplet nuclei**. (1m)
- A leading communicable cause of death in India; the national programme is the **NTEP (formerly RNTCP)**,
  which targets TB elimination using free diagnosis and treatment. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (3m)**
- Inhaled bacilli reach alveoli → engulfed by macrophages → **primary (Ghon) focus** with hilar node =
  **Ghon/primary complex**; cell-mediated (Th1) immunity walls it off as a **caseating granuloma**. (1½m)
- **Post-primary (reactivation)** TB — typically **upper-lobe apical**, occurring with waning immunity
  (HIV, diabetes, malnutrition, immunosuppression, elderly); cavitation → highly infectious sputum. (1m)
- **Transmission** by droplet nuclei from a smear-positive case; risk factors — overcrowding, poverty,
  HIV, diabetes, silicosis. (½m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- **Cough ≥2 weeks**, evening-rise **fever**, **night sweats**, **weight loss/anorexia**,
  **haemoptysis**. (1m)
- Signs may be minimal; apical crepitations, signs of consolidation/cavitation/effusion;
  extra-pulmonary involvement if disseminated. (1m)

**Investigations / diagnosis (3m)**
- **NAAT (CBNAAT / GeneXpert MTB/RIF) on sputum** is the **NTEP first-line/upfront diagnostic** — detects
  *M. tuberculosis* **and rifampicin resistance** rapidly; also sputum **AFB smear (ZN/fluorescent
  microscopy)** and **culture (gold standard, also for drug-susceptibility testing)**. (1½m)
- **Chest X-ray** — upper-lobe infiltrates, cavitation, fibrosis, miliary mottling. (1m)
- Baseline before therapy: **HIV testing, blood glucose**, weight, LFT/RFT, vision (for ethambutol). (½m)

**Management / treatment under NTEP (5m)**
- **Daily regimen with fixed-dose combinations, weight-band based (the mark-magnet) (2m):**
  - **Intensive phase (2 months): HRZE** — **H**(isoniazid) + **R**(ifampicin) + **Z**(pyrazinamide) +
    **E**(thambutol), daily. (1m)
  - **Continuation phase (4 months): HRE** daily → **total 6 months** for drug-sensitive pulmonary TB. (1m)
- **Drug-resistant TB (1m):** rifampicin-resistant/MDR-TB confirmed on NAAT → shift to a **programmatic
  longer/shorter MDR regimen** (e.g. bedaquiline-containing) under PMDT — refer; do not treat with first-line
  drugs alone. (1m)
- **Monitoring & adjuncts (1m):** follow-up sputum smear at end of intensive phase and at completion;
  **pyridoxine (vitamin B6)** with isoniazid to prevent neuropathy; monitor for **hepatotoxicity** (H, R, Z);
  **ethambutol → optic neuritis** (check vision); record on **Ni-kshay** with treatment supporter/DOT. (1m)
- **Public-health measures (1m):** notification (TB is notifiable), contact tracing/screening, airborne
  infection control, nutritional support (**Ni-kshay Poshan Yojana**), HIV co-management. (1m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — NTEP regimen** (intensive HRZE × 2m → continuation HRE × 4m; total 6m) — ~1m
- [ ] **Table — anti-TB drug toxicities** (H→neuropathy/hepatitis, R→hepatitis/orange urine, Z→hepatitis/
  hyperuricaemia, E→optic neuritis) — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Tuberculosis*. · Davidson's — *Respiratory → Tuberculosis*. · API — *Infectious
diseases → Tuberculosis*; current **NTEP/Index-TB / national TB guidelines**. [verify current edition —
programme regimens are periodically revised]

### Q5. Community-acquired pneumonia — aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management (10m)
<!-- id: sys05-laq-q5 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management of
community-acquired pneumonia (CAP).

**Definition (2m)**
- Pneumonia = acute infection of the **lung parenchyma (alveoli)** causing consolidation, with fever,
  respiratory symptoms and new infiltrates on imaging. (1m)
- **Community-acquired** = acquired outside hospital (or within 48 h of admission), as distinct from
  hospital-acquired/ventilator-associated pneumonia. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (2m)**
- **Commonest organism: *Streptococcus pneumoniae*** (typical); others — *Haemophilus influenzae*,
  *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Klebsiella*; **atypicals** — *Mycoplasma*, *Chlamydophila*, *Legionella*;
  viral (influenza). (1m)
- Organisms reach lower airways (micro-aspiration/inhalation) → overwhelm defences → alveolar inflammatory
  exudate → **consolidation** and V/Q mismatch; predisposing factors — extremes of age, smoking, COPD,
  diabetes, immunosuppression, aspiration. (1m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- **Fever with chills/rigors, productive cough (rusty/purulent sputum), pleuritic chest pain,
  breathlessness**. (1m)
- **Signs of consolidation:** tachypnoea, reduced expansion, **dull percussion**, **bronchial breathing**,
  crepitations, increased vocal resonance; tachycardia, hypoxia in severe cases. (1m)

**Investigations (2m)**
- **Chest X-ray — confirms diagnosis** (lobar/patchy consolidation ± effusion); **FBC** (neutrophil
  leucocytosis), CRP, urea, electrolytes (urea feeds CURB-65). (1m)
- **Microbiology:** sputum Gram stain/culture, blood cultures, pulse oximetry/ABG; urinary antigen
  (pneumococcal/Legionella) in severe cases. (1m)

**Management (2m)**
- **Assess severity with CURB-65** (see `03-criteria.md`) to decide outpatient vs admission vs ICU. (½m)
- **Empirical antibiotics (the mark-magnet):** **mild/outpatient — oral amoxicillin** (± macrolide for
  atypical cover); **moderate-severe/admitted — IV co-amoxiclav/ceftriaxone + a macrolide** (e.g.
  azithromycin); de-escalate to culture-guided therapy; usual duration **5–7 days**. (1m)
- **Supportive:** oxygen (target SpO2 94–98%), fluids, antipyretics/analgesia, VTE prophylaxis; drain
  significant **parapneumonic effusion/empyema**; vaccination (pneumococcal, influenza) for prevention. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — typical vs atypical organisms** (pathogen, features, antibiotic) — ~1m
- [ ] **Flowchart — CURB-65 score → site of care** (0–1 home / 2 ward / ≥3 ICU) — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Pneumonia*. · Davidson's — *Respiratory → Pneumonia*. · API — *Respiratory →
Community-acquired pneumonia*. [verify current edition]
