### Q1. Malaria — life cycle, clinical features, complications and treatment including cerebral malaria (15m)
<!-- id: sys07-laq-q1 -->

**Restated:** Describe the life cycle of the malarial parasite. Discuss the clinical features, complications
and treatment of malaria, with emphasis on severe/cerebral malaria.

**Definition (1m)**
- Malaria = a protozoal infection by **Plasmodium** species (*P. falciparum, P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae,
  P. knowlesi*) transmitted by the bite of an infected female **Anopheles** mosquito; *P. falciparum* causes
  most severe disease and deaths. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis — life cycle (3m)**
- **Exo-erythrocytic (hepatic) phase (1m):** the mosquito injects **sporozoites** → invade hepatocytes →
  multiply into **schizonts** → release **merozoites**. *P. vivax/ovale* form dormant **hypnozoites**
  (cause relapse). (1m)
- **Erythrocytic phase (1m):** merozoites invade RBCs → ring → trophozoite → schizont → rupture releases
  fresh merozoites (synchronous rupture = periodic **fever paroxysms**); some form **gametocytes**. (1m)
- **Sporogony in mosquito + falciparum pathology (1m):** gametocytes taken up by the mosquito complete the
  sexual cycle. *P. falciparum* causes **cytoadherence/sequestration** of parasitised RBCs in cerebral and
  other microvasculature → the basis of severe disease. (1m)

**Clinical features (3m)**
- **Classic paroxysm (1½m):** cold stage (rigor) → hot stage (high fever) → sweating stage; periodicity —
  **tertian** (48 h: vivax/ovale/falciparum) or **quartan** (72 h: malariae). With anaemia, splenomegaly,
  hepatomegaly. (1½m)
- **Severe (falciparum) features (1½m):** impaired consciousness (**cerebral malaria**), seizures, severe
  anaemia, **hypoglycaemia**, metabolic acidosis/respiratory distress, AKI, **blackwater fever** (massive
  intravascular haemolysis → haemoglobinuria), jaundice, shock, DIC, pulmonary oedema/ARDS,
  **hyperparasitaemia (>10%)**. (1½m)

**Investigations (3m)**
- **Peripheral smear (thick + thin)** — the gold standard: thick film for detection, **thin film for species
  + parasite count**; repeat 6–12 hourly if first negative. (1m)
- **Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs)** — antigen detection (HRP-2 for falciparum, pLDH); useful where
  microscopy unavailable. (1m)
- **Supportive bloods:** FBC (anaemia, thrombocytopenia), glucose (hypoglycaemia), renal/liver function,
  lactate, blood gas; G6PD status before primaquine. (1m)

**Management (5m)**
- **Uncomplicated falciparum (1m):** **artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT)** — e.g.
  **artemether-lumefantrine** for 3 days; add a **single gametocidal dose of primaquine** (WHO: single low
  dose 0.25 mg/kg, G6PD-safe; older NVBDCP regimen used 0.75 mg/kg). (1m)
- **Vivax/ovale (1m):** **chloroquine** (where sensitive) for the blood stage **+ primaquine 0.25 mg/kg/day
  × 14 days** for radical cure of hypnozoites (check **G6PD** first — risk of haemolysis). (1m)
- **Severe / cerebral malaria — emergency (the mark-magnet) (2m):** **IV artesunate 2.4 mg/kg at 0, 12, 24 h
  then daily**, switch to a full oral ACT course once able to take orally (artesunate is superior to
  quinine — SEAQUAMAT/AQUAMAT). (1m)
  - **Supportive:** ICU; treat **hypoglycaemia** (IV dextrose), seizures (benzodiazepine), fluids cautiously
    (avoid pulmonary oedema), transfuse for severe anaemia, dialysis for AKI; monitor parasitaemia. **Do NOT
    give steroids in cerebral malaria** (harmful). (1m)
- **Prevention (1m):** vector control (insecticide-treated nets, indoor spraying), chemoprophylaxis for
  travellers, prompt treatment to cut transmission. (1m)

### Q2. Dengue — classification, warning signs and management (15m)
<!-- id: sys07-laq-q2 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, WHO classification, clinical features (with warning signs) and
management of dengue fever.

**Definition (1m)**
- Dengue = an acute arboviral infection caused by the **dengue virus** (a flavivirus, 4 serotypes
  **DENV 1–4**) transmitted by the day-biting *Aedes aegypti* mosquito; spectrum ranges from a self-limiting
  febrile illness to severe dengue with shock/haemorrhage. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (3m)**
- **Viral entry + viraemia (1m):** *Aedes* bite inoculates virus → replication in monocytes/macrophages →
  viraemia drives the febrile phase. (1m)
- **Plasma leakage (1m):** immune activation (cytokines) → **increased capillary permeability** → plasma
  leak into pleural/peritoneal spaces, haemoconcentration and hypovolaemia — the hallmark of severe
  dengue. (1m)
- **Severe disease mechanism (1m):** secondary infection with a **different serotype** → **antibody-dependent
  enhancement (ADE)** → higher risk of severe dengue; thrombocytopenia + coagulopathy → bleeding. (1m)

**Clinical features (3m)**
- **Febrile phase (1m):** sudden high fever, severe headache, **retro-orbital pain, myalgia/arthralgia
  ("breakbone fever")**, rash, leucopenia; lasts 2–7 days. (1m)
- **Critical phase (around defervescence, day 3–7) (1m):** plasma leakage → effusions, ascites; **warning
  signs** appear here; risk of shock. (1m)
- **Warning signs (1m):** abdominal pain/tenderness, persistent vomiting, clinical fluid accumulation,
  mucosal bleeding, lethargy/restlessness, **liver enlargement >2 cm**, rising haematocrit with rapid fall
  in platelets. (1m)

**Investigations (3m)**
- **Diagnosis (1½m):** **NS1 antigen** (positive early, days 1–5); **IgM/IgG serology** (after day 5);
  RT-PCR in early phase. (1½m)
- **Monitoring (1½m):** **serial CBC** — falling platelets and **rising haematocrit** (≥20% rise signals
  plasma leak); LFTs (transaminitis), coagulation; ultrasound for effusions/ascites/gallbladder wall
  oedema. (1½m)

**Management (5m)**
- **No specific antiviral — fluid management is the core (2m):** group by WHO category. **Group A (no warning
  signs):** oral fluids, paracetamol, home care with daily review. **Group B (warning signs / co-morbidity):**
  admit, **IV isotonic crystalloid** (e.g. 0.9% saline / Ringer lactate) titrated to haematocrit and urine
  output. (2m)
- **Severe dengue / shock (Group C) (1½m):** ICU; **rapid IV crystalloid boluses (10–20 mL/kg)**, reassess
  and step down as leakage resolves; colloids/blood for refractory shock or significant bleeding; avoid
  fluid overload during the **reabsorption phase**. (1½m)
- **Supportive + avoid harm (1m):** paracetamol for fever; **AVOID NSAIDs/aspirin and IM injections**
  (bleeding risk); **platelet transfusion only for significant bleeding or per threshold**, not for the
  number alone. (1m)
- **Monitoring/discharge (½m):** watch for the critical phase; discharge when afebrile, improving, platelets
  rising and no leakage. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — WHO dengue categories A / B / C** (criteria → action) — ~1m
- [ ] **Graph/diagram — phases of dengue** (febrile → critical/leak → recovery vs platelets/haematocrit) — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Flaviviruses / Dengue*. · Davidson's — *Viral infections → Dengue*. · API —
*Tropical → Dengue*; WHO dengue guidelines. [verify current edition]

### Q3. Enteric fever (typhoid) — aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management (10m)
<!-- id: sys07-laq-q3 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management of enteric
(typhoid) fever.

**Definition (1m)**
- Enteric fever = a systemic febrile illness caused by **Salmonella enterica serotype Typhi** (and
  Paratyphi A/B/C), spread by the **faeco-oral route** (contaminated food/water); a common cause of
  prolonged fever in endemic India. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (2m)**
- Ingested bacilli survive gastric acid → invade **Peyer's patches** of the terminal ileum → spread to
  mesenteric nodes → **primary bacteraemia** → seeding of the reticulo-endothelial system (liver, spleen,
  gallbladder). (1m)
- **Secondary bacteraemia** produces the clinical illness; gallbladder colonisation causes the **chronic
  carrier** state. Incubation ~1–2 weeks. (1m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- **Stepladder fever** (progressively rising), headache, malaise, anorexia, **relative bradycardia
  (Faget's sign)**, coated tongue, abdominal pain, constipation or diarrhoea. (1m)
- **Signs:** **rose spots**, **hepatosplenomegaly**; complications in the **3rd week** — **intestinal
  perforation/haemorrhage** (from Peyer's-patch necrosis), encephalopathy ("typhoid state"), myocarditis. (1m)

**Investigations (2m)**
- **Blood culture** — the **diagnostic gold standard**, highest yield in the **first week**; bone-marrow
  culture is most sensitive (esp. after antibiotics); stool/urine culture positive later. (1m)
- **Widal test** — supportive only (rising titres), limited specificity in endemic areas; FBC may show
  leucopenia with relative lymphocytosis; LFT mild derangement. (1m)

**Management (3m)**
- **Antibiotics (2m):** guided by local resistance. **Ceftriaxone 2 g IV once daily × 10–14 days** for severe
  illness; **azithromycin 500 mg–1 g/day × 7 days** (good for uncomplicated and MDR strains);
  **fluoroquinolones** only where susceptible (rising resistance). Treat the **carrier** with a prolonged
  course (± cholecystectomy if gallstones). (2m)
- **Supportive + complications (1m):** hydration, antipyretics, nutrition; **monitor for perforation/bleeding**
  (acute abdomen → surgical referral); dexamethasone for severe typhoid with shock/encephalopathy; prevent
  with sanitation, safe water and **typhoid vaccination**. (1m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Timeline — weekly course** (week 1 bacteraemia → week 2 rose spots/organomegaly → week 3 complications) — ~1m
- [ ] **Table — investigations by week** (blood culture / Widal / stool culture yield) — ~½m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Salmonellosis / Typhoid fever*. · Davidson's — *Enteric fever*. · API —
*Tropical → Enteric fever*. [verify current edition]

### Q4. Leptospirosis — aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management (10m)
<!-- id: sys07-laq-q4 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management of
leptospirosis, including Weil's disease.

**Definition (1m)**
- Leptospirosis = a **zoonotic** infection by the spirochaete **Leptospira interrogans**, acquired through
  contact of skin/mucosa with water or soil contaminated by the **urine of infected animals (esp. rodents)**;
  common after floods and in agricultural/sewage workers. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (2m)**
- Leptospires penetrate abraded skin/mucous membranes → **leptospiraemia** → disseminate widely. (1m)
- Cause a **systemic vasculitis with endothelial damage** → capillary leak, affecting kidneys (interstitial
  nephritis), liver, lungs and muscles; severe form = **Weil's disease** (jaundice + AKI + bleeding). (1m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- **Biphasic illness (1m):** septicaemic phase — abrupt fever, severe **myalgia (calf)**, headache, and the
  characteristic **conjunctival suffusion**; then an immune phase. (1m)
- **Severe / Weil's disease (1m):** **jaundice, acute kidney injury, haemorrhage** (incl. pulmonary
  haemorrhage), myocarditis, aseptic meningitis. (1m)

**Investigations (2m)**
- **Serology — MAT (microscopic agglutination test)** is the reference standard (paired sera); **IgM ELISA**
  for early diagnosis; PCR in the first week. (1m)
- **Supportive:** neutrophil leucocytosis, thrombocytopenia, raised bilirubin with relatively modest
  transaminases, raised CK, deranged renal function. (1m)

**Management (3m)**
- **Antibiotics (1½m):** mild — **doxycycline 100 mg BD × 7 days** (or azithromycin/amoxicillin);
  **severe — IV penicillin G** (1.5 MU 6-hourly) **or IV ceftriaxone 1 g daily**; start early (a
  **Jarisch-Herxheimer** reaction may follow). (1½m)
- **Supportive (1m):** ICU for severe disease; **dialysis for AKI**, manage pulmonary haemorrhage and
  bleeding, fluids and organ support. (1m)
- **Prevention (½m):** rodent control, protective clothing, **doxycycline 200 mg weekly** chemoprophylaxis
  for high-risk exposure. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — Weil's disease triad** (jaundice + AKI + haemorrhage) and organ involvement — ~1m
- [ ] **Flowchart — biphasic course** (septicaemic phase → immune phase) — ~½m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Leptospirosis*. · Davidson's — *Leptospirosis* (zoonoses). · API —
*Tropical → Leptospirosis*. [verify current edition]

### Q5. Approach to a patient with fever and thrombocytopenia (10m)
<!-- id: sys07-laq-q5 -->

**Restated:** Outline a clinical approach to a patient presenting with acute febrile illness and
thrombocytopenia.

**Definition / framing (1m)**
- **Fever with thrombocytopenia** = an acute febrile illness with platelet count **<150,000/µL** — a common
  emergency in tropical India where it signals several treatable infections; the aim is **rapid aetiological
  triage** and detection of bleeding/shock. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis — causes (3m)**
- **Tropical infections (2m):** **dengue** (most common), **malaria**, **leptospirosis**, **scrub typhus**,
  enteric fever, **chikungunya**, viral hepatitis; sepsis with DIC. (2m)
- **Non-tropical / other (1m):** other viral fevers, **DIC**, haematological causes (ITP, leukaemia, marrow
  failure), drug-induced, hypersplenism. Mechanisms — decreased production, increased destruction/consumption,
  or sequestration. (1m)

**Clinical features / assessment (2m)**
- **Focused history (1m):** travel/rural exposure, rash, eschar (scrub typhus), conjunctival suffusion/calf
  pain (lepto), arthralgia, bleeding, urine output; epidemiology/season. (1m)
- **Examination + danger signs (1m):** vitals (shock), **bleeding (petechiae, mucosal)**, hepatosplenomegaly,
  eschar, jaundice, signs of plasma leakage; assess for warning signs of severe dengue. (1m)

**Investigations (2m)**
- **First-line (1m):** **CBC with platelet count + peripheral smear** (malaria parasite, schistocytes),
  **dengue NS1/serology, malaria RDT**, urine output, haematocrit trend. (1m)
- **Second-line (1m):** **leptospira & scrub-typhus serology**, blood culture (enteric fever), LFT/RFT,
  coagulation profile (**PT/aPTT, fibrinogen, D-dimer** for DIC), Weil-Felix; repeat platelets to trend. (1m)

**Management (2m)**
- **Empirical + specific (1m):** resuscitate (fluids for leak/shock); start **empirical doxycycline** to
  cover scrub typhus/leptospirosis when undifferentiated, **antimalarials** if smear/RDT positive; treat the
  confirmed cause; **avoid NSAIDs/IM injections**. (1m)
- **Platelet support + monitoring (1m):** **transfuse platelets only for significant bleeding or very low
  counts per threshold** (not the number alone); serial monitoring for bleeding, organ failure and DIC;
  escalate to ICU as needed. (1m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — fever + thrombocytopenia: cause ⇒ clue ⇒ test** (dengue/malaria/lepto/scrub/enteric) — ~1m
- [ ] **Flowchart — triage algorithm** (history/exam → first-line tests → empirical Rx → specific Rx) — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Approach to the Patient with Fever* / relevant tropical chapters. · Davidson's —
*Fever of undetermined cause / Infection*. · API — *Approach to acute febrile illness / Fever with
thrombocytopenia*. [verify current edition]
