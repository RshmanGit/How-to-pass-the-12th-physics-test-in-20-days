### Q1. Sepsis and septic shock — definition, pathogenesis and Surviving Sepsis management (10m)
<!-- id: sys11-q1 -->

**Restated:** Define sepsis and septic shock. Discuss their pathogenesis, clinical features, investigations
and management as per the Surviving Sepsis Campaign.

**Definition (2m)**
- **Sepsis** = **life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection**
  (Sepsis-3); operationally an acute rise in **SOFA score ≥2** points due to infection. **qSOFA** (≥2 of:
  RR ≥22/min, altered mentation, SBP ≤100 mmHg) is a bedside prompt to look harder, not a diagnostic score. (1m)
- **Septic shock** = a subset of sepsis with **circulatory and cellular/metabolic abnormality** profound
  enough to substantially increase mortality — clinically, **persisting hypotension requiring vasopressors
  to keep MAP ≥65 mmHg AND lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation**. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (2m)**
- Common sources: **lung (commonest), abdomen, urinary tract, skin/soft tissue, lines/devices**; organisms
  gram-positive, gram-negative or fungal. (½m)
- Pathogen-associated molecular patterns trigger an **exaggerated innate immune response** — cytokine surge
  (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6), complement and coagulation activation. (½m)
- **Endothelial injury** → capillary leak, vasodilatation and microvascular thrombosis →
  **distributive shock** with maldistributed flow and **tissue hypoperfusion** (raised lactate). (½m)
- Result is **multi-organ dysfunction** — ARDS, acute kidney injury, DIC, hepatic and CNS dysfunction. (½m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- Source-specific features + **SIRS-type signs**: fever or hypothermia, tachycardia, tachypnoea. (½m)
- **Hypoperfusion:** hypotension, oliguria, altered mentation, mottled/cool peripheries; in **warm**
  early septic shock peripheries may be flushed with wide pulse pressure. (1m)
- **Organ dysfunction:** hypoxaemia (ARDS), rising creatinine, coagulopathy/bleeding, ileus, jaundice. (½m)

**Investigations (2m)**
- **Serum lactate** (perfusion marker, guides resuscitation) and **2 sets of blood cultures before
  antibiotics** (do not delay antibiotics >45 min for cultures). (1m)
- Source work-up: cultures of urine/sputum/CSF/pus, **imaging** (CXR, USG/CT abdomen) for a drainable focus. (½m)
- Organ-function panel: ABG, FBC, renal/liver function, coagulation profile + fibrinogen, CRP/procalcitonin. (½m)

**Management — Surviving Sepsis "Hour-1 bundle" (the mark-magnet) (2m)**
- **Measure lactate** (repeat if >2 to guide resuscitation). (¼m)
- **Obtain blood cultures before antibiotics.** (¼m)
- **Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics within 1 hour** — empirical, source/local-resistance guided (e.g.
  **piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV** ± an aminoglycoside; add vancomycin if MRSA risk); **de-escalate on
  cultures**. (½m)
- **Fluid resuscitation: 30 mL/kg IV crystalloid** (balanced solution preferred) for hypotension or
  lactate ≥4 mmol/L, given as boluses with reassessment. (½m)
- **Vasopressors if MAP <65 mmHg** despite fluids — **noradrenaline first-line** (add vasopressin, then
  consider hydrocortisone 200 mg/day in refractory shock). (½m)
- **Source control** (drain abscess, remove infected line) as early as feasible; supportive organ care in ICU. (—)

- [ ] **Box — Hour-1 bundle** (lactate · cultures · antibiotics · 30 mL/kg fluid · vasopressor for MAP <65) — ~1m
- [ ] **Table — Sepsis-3 definitions** (SIRS vs sepsis vs septic shock, with qSOFA/lactate cut-offs) — ~1m

### Q2. Shock — definition, classification and clinical approach (10m)
<!-- id: sys11-q2 -->

**Restated:** Define shock. Classify the types of shock and discuss the clinical approach to a patient in
shock.

**Definition (2m)**
- **Shock** = acute **circulatory failure** causing inadequate tissue **oxygen delivery/perfusion** relative
  to demand, leading to cellular hypoxia, anaerobic metabolism (**raised lactate**) and, if uncorrected,
  multi-organ failure. (1m)
- It is **not defined by blood pressure alone** — shock can exist with a normal BP (compensated) and
  hypotension can occur without shock. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis / classification (3m)**
- **Hypovolaemic** — ↓ intravascular volume: haemorrhage, GI losses, burns, dehydration. Low CVP/preload. (¾m)
- **Cardiogenic** — pump failure: acute MI, arrhythmia, severe valve disease, cardiomyopathy. High filling
  pressures, low output. (¾m)
- **Distributive** — pathological vasodilatation/maldistribution: **septic** (commonest), anaphylactic,
  neurogenic. Low SVR, **warm** peripheries early. (¾m)
- **Obstructive** — mechanical obstruction to flow: **tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade, massive
  pulmonary embolism**. (¾m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- **Hypoperfusion signs:** tachycardia, hypotension, narrow pulse pressure, **prolonged capillary refill**,
  cool clammy skin (warm in distributive), oliguria, altered mentation. (1m)
- **Pointers to type:** raised JVP + lung crepitations (cardiogenic), distended neck veins + absent breath
  sounds (tension pneumothorax) or muffled heart sounds (tamponade), bleeding/fluid loss (hypovolaemic),
  fever/source + warm flush (septic). (1m)

**Investigations (1m)**
- **Lactate + ABG** (severity/perfusion), FBC, renal/liver function, coagulation, group & save; **ECG +
  troponin** (cardiogenic), bedside **echo/POCUS** and CXR to define the mechanism; cultures if septic. (1m)

**Management (2m)**
- **ABC + restore perfusion (1m):** high-flow oxygen, two large-bore IV cannulae; **fluid challenge**
  (crystalloid bolus) for hypovolaemic/distributive — **caution in cardiogenic**; target **MAP ≥65 mmHg**
  and urine output; **vasopressors** (noradrenaline) if fluid-refractory. (1m)
- **Treat the cause (1m):** stop haemorrhage/transfuse; revascularise MI ± inotropes/mechanical support
  (cardiogenic); **needle/finger decompression** for tension pneumothorax, **pericardiocentesis** for
  tamponade, anticoagulate/lyse massive PE; antibiotics + source control for sepsis; adrenaline for
  anaphylaxis. (1m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — four shock types** (cause · CVP/preload · cardiac output · SVR · skin) — ~1½m
- [ ] **Flowchart — approach to shock** (recognise → ABC → fluid challenge → vasopressor → treat cause) — ~½m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Approach to the Patient with Shock*. · Davidson's — *Shock & resuscitation*. ·
API — *Critical Care → Shock*. [verify current edition]

### Q3. Short note: Arterial blood gas (ABG) / acid-base interpretation (5m)
<!-- id: sys11-q3 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on the stepwise interpretation of an arterial blood gas / acid-base
disorder.

- **Normal values (1m):** **pH 7.35–7.45**, **PaCO₂ 35–45 mmHg**, **HCO₃⁻ 22–26 mmol/L**, PaO₂ 80–100 mmHg,
  base excess ±2. (1m)
- **Stepwise approach (2m):**
  - **Step 1 — pH:** <7.35 acidaemia, >7.45 alkalaemia. (½m)
  - **Step 2 — primary disturbance:** match the deranged value — **CO₂** moves with pH oppositely in a
    **respiratory** problem; **HCO₃⁻** moves with pH in the same direction in a **metabolic** problem. (½m)
  - **Step 3 — compensation:** check whether the other system has shifted appropriately (e.g. metabolic
    acidosis → expected fall in CO₂ by **Winter's formula: PaCO₂ = 1.5 × HCO₃⁻ + 8 ± 2**). (½m)
  - **Step 4 — anion gap** in metabolic acidosis: **AG = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻)**, normal **8–12 mmol/L**. (½m)
- **Causes (1m):** **High-AG metabolic acidosis (MUDPILES)** — methanol, uraemia, DKA, propylene glycol,
  isoniazid/iron, lactic acidosis, ethylene glycol, salicylates; **normal-AG (hyperchloraemic)** — diarrhoea,
  renal tubular acidosis. (1m)
- **Worked example (1m):** pH 7.20, PaCO₂ 25, HCO₃⁻ 10, Na⁺ 140, Cl⁻ 100 → acidaemia; low HCO₃⁻ =
  **metabolic acidosis**; expected CO₂ = 1.5 × 10 + 8 = **23 ± 2** (measured 25 ≈ appropriate respiratory
  compensation); AG = 140 − (100 + 10) = **30 (high)** → **high-AG metabolic acidosis** (e.g. DKA/lactate). (1m)

**What to tabulate:** [ ] **Primary disorder → expected compensation** (the 4 disorders + Winter's formula) — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Acidosis and Alkalosis*. · Davidson's — *Acid–base disorders*. ·
API — *Fluid, electrolyte & acid-base*. [verify current edition]

### Q4. Short note: Organophosphate (OP) poisoning (5m)
<!-- id: sys11-q4 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on the clinical features and management of organophosphate poisoning.

- **Definition / mechanism (1m):** OP compounds (insecticides) **irreversibly inhibit acetylcholinesterase**,
  causing **acetylcholine accumulation** at muscarinic, nicotinic and CNS synapses → a cholinergic crisis. (1m)
- **Clinical features (1½m):**
  - **Muscarinic (DUMBELS):** Diarrhoea, Urination, Miosis, Bronchorrhoea/Bronchospasm, Emesis,
    Lacrimation, Salivation/Sweating — **bronchorrhoea + bradycardia are the killers**. (¾m)
  - **Nicotinic:** fasciculations, muscle weakness, paralysis (respiratory failure); **CNS:** agitation,
    seizures, coma. A **garlic odour** and the toxidrome clinch it. (¾m)
- **Investigations (½m):** clinical diagnosis; **plasma/RBC cholinesterase low** (supports, guides
  recovery); monitor ABG/SpO₂ for respiratory failure. (½m)
- **Management (2m):**
  - **ABC** — secure airway, **suction secretions, high-flow O₂**; decontaminate (remove clothing, wash
    skin); intubate/ventilate for respiratory failure. (½m)
  - **Atropine** — **2–4 mg IV bolus, doubled every 5 min** until **atropinisation** (dry chest/secretions
    cleared, HR >80, SBP >80) — titrate to chest, not pupils. (¾m)
  - **Pralidoxime (oxime, reactivates cholinesterase)** — **30 mg/kg IV loading** then infusion; most
    effective **before ageing** of the enzyme. (½m)
  - **Diazepam** for seizures/agitation; supportive ICU care. (¼m)

**What to tabulate:** [ ] **Muscarinic (DUMBELS) vs nicotinic vs CNS features** — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Poisoning & Drug Overdose*. · Davidson's — *Poisoning*. ·
API — *Toxicology → Organophosphates*. [verify current edition + dosing]

### Q5. Short note: Paracetamol (acetaminophen) poisoning (5m)
<!-- id: sys11-q5 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on the clinical course and management of paracetamol overdose.

- **Mechanism (1m):** in overdose, glutathione is depleted and the toxic metabolite **NAPQI** accumulates,
  causing **centrilobular hepatic necrosis**; hepatotoxic dose ≈ **>150 mg/kg** (or >10–12 g). (1m)
- **Clinical phases (1½m):**
  - **Phase 1 (0–24 h):** asymptomatic or nausea/vomiting, malaise. (½m)
  - **Phase 2 (24–72 h):** right-upper-quadrant pain, **rising transaminases**. (½m)
  - **Phase 3 (72–96 h):** peak hepatotoxicity — **fulminant hepatic failure**, encephalopathy, coagulopathy,
    AKI, lactic acidosis. **Phase 4:** recovery or death. (½m)
- **Investigations (½m):** **timed serum paracetamol level (≥4 h post-ingestion)** plotted on the
  **Rumack-Matthew nomogram**; LFTs, INR/PT, renal function, ABG, glucose. (½m)
- **Management (2m):**
  - **Activated charcoal 1 g/kg** if presenting **within 1 hour** of ingestion. (½m)
  - **N-acetylcysteine (NAC)** — antidote, replenishes glutathione; give if level is above the nomogram
    treatment line, staggered/late or high-risk presentation: **IV regimen 150 mg/kg over 1 h → 50 mg/kg
    over 4 h → 100 mg/kg over 16 h**; most effective **within 8 hours** but give late if indicated. (1m)
  - Refer for **liver transplant** by **King's College criteria** (e.g. pH <7.3, or the triad of INR >6.5 +
    creatinine >300 µmol/L + grade III–IV encephalopathy). (½m)

**What to tabulate:** [ ] **Four clinical phases (time vs features)**, and the NAC dosing schedule — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Acetaminophen Poisoning*. · Davidson's — *Poisoning → Paracetamol*. ·
API — *Toxicology*. [verify current edition + nomogram/dosing]

### Q6. Short note: Snake bite envenomation (5m)
<!-- id: sys11-q6 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on the clinical features and management of venomous snake bite.

- **Definition (1m):** systemic/local toxicity from envenomation by venomous snakes; India's "**big four**"
  — **cobra and krait (neurotoxic)**, **Russell's viper and saw-scaled viper (vasculotoxic/haemotoxic)**. (1m)
- **Clinical features (1½m):**
  - **Neurotoxic (elapid):** ptosis, ophthalmoplegia, bulbar/respiratory muscle paralysis; krait bites
    often **painless, nocturnal, with abdominal pain**. (¾m)
  - **Vasculotoxic (viper):** local swelling/blistering/necrosis, **coagulopathy and spontaneous bleeding**,
    AKI; **haematotoxic** with prolonged clotting. (¾m)
- **Investigations (½m):** **20-minute whole-blood clotting test (20-WBCT)** at the bedside, PT/INR,
  platelets, renal function, urine (myoglobinuria/haematuria), ECG. (½m)
- **Management (2m):**
  - **First aid:** reassure, immobilise the limb (splint), keep still, transport urgently; **no tourniquet,
    no incision/suction**. (½m)
  - **Polyvalent anti-snake venom (ASV)** — give for any sign of **systemic envenomation or positive
    20-WBCT/neurotoxicity**: **8–10 vials IV** initially, repeated 6-hourly per response; manage
    **anaphylaxis** (adrenaline at bedside). (1m)
  - **Neostigmine + atropine** for neurotoxic (anticholinesterase) paralysis; **ventilate** if respiratory
    failure; supportive — blood products for coagulopathy, dialysis for AKI. (½m)

**What to tabulate:** [ ] **Neurotoxic vs vasculotoxic** (snakes, features, key treatment) — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Disorders Caused by Venomous Snakebites*. · API — *Toxicology → Snake bite*

### Q7. Short note: Acute respiratory failure and basics of mechanical ventilation (5m)
<!-- id: sys11-q7 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on acute respiratory failure and the basics of mechanical ventilation.

- **Definition / types (1½m):** failure of gas exchange.
  - **Type 1 (hypoxaemic):** **PaO₂ <60 mmHg** with normal/low PaCO₂ — V/Q mismatch/shunt (pneumonia,
    ARDS, pulmonary oedema, PE). (¾m)
  - **Type 2 (hypercapnic):** **PaCO₂ >50 mmHg** with hypoxaemia — alveolar hypoventilation (COPD,
    neuromuscular weakness, opioid overdose, chest-wall disease). (¾m)
- **Clinical features (1m):** dyspnoea, tachypnoea, accessory-muscle use, cyanosis (hypoxia);
  drowsiness, headache, flap, warm peripheries (hypercapnia); diagnosis confirmed on **ABG**. (1m)
- **Management / ventilation basics (2½m):**
  - **Oxygen** (controlled, target SpO₂ 94–98%; **88–92%** in CO₂ retainers); treat the cause. (½m)
  - **Non-invasive ventilation (NIV):** **BiPAP** for type 2 (COPD exacerbation, acidotic) and **CPAP** for
    cardiogenic pulmonary oedema. (½m)
  - **Invasive ventilation** if NIV fails / unsafe airway / coma: set mode (volume/pressure), **tidal volume
    6 mL/kg ideal body weight (lung-protective for ARDS)**, **PEEP**, FiO₂ to target SpO₂, plateau pressure
    <30 cmH₂O. (1m)
  - Monitor ABG, wean as the cause resolves; watch for VAP and barotrauma. (½m)

**What to tabulate:** [ ] **Type 1 vs Type 2 respiratory failure** (PaO₂/PaCO₂, causes), and NIV indications — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome* / *Mechanical Ventilatory Support*. ·
Davidson's — *Respiratory failure*. · API — *Critical Care → Ventilation*. [verify current edition]

### Q8. Short note: Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) / ACLS overview (5m)
<!-- id: sys11-q8 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on cardiopulmonary resuscitation and the basics of adult ACLS.

- **Definition (½m):** CPR = emergency restoration of circulation and ventilation in cardiac/respiratory
  arrest to maintain organ perfusion until return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC). (½m)
- **Basic life support — high-quality CPR (1½m):**
  - Confirm unresponsiveness + no normal breathing/pulse (≤10 s check), call for help/defibrillator. (½m)
  - **Chest compressions:** centre of chest, **rate 100–120/min, depth 5–6 cm**, full recoil, minimise
    interruptions; **compression : ventilation 30 : 2** (single rescuer, unprotected airway). (½m)
  - **Early defibrillation** for shockable rhythms is the key determinant of survival. (½m)
- **ACLS — rhythm-based (2m):**
  - **Shockable (VF / pulseless VT):** **defibrillate** (biphasic ~150–200 J), resume CPR immediately;
    after the 3rd shock give **adrenaline 1 mg IV** then **amiodarone 300 mg IV**. (1m)
  - **Non-shockable (asystole / PEA):** CPR + **adrenaline 1 mg IV every 3–5 min**; do **not** shock. (½m)
  - Treat reversible causes — **4 Hs & 4 Ts** (Hypoxia, Hypovolaemia, Hypo/hyperkalaemia/metabolic,
    Hypothermia; Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade, Toxins, Thrombosis). (½m)
- **Post-ROSC (½m):** secure airway/oxygenation, 12-lead ECG, targeted temperature management, treat the
  cause, ICU care. (½m)

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Cardiovascular Collapse, Cardiac Arrest & Sudden Cardiac Death*. · Davidson's —
