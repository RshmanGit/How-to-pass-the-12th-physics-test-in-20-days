### Q1. Acute kidney injury — causes, evaluation and management (15m)
<!-- id: sys04-laq-q1 -->

**Restated:** Define acute kidney injury. Discuss its causes, clinical evaluation and management.

**Definition (1m)**
- AKI = an **abrupt (within hours–days) decline in glomerular filtration rate**, defined by **KDIGO** as a
  rise in serum creatinine ≥ **0.3 mg/dL within 48 h**, or ≥ **1.5× baseline within 7 days**, or **urine
  output < 0.5 mL/kg/h for ≥ 6 h**. Potentially reversible if the cause is corrected early. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (3m)**
- **Pre-renal (commonest, ~55%) — renal hypoperfusion, tubules intact (½m):** volume loss (vomiting,
  diarrhoea, haemorrhage, burns), sepsis/vasodilation, cardiac failure, hepatorenal syndrome,
  NSAIDs/ACE-inhibitors altering glomerular autoregulation. Reversible if perfusion restored before
  ischaemic damage. (1m)
- **Intrinsic renal (~40%) (1m):** **acute tubular necrosis** (ischaemic — prolonged pre-renal; or toxic —
  aminoglycosides, contrast, myoglobin in rhabdomyolysis) is commonest; also **acute interstitial
  nephritis** (drugs — NSAIDs, penicillins, PPIs), **glomerulonephritis**, and vascular causes. (1m)
- **Post-renal (~5%) — obstruction (½m):** bilateral (or single functioning kidney) tract obstruction —
  prostate, calculi, pelvic malignancy, retroperitoneal fibrosis; relief restores function early. (½m)

**Clinical features (3m)**
- Often **oliguria** (may be non-oliguric); features of the **cause** (sepsis, hypovolaemia, bladder
  outflow symptoms). (1m)
- Features of **uraemia / fluid overload:** nausea, drowsiness/encephalopathy, asterixis, pericardial rub,
  pulmonary oedema, raised JVP, peripheral oedema. (1m)
- Assess **volume status** carefully (postural BP, JVP, skin turgor, fluid balance/weight charts) — it
  steers management; examine for a palpable bladder and large kidneys. (1m)

**Investigations (3m)**
- **Bloods:** serum creatinine + urea (trend), **electrolytes (K⁺ critical)**, bicarbonate (metabolic
  acidosis), FBC, CRP; **VBG** for acidosis/K⁺. (1m)
- **Urine:** dipstick (blood/protein → glomerular/AIN), microscopy (**muddy-brown casts → ATN**, red-cell
  casts → GN, eosinophils → AIN); **urinary sodium/FENa** to separate pre-renal (FENa < 1%) from ATN
  (FENa > 2%) — see `04-numericals.md`. (1m)
- **Imaging — renal ultrasound** (exclude obstruction/hydronephrosis; small kidneys suggest CKD); immunology
  (ANA, ANCA, anti-GBM, complement) and **renal biopsy** if intrinsic cause unexplained. (1m)

**Management (5m)**
- **Treat the cause + optimise perfusion (the mark-magnet) (2m):**
  - **Pre-renal:** restore circulating volume with **IV crystalloid** titrated to clinical euvolaemia;
    treat sepsis; **stop nephrotoxins** (NSAIDs, ACE-i/ARB, aminoglycosides) and review/renally-dose drugs. (1m)
  - **Post-renal:** relieve obstruction — **urinary catheter** for outflow obstruction, nephrostomy/stent for
    upper tract. **Intrinsic:** treat the specific disease (stop offending drug in AIN; immunosuppression in
    GN/vasculitis). (1m)
- **Supportive (1½m):** manage **hyperkalaemia** (see `02-saq.md`), acidosis and fluid balance; daily
  weights and strict input/output; nutrition; avoid/adjust nephrotoxic and renally-cleared drugs; treat
  pulmonary oedema (oxygen, diuretic ± nitrates). (1½m)
- **Renal replacement therapy — emergency dialysis (1½m):** indicated for refractory features —
  **"AEIOU": A**cidosis (severe, refractory), **E**lectrolytes (refractory **hyperkalaemia**),
  **I**ntoxication (dialysable poison), **O**verload (refractory pulmonary oedema), **U**raemia
  (encephalopathy, pericarditis). (1½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — pre-renal vs intrinsic (ATN) vs post-renal** (mechanism, urine Na/FENa, casts, US) — ~1m
- [ ] **Flowchart — AKI approach** (assess volume → urine/US → treat cause → RRT if AEIOU) — ~1m

### Q2. Chronic kidney disease and renal replacement therapy (15m)
<!-- id: sys04-laq-q2 -->

**Restated:** Define chronic kidney disease. Discuss its causes, clinical features, investigations,
management and the modalities of renal replacement therapy.

**Definition (1m)**
- CKD = **abnormalities of kidney structure or function present for ≥ 3 months** with implications for
  health — i.e. **GFR < 60 mL/min/1.73 m²** and/or markers of damage (albuminuria ACR ≥ 30 mg/g, urine
  sediment, electrolyte, histological or imaging abnormalities). Staged by **GFR (G1–G5)** and **albuminuria
  (A1–A3)** — see `03-criteria.md`. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (3m)**
- **Commonest causes:** **diabetic nephropathy** (leading cause), **hypertensive nephrosclerosis**, chronic
  glomerulonephritis. (1m)
- Others: polycystic kidney disease, chronic interstitial/obstructive disease, reflux nephropathy. (1m)
- **Mechanism:** irreversible nephron loss → compensatory **hyperfiltration** in surviving nephrons →
  glomerulosclerosis and progressive fibrosis (a self-perpetuating cycle that proteinuria and hypertension
  accelerate). (1m)

**Clinical features (3m)**
- Often **asymptomatic** until advanced; nocturia, fatigue. (½m)
- **Uraemic syndrome:** anorexia, nausea, pruritus, restless legs, encephalopathy, pericarditis. (1m)
- **Multisystem consequences:** **anaemia** (↓ erythropoietin), **CKD-mineral bone disease** (↓ vitamin D,
  ↓ Ca²⁺, ↑ phosphate, secondary hyperparathyroidism, renal osteodystrophy), **hypertension** and accelerated
  cardiovascular disease (the leading cause of death), fluid overload, metabolic acidosis, hyperkalaemia. (1½m)

**Investigations (3m)**
- **Bloods:** creatinine with **eGFR** (see `04-numericals.md`), urea, electrolytes, **Ca/PO₄/PTH**,
  bicarbonate, **Hb** (normocytic anaemia), HbA1c/glucose, lipids. (1m)
- **Urine:** **albumin:creatinine ratio (ACR)** to quantify proteinuria/stage A; microscopy/dipstick. (1m)
- **Imaging/cause work-up:** **renal ultrasound** (small, echogenic kidneys = chronicity; large cystic =
  PCKD); immunology and **biopsy** for unexplained CKD with preserved kidney size. (1m)

**Management (5m)**
- **Slow progression (the mark-magnet) (2m):** **strict BP control** with an **ACE-inhibitor or ARB**
  (also reduces proteinuria — first-line in diabetic/proteinuric CKD); **glycaemic control**;
  **SGLT2 inhibitors** (dapagliflozin) are now standard to slow progression; restrict dietary salt/protein;
  treat dyslipidaemia (statin); smoking cessation; **avoid nephrotoxins** and renally dose drugs. (2m)
- **Treat complications (1½m):** **anaemia** — iron then **erythropoiesis-stimulating agents** (target Hb
  ~10–11.5 g/dL); **CKD-MBD** — phosphate binders, activated vitamin D, dietary phosphate restriction;
  **acidosis** — oral sodium bicarbonate; **hyperkalaemia** and fluid overload (loop diuretic). (1½m)
- **Renal replacement therapy in ESRD (G5, ~ eGFR < 15 or uraemic) (1½m):**
  - **Haemodialysis** — diffusion across a dialyser, typically 4 h × 3/week via AV fistula (preferred) or
    catheter. **Peritoneal dialysis (CAPD)** — peritoneum as membrane, home-based, daily exchanges.
  - **Renal transplantation** — the **treatment of choice** for suitable patients (best survival/quality of
    life); lifelong immunosuppression. (Modalities compared in `02-saq.md`.) (1½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — KDIGO GFR (G1–G5) × albuminuria (A1–A3) staging grid** — ~1m
- [ ] **Table — complications of CKD by system** (anaemia / CKD-MBD / CVS / acid-base) with management — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Chronic Kidney Disease* & *Dialysis / Transplantation*. · Davidson's —
*Chronic kidney disease*. · API — *Nephrology → CKD & RRT*. [verify current edition]

### Q3. Nephrotic syndrome (10m)
<!-- id: sys04-laq-q3 -->

**Restated:** Define nephrotic syndrome. Discuss its causes, clinical features, investigations and
management.

**Definition (1m)**
- Nephrotic syndrome = the triad of **heavy proteinuria (> 3.5 g/24 h)**, **hypoalbuminaemia (< 30 g/L;
  texts cite < 3.0 g/dL, some < 2.5 g/dL)** and **oedema**, usually with **hyperlipidaemia** and lipiduria. The defect is increased **glomerular
  permeability to protein** with a relatively **bland** urinary sediment. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (2m)**
- **Primary (glomerular):** **minimal change disease** (commonest in children), **focal segmental
  glomerulosclerosis** (common cause in adults), **membranous nephropathy** (common primary cause in older
  adults — anti-PLA2R associated). (1m)
- **Secondary:** **diabetes mellitus** (commonest secondary cause overall), amyloidosis, SLE (lupus
  nephritis), infections, drugs. Podocyte injury → loss of charge/size barrier → albuminuria. (1m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- **Oedema** — periorbital/facial in the morning, dependent and pitting, may progress to anasarca/ascites;
  frothy urine. (1m)
- **Complications:** **thromboembolism** (urinary antithrombin loss → hypercoagulable state — renal vein
  thrombosis, DVT/PE), **infection** (urinary immunoglobulin loss — esp. pneumococcal/SBP), acute kidney
  injury, hyperlipidaemia. (1m)

**Investigations (2m)**
- **Urine:** **24-h protein** or **spot protein:creatinine / ACR** (quantify); microscopy (bland ±
  oval fat bodies/fatty casts). (1m)
- **Bloods:** serum albumin, lipids, renal function, glucose/HbA1c; **cause work-up** — ANA/dsDNA,
  complement, hepatitis B/C, **anti-PLA2R** (membranous); **renal biopsy** in adults (and steroid-resistant
  children) for histological diagnosis. (1m)

**Management (3m)**
- **Specific therapy (1m):** treat the cause — **corticosteroids** (minimal change is usually
  steroid-responsive); immunosuppression (calcineurin inhibitors, cyclophosphamide, rituximab) for
  steroid-resistant/membranous/FSGS; tight glycaemic control in diabetic disease. (1m)
- **Reduce proteinuria + oedema (1m):** **ACE-inhibitor / ARB** (anti-proteinuric); **dietary salt
  restriction + loop diuretic** (e.g. furosemide) for oedema; modest protein intake. (1m)
- **Prevent/treat complications (1m):** statin for hyperlipidaemia; **anticoagulation** if thrombosis (or
  prophylaxis when albumin very low); prompt treatment of infection ± pneumococcal vaccination. (1m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — primary causes** (minimal change / FSGS / membranous: age, biopsy, response) — ~1m
- [ ] **Table — nephrotic vs nephritic syndrome** (proteinuria, haematuria, BP, sediment) — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Glomerular Diseases* (Nephrotic syndromes). · Davidson's — *Glomerular disease*. ·
API — *Nephrology → Nephrotic syndrome*. [verify current edition]

### Q4. Nephritic syndrome / acute glomerulonephritis (10m)
<!-- id: sys04-laq-q4 -->

**Restated:** Define nephritic syndrome. Discuss the causes, clinical features, investigations and
management of acute glomerulonephritis.

**Definition (1m)**
- Nephritic syndrome = a glomerular **inflammatory** syndrome characterised by **haematuria (dysmorphic RBCs
  + red-cell casts)**, **hypertension**, **oedema**, **oliguria** and a **modest proteinuria** (usually
  sub-nephrotic) with rising creatinine. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (2m)**
- **Post-infectious GN** — classically **post-streptococcal** (1–3 weeks after a throat/skin streptococcal
  infection, immune-complex mediated, low C3). (1m)
- Others: **IgA nephropathy** (commonest GN worldwide; haematuria with/after URTI), **lupus nephritis**,
  **ANCA-associated / anti-GBM (Goodpasture)** vasculitis — these may present as **rapidly progressive GN
  (crescentic)**, a nephrological emergency. (1m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- **Cola-/smoky-coloured urine** (macroscopic haematuria), facial/periorbital **oedema**, **hypertension**,
  reduced urine output. (1m)
- Look for the **systemic** cause: recent sore throat/skin infection (PSGN), haemoptysis (anti-GBM/ANCA),
  rash/arthralgia (lupus, vasculitis); features of fluid overload (pulmonary oedema, raised JVP). (1m)

**Investigations (2m)**
- **Urine:** microscopy — **dysmorphic RBCs and red-cell casts** (the hallmark); proteinuria quantified by
  PCR/ACR (sub-nephrotic). (1m)
- **Bloods + immunology:** creatinine/eGFR, electrolytes; **complement (low C3 in PSGN/lupus)**, **ASO/anti-
  DNase B**, **ANA/dsDNA, ANCA, anti-GBM**; **renal biopsy** (especially if RPGN — to identify crescents and
  guide immunosuppression). (1m)

**Management (3m)**
- **Supportive (the mainstay in PSGN) (1½m):** **salt and fluid restriction**, **loop diuretic** and
  **antihypertensives** for volume overload/hypertension; treat any ongoing infection (antibiotics in PSGN);
  manage AKI/hyperkalaemia; dialysis if indicated. Post-streptococcal GN in children is usually
  **self-limiting** with good recovery. (1½m)
- **Specific / immunosuppression (1m):** for **RPGN, lupus, ANCA/anti-GBM** — **high-dose corticosteroids +
  cyclophosphamide or rituximab**, and **plasma exchange** for anti-GBM (Goodpasture) / severe vasculitis.
  Time-critical — delay loses nephrons. (1m)
- **Follow-up (½m):** monitor BP, renal function and proteinuria; treat residual hypertension/proteinuria
  with ACE-i/ARB. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — causes by complement** (low C3: PSGN, lupus, MPGN · normal C3: IgA, ANCA, anti-GBM) — ~1m
- [ ] **Flowchart — approach to acute GN** (urine casts → immunology/complement → biopsy if RPGN →
  supportive ± immunosuppression) — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Glomerular Diseases* (Nephritic / RPGN). · Davidson's — *Glomerular disease*. ·
API — *Nephrology → Acute glomerulonephritis*. [verify current edition]
