### Q1. Short note: Haemodialysis vs peritoneal dialysis (CAPD) (5m)
<!-- id: sys04-saq-q1 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note comparing haemodialysis and peritoneal dialysis (CAPD).

- **Principle (1m):** **Haemodialysis (HD)** — blood is pumped through an extracorporeal **dialyser**;
  solutes cleared by **diffusion** across a semipermeable membrane against a counter-current dialysate,
  fluid removed by **ultrafiltration**. **Peritoneal dialysis (PD/CAPD)** — the patient's **peritoneal
  membrane** is the dialyser; dialysate dwells in the peritoneal cavity and is exchanged. (1m)
- **Access (1m):** HD — **arteriovenous fistula** (preferred, durable) or tunnelled venous catheter. PD —
  a **Tenckhoff peritoneal catheter**. (1m)
- **Schedule / setting (1m):** HD — typically **4 h × 3 sessions/week**, usually hospital/centre-based. CAPD
  — **home-based, continuous**, ~4 exchanges/day (or automated overnight, APD); preserves independence. (1m)
- **Key complications (1m):** HD — hypotension, disequilibrium, access infection/thrombosis,
  bleeding. PD — **bacterial peritonitis** (cloudy effluent — the major complication), exit-site infection,
  catheter malfunction, hernia, membrane failure over time. (1m)

### Q2. Short note: Hyperkalaemia — recognition and management (5m)
<!-- id: sys04-saq-q2 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on the recognition and emergency management of hyperkalaemia.

- **Definition + causes (1m):** serum **K⁺ > 5.5 mmol/L** (severe > 6.5); causes — **AKI/CKD** (reduced
  excretion), drugs (ACE-i/ARB, spironolactone, NSAIDs), tissue breakdown (rhabdomyolysis, haemolysis,
  tumour lysis), acidosis, Addison's; exclude **pseudohyperkalaemia** (haemolysed sample). (1m)
- **Recognition (1m):** often asymptomatic; muscle weakness, palpitations; **ECG is the priority** —
  **tall tented T waves → flattened P → widened QRS → sine-wave → VF/asystole**. Always do an ECG in
  significant hyperkalaemia. (1m)
- **Emergency management — stepwise (2m):**
  - **Step 1 — protect the myocardium (if ECG changes or K⁺ ≥ 6.5):** **IV calcium gluconate 10% 10 mL
    over 5–10 min** (repeat if changes persist) — stabilises membrane, does **not** lower K⁺. (1m)
  - **Step 2 — shift K⁺ into cells:** **insulin 10 units (soluble/Actrapid) in 25 g glucose (50 mL of 50%)
    IV** over 15–30 min (monitor glucose); **nebulised salbutamol 10–20 mg**. (½m)
  - **Step 3 — remove K⁺ from body:** **oral potassium binder** — newer agents **patiromer / sodium
    zirconium cyclosilicate** (preferred; classic resin = sodium/calcium polystyrene sulfonate); loop diuretic
    if making urine; **haemodialysis** if refractory or in established renal failure. (½m)
- **Always (1m):** stop K⁺-raising drugs and K⁺ supplements, treat the cause, and recheck K⁺ + ECG. (1m)

[ ] **3-step management** (stabilise → shift → remove) — ~½m

potassium*. [verify current edition]

### Q3. Short note: Renal tubular acidosis (5m)
<!-- id: sys04-saq-q3 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on renal tubular acidosis (RTA).

- **Definition (1m):** a group of disorders with a **normal anion-gap (hyperchloraemic) metabolic acidosis**
  due to impaired tubular **H⁺ excretion** or **HCO₃⁻ reabsorption**, with a **relatively preserved GFR**. (1m)
- **Types (2m):**
  - **Type 1 (distal):** failure of distal H⁺ secretion → inability to acidify urine (**urine pH > 5.5**),
    **hypokalaemia**, nephrocalcinosis/stones; causes — Sjögren's, autoimmune, drugs. (1m)
  - **Type 2 (proximal):** impaired proximal HCO₃⁻ reabsorption → bicarbonate wasting,
    **hypokalaemia**; often part of **Fanconi syndrome** (glycosuria, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria). (½m)
  - **Type 4 (hyperkalaemic):** **hypoaldosteronism / aldosterone resistance** → **hyperkalaemia**;
    commonest type — seen in diabetic nephropathy, drugs (ACE-i, NSAIDs, heparin). (½m)
- **Investigations (1m):** ABG/venous gas (normal-gap acidosis), serum K⁺, **urine pH**, urinary anion gap
  (**positive in distal type 1** — impaired NH₄⁺ excretion; **negative in proximal type 2**, where distal
  acidification is intact), serum bicarbonate; identify the underlying cause. (1m)
- **Management (1m):** **oral alkali** — sodium bicarbonate or potassium citrate (with **potassium
  replacement** in types 1 & 2); for **type 4** — low-potassium diet, **fludrocortisone**, loop diuretic,
  and stop offending drugs; treat the underlying disease. (1m)

### Q4. Short note: Approach to haematuria (5m)
<!-- id: sys04-saq-q4 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on the clinical approach to haematuria.

- **Definition (1m):** presence of blood in urine — **macroscopic (visible)** or **microscopic (≥ 3 RBCs/hpf)**;
  first **confirm** it (dipstick positive but microscopy negative → **haemoglobinuria/myoglobinuria**;
  red urine without blood → beetroot, rifampicin, porphyria). (1m)
- **Localise — glomerular vs non-glomerular (1½m):**
  - **Glomerular:** **dysmorphic RBCs, red-cell casts, proteinuria**, brown/cola urine — points to
    glomerulonephritis (IgA, PSGN, thin basement membrane, Alport's). (1m)
  - **Non-glomerular (urological):** isomorphic RBCs, clots, terminal/initial stream — **stones,
    infection (UTI), trauma, BPH, and urinary-tract malignancy** (bladder/renal — esp. painless
    haematuria in an older smoker). (½m)
- **Investigations (1½m):** **urine microscopy + culture**, cytology; renal function + ACR; **imaging**
  (USG; **CT urography** for stones/tumour); **cystoscopy** for non-glomerular haematuria in those at
  cancer risk (> 40 y, smoker); refer for **renal biopsy** if a glomerular cause is suspected. (1½m)
- **Red flags (1m):** **painless visible haematuria** in adults → urgent malignancy work-up; haematuria
  with hypertension, oedema, proteinuria and casts → nephritic syndrome work-up. (1m)
