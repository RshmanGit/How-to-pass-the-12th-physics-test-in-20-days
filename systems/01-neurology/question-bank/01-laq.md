### Q1. Acute ischaemic stroke — aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management (15m)
<!-- id: sys01-laq-q1 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management of acute
ischaemic stroke (cerebrovascular accident).

**Definition (1m)**
- Stroke = rapidly developing clinical signs of **focal (or global) neurological deficit lasting >24 h**
  (or leading to death) of presumed **vascular** origin. Ischaemic stroke (~80%) = infarction from arterial
  occlusion; the rest are haemorrhagic. A deficit fully resolving <24 h with no infarct = TIA. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (3m)**
- **Large-artery atherosclerosis** (carotid/vertebral) → thrombosis or artery-to-artery embolism. (½m)
- **Cardioembolism** — atrial fibrillation (commonest), recent MI, prosthetic valves, infective
  endocarditis. (½m)
- **Small-vessel (lacunar)** disease — lipohyalinosis from chronic hypertension/diabetes. (½m)
- Less common: dissection, vasculitis, hypercoagulable/prothrombotic states, venous infarction. (½m)
- **Risk factors:** hypertension (most important modifiable), diabetes, smoking, dyslipidaemia, AF, age. (½m)
- **Mechanism:** occlusion → ischaemic core (irreversible) surrounded by **penumbra** (salvageable if
  reperfused early) — the rationale for time-critical thrombolysis/thrombectomy. (½m)

**Clinical features (3m)**
- **Sudden** focal deficit; pattern follows the territory: (½m)
  - **MCA** — contralateral hemiparesis/hemisensory loss (face+arm > leg), aphasia (dominant) or neglect
    (non-dominant), gaze deviation toward the lesion. (1m)
  - **ACA** — contralateral leg > arm weakness. **PCA** — homonymous hemianopia, visual deficits. (½m)
  - **Posterior circulation/brainstem** — vertigo, diplopia, ataxia, crossed signs, dysarthria/dysphagia. (½m)
- Use **NIHSS** to quantify severity; assess swallow, GCS, and **time of onset** (last-seen-well). (½m)

**Investigations (3m)**
- **Non-contrast CT head — FIRST** (immediately): excludes haemorrhage before thrombolysis; early
  ischaemic signs may be subtle. (1m)
- **MRI with DWI** — most sensitive for early infarct; CT/MR angiography to find large-vessel occlusion
  (thrombectomy candidates); CT perfusion for extended-window selection. (1m)
- **Cause work-up:** ECG ± Holter (AF), echocardiography, carotid Doppler, blood glucose (mandatory —
  hypoglycaemia mimics stroke), lipids, FBC, coagulation; young/cryptogenic → thrombophilia, vasculitis. (1m)

**Management (5m)**
- **Hyperacute / reperfusion (the mark-magnet) (2m):**
  - **IV thrombolysis — alteplase 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg)**, 10% bolus then infusion over 1 h, within
    **4.5 h** of onset, after CT excludes bleed and exclusion criteria checked. **Tenecteplase 0.25 mg/kg
    (max 25 mg) single bolus** is now preferred in several guidelines (esp. large-vessel occlusion) —
    confirm which agent your exam expects. (1m)
  - **Mechanical thrombectomy** for large-vessel anterior-circulation occlusion, up to **6 h** routinely;
    selected cases to **24 h** on perfusion/clinical mismatch (**DAWN / DEFUSE-3**). (½m)
  - If not for thrombolysis: **aspirin 300 mg** as soon as possible (within **24 h**) once haemorrhage is
    excluded; **if thrombolysed, delay antiplatelet until the 24 h post-lysis scan**. (½m)
- **Supportive / stroke-unit care (1½m):** admit to a stroke unit; maintain airway/oxygenation, normo-
  glycaemia, normothermia; **permissive hypertension** acutely (treat BP only if >185/110 before lysis or
  >220/120 otherwise); swallow assessment before oral intake; VTE prophylaxis; early physiotherapy/rehab. (1½m)
- **Secondary prevention (1½m):** antiplatelet (aspirin → long-term clopidogrel) **or anticoagulation if AF**
  (DOAC/warfarin); **high-intensity statin**; BP and diabetes control; smoking cessation; **carotid
  endarterectomy** for symptomatic 70–99% stenosis. (1½m)

- [ ] **Table — arterial territory ⇒ deficit** (ACA / MCA / PCA / brainstem) — ~1m
- [ ] **Flowchart — hyperacute pathway** (onset time → CT → thrombolysis ±4.5 h / thrombectomy → stroke unit) — ~1m

### Q2. Epilepsy — classification, and approach to a patient with seizures including status epilepticus (15m)
<!-- id: sys01-laq-q2 -->

**Restated:** Classify seizure disorders. Discuss the approach to a patient with seizures and outline the
management of status epilepticus.

**Definition (1m)**
- A **seizure** is a transient occurrence of signs/symptoms due to abnormal excessive/synchronous neuronal
  activity; **epilepsy** = an enduring predisposition to recurrent unprovoked seizures (operationally ≥2
  unprovoked seizures >24 h apart, or 1 with high recurrence risk). (1m)

**Classification (ILAE) (3m)**
- **By onset (3m):**
  - **Focal onset** — aware vs impaired awareness; motor vs non-motor; ± evolution to bilateral
    tonic-clonic. (1m)
  - **Generalised onset** — tonic-clonic, absence, myoclonic, tonic, atonic. (1m)
  - **Unknown onset / unclassified.** Also classify by **aetiology**: structural, genetic, infectious,
    metabolic, immune, unknown. (1m)

**Clinical approach / features (3m)**
- **History is key (eyewitness account):** aura, pattern, tongue-biting, incontinence, post-ictal confusion
  → favour seizure over syncope. (1m)
- **Identify provoked vs unprovoked:** fever, alcohol withdrawal, hypoglycaemia, hyponatraemia, drugs,
  sleep deprivation. (1m)
- Examination for focal deficit, neurocutaneous markers, signs of raised ICP. (1m)

**Investigations (3m)**
- **EEG** — supports diagnosis/classifies (interictal epileptiform discharges); a normal EEG does not
  exclude epilepsy. (1m)
- **Neuroimaging — MRI brain** (preferred) to find a structural cause; CT if acute/unavailable. (1m)
- **Bloods:** glucose, electrolytes (Na, Ca, Mg), renal/liver function, toxicology; consider LP if CNS
  infection suspected. (1m)

**Management (5m)**
- **Chronic epilepsy (2m):** start an antiepileptic **after a second unprovoked seizure (or first if high
  risk)**; choose by seizure type — focal: levetiracetam/lamotrigine/carbamazepine; generalised: valproate
  (avoid in women of child-bearing potential), lamotrigine, levetiracetam. **Monotherapy first**, titrate;
  counsel on adherence, driving, pregnancy. (2m)
- **Status epilepticus — emergency (the mark-magnet) (3m):** defined as a seizure ≥5 min or repeated
  seizures without recovery.
  - **ABC**, oxygen, IV access, **check glucose** (give glucose ± thiamine if low). (½m)
  - **Step 1 (benzodiazepine):** IV **lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg (≈4 mg)**, repeat once; or IM midazolam /
    rectal diazepam if no access. (1m)
  - **Step 2 (if persists) — second-line AED (doses differ by agent, per ESETT):** IV **levetiracetam
    60 mg/kg (max 4.5 g)** OR **sodium valproate 40 mg/kg (max 3 g)** OR **(fos)phenytoin 20 mg/kg PE
    (max 1.5 g)**. (1m)
  - **Step 3 (refractory):** intubate + **general anaesthesia** (midazolam/propofol/thiopentone infusion)
    in ICU with EEG monitoring; treat the precipitant. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — ILAE classification** (focal / generalised / unknown, with subtypes) — ~1m
- [ ] **Flowchart — status epilepticus timeline** (0–5 min ABC/glucose → benzo → 2nd agent → anaesthesia) — ~1m

### Q3. Myasthenia gravis — aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management (10m)
<!-- id: sys01-laq-q3 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management of myasthenia
gravis, including myasthenic crisis.

**Definition (1m)**
- Myasthenia gravis (MG) = an **autoimmune disorder of the neuromuscular junction** causing **fatigable**
  skeletal-muscle weakness (worse with activity, better with rest). (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (2m)**
- **Autoantibodies against post-synaptic nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (anti-AChR)** (~85%); a subset
  have **anti-MuSK** antibodies. (1m)
- Antibodies → receptor blockade, complement-mediated destruction and reduced AChR density → failure of
  neuromuscular transmission. Strong **thymic association** — thymic hyperplasia (common) or **thymoma**
  (~10–15%). (1m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- **Fatigable weakness** worsening through the day. **Ocular** onset common: **ptosis, diplopia**. (½m)
- **Bulbar:** dysarthria, dysphagia, fatigable chewing; **facial/neck/proximal limb** weakness. (½m)
- **Reflexes and sensation normal**; no autonomic involvement. (½m)
- **Myasthenic crisis:** respiratory muscle weakness → respiratory failure — a medical emergency. (½m)

**Investigations (2m)**
- **Serology:** anti-AChR antibodies (specific); anti-MuSK if negative. (½m)
- **Electrophysiology:** **repetitive nerve stimulation → decremental response**; single-fibre EMG (most
  sensitive — increased jitter). (1m)
- **Bedside:** ice-pack test / fatigability of ptosis; **CT/MRI thorax for thymoma**; TFTs (associated
  autoimmune disease). (½m)

**Management (3m)**
- **Symptomatic (1m):** **pyridostigmine** (acetylcholinesterase inhibitor), first-line for symptom relief.
- **Immunotherapy (1m):** **corticosteroids** (start low, uptitrate) + steroid-sparing immunosuppressant
  (azathioprine/mycophenolate); rituximab for refractory/MuSK disease. (1m)
- **Thymectomy** — for thymoma, and benefits selected non-thymomatous AChR-positive patients. (½m)
- **Myasthenic crisis (½m):** ICU, monitor **FVC**, ventilate if failing; **IVIG or plasma exchange**;
  identify trigger (infection, drugs e.g. aminoglycosides); avoid abrupt steroid swings. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Diagram — neuromuscular junction** with anti-AChR antibody site of action — ~1m
- [ ] **Table — MG vs Lambert-Eaton** (antibody, fatigue vs facilitation, association) — ~½m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Myasthenia Gravis & Other NMJ Diseases*. · Davidson's — *Neuromuscular junction
disorders*. · API — *Neurology*. [verify current edition]

### Q4. Guillain-Barré syndrome — aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management (10m)
<!-- id: sys01-laq-q4 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, investigations and management of
Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS).

**Definition (1m)**
- GBS = an acute, immune-mediated **inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy** causing acute,
  **ascending, areflexic, symmetrical flaccid paralysis**, typically monophasic, reaching nadir within
  4 weeks. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (2m)**
- **Post-infectious autoimmune** process; antecedent infection 1–3 weeks prior in ~⅔ — classically
  *Campylobacter jejuni* (also CMV, EBV, Mycoplasma, Zika). (½m)
- **Molecular mimicry** — antibodies to microbial antigens cross-react with peripheral-nerve gangliosides
  (anti-GM1; anti-GQ1b in Miller-Fisher). (1m)
- Immune attack → demyelination ± axonal damage of roots/peripheral nerves → conduction block.
  **Subtypes:** AIDP (commonest), AMAN/AMSAN (axonal), Miller-Fisher variant. (½m)

**Clinical features (2m)**
- **Ascending** symmetrical limb weakness (legs→arms) with **areflexia**; sensory symptoms mild relative
  to motor; back/limb pain. (1m)
- **Cranial nerves** — bifacial/bulbar weakness; **Miller-Fisher triad** = ophthalmoplegia + ataxia +
  areflexia. (½m)
- **Dangerous features:** respiratory failure (diaphragm) and **autonomic dysfunction** (arrhythmia, BP
  swings) — leading causes of death. (½m)

**Investigations (2m)**
- **CSF — albuminocytological dissociation:** raised protein with normal cell count (may be normal in
  week 1). (1m)
- **Nerve conduction studies** — demyelinating pattern (prolonged distal latency, conduction block,
  prolonged/absent F-waves). (½m)
- **Serial spirometry / FVC** to predict respiratory failure; anti-ganglioside antibodies; stool/serology
  for *Campylobacter*. (½m)

**Management (3m)**
- **Monitoring & supportive (life-saving) (1m):** admit; **serial FVC** — intubate/ventilate if
  **FVC <15 mL/kg** or falling; cardiac monitoring for autonomic instability; VTE prophylaxis; pain
  control; physiotherapy.
- **Immunotherapy (1m):** **IVIG 0.4 g/kg/day × 5 days** *OR* **plasma exchange** — equally effective,
  **do not combine**; best within 2 weeks of onset. **Steroids are NOT effective.** (1m)
- **Prognosis (1m):** most recover over weeks–months; poor markers = rapid onset, axonal subtype, older
  age, ventilator need, preceding *Campylobacter*. (1m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Table — GBS subtypes** (AIDP / AMAN / AMSAN / Miller-Fisher: pathology, antibody) — ~1m
- [ ] **Flowchart — acute management** (admit → serial FVC → IVIG or PLEX → ICU triggers) — ~1m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Guillain-Barré Syndrome & Other Immune-Mediated Neuropathies*. · Davidson's —
*Peripheral nerve disorders*. · API — *Neurology → Acute neuromuscular paralysis*. [verify current edition]

### Q5. Parkinson's disease — aetiopathogenesis, clinical features, and management (10m)
<!-- id: sys01-laq-q5 -->

**Restated:** Discuss the aetiopathogenesis, clinical features and management of Parkinson's disease.

**Definition (1m)**
- Parkinson's disease (PD) = a progressive **neurodegenerative** disorder from loss of **dopaminergic
  neurons in the substantia nigra pars compacta**, characterised by a hypokinetic movement disorder. (1m)

**Aetiopathogenesis (2m)**
- Degeneration of nigrostriatal dopaminergic neurons → **dopamine deficiency** in the striatum → impaired
  basal-ganglia motor output. (1m)
- Histological hallmark = **Lewy bodies** (α-synuclein aggregates). Multifactorial — ageing, genetic
  (e.g. *SNCA, LRRK2, PARKIN*) and environmental factors; distinguish from secondary/atypical
  parkinsonism (drug-induced, vascular, MSA, PSP). (1m)

**Clinical features (3m)**
- **Cardinal motor features ("TRAP") (2m):** resting **Tremor** (pill-rolling, 4–6 Hz), cogwheel
  **Rigidity**, **Akinesia/bradykinesia**, **Postural instability** (later). Typically **asymmetrical**
  onset. (2m)
- Other: hypomimia, micrographia, shuffling festinant gait, stooped posture; **non-motor** — anosmia,
  constipation, REM-sleep behaviour disorder, depression, later cognitive decline. (1m)

**Investigations (1m)**
- Primarily **clinical diagnosis**; MRI/DaTscan mainly to exclude mimics; a **good response to levodopa**
  supports the diagnosis. (1m)

**Management (3m)**
- **Pharmacological (2m):** **Levodopa + carbidopa** (most effective for motor symptoms — first-line in
  significant disability); **dopamine agonists** (pramipexole/ropinirole) and **MAO-B inhibitors**
  (selegiline/rasagiline) — useful early/younger patients to delay levodopa; COMT inhibitors, amantadine
  for dyskinesia. Anticholinergics for tremor (caution in elderly). (2m)
- **Long-term issues:** motor fluctuations and **levodopa-induced dyskinesias** — manage with dosing
  adjustments, **deep brain stimulation (DBS)** in selected advanced patients. (½m)
- **Multidisciplinary:** physiotherapy, occupational/speech therapy, treat non-motor symptoms. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate (independent marks):**
- [ ] **Diagram — nigrostriatal pathway** (substantia nigra → striatum, dopamine deficiency) — ~1m
- [ ] **Table — PD vs essential tremor / atypical parkinsonism** — ~½m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Parkinson's Disease & Other Movement Disorders*. · Davidson's — *Movement
disorders*. · API — *Neurology → Parkinsonism*. [verify current edition]
