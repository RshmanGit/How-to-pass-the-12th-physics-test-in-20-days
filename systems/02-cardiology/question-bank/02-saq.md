### Q1. Short note: Atrial fibrillation and approach to arrhythmia (5m)
<!-- id: sys02-saq-q1 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on atrial fibrillation (AF) and the general approach to a cardiac arrhythmia.

- **Definition (1m):** AF = a **supraventricular tachyarrhythmia** with **uncoordinated atrial activation**
  → irregularly irregular ventricular response and loss of organised atrial contraction; ECG shows **absent
  P waves, irregular R-R intervals, fibrillatory baseline**. (1m)
- **Causes / approach (1m):** ischaemic/hypertensive/valvular (mitral) heart disease, **thyrotoxicosis**,
  alcohol, sepsis, PE; general arrhythmia approach — **assess haemodynamic stability first** (unstable →
  emergency **synchronised DC cardioversion**), 12-lead ECG, identify and treat the precipitant. (1m)
- **Management — "rate, rhythm, anticoagulate" (2m):**
  - **Rate control:** β-blocker or rate-limiting calcium-channel blocker (diltiazem/verapamil); digoxin
    if sedentary/HF. (½m)
  - **Rhythm control:** DC or pharmacological cardioversion (flecainide if no structural disease; amiodarone),
    catheter ablation; anticoagulate around cardioversion. (½m)
  - **Stroke prevention:** assess with **CHA₂DS₂-VASc** (and bleeding risk **HAS-BLED**, see `03-criteria.md`)
    → **DOAC** (or warfarin in valvular AF) when indicated. (1m)
- **Key pearl (1m):** AF causes **stasis in the left atrial appendage → thromboembolic stroke**; thromboembolic
  risk is independent of whether AF is paroxysmal or persistent — anticoagulate on **score**, not rhythm. (1m)

### Q2. Short note: Rheumatic fever and rheumatic heart disease (5m)
<!-- id: sys02-saq-q2 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on acute rheumatic fever (ARF) and rheumatic heart disease (RHD).

- **Definition (1m):** ARF = an **immune-mediated, multisystem inflammatory** sequel to **group A
  β-haemolytic streptococcal (GAS) pharyngitis**, occurring ~2–4 weeks after; **RHD** = the chronic valvular
  damage that follows recurrent/severe ARF. (1m)
- **Pathogenesis (½m):** **molecular mimicry** — antibodies/T-cells against streptococcal M-protein
  cross-react with cardiac (and joint/brain) tissue. (½m)
- **Clinical features (1½m):** **migratory polyarthritis** (large joints), **carditis** (pancarditis —
  the only feature with long-term sequelae; mitral > aortic valve), **Sydenham's chorea**, **erythema
  marginatum**, **subcutaneous nodules**; fever, raised ESR/CRP. Diagnosis by the **modified Jones criteria**
  (see `03-criteria.md`). (1½m)
- **Management (1½m):** **eradicate GAS — benzathine penicillin** (single IM dose, or oral penicillin V
  10 days); **anti-inflammatory** (aspirin/NSAIDs; corticosteroids for severe carditis); manage heart
  failure; **chorea** — supportive ± carbamazepine/valproate. **Secondary prophylaxis — IM benzathine
  penicillin every 3–4 weeks** for years (duration depends on carditis/RHD). (1½m)

### Q3. Short note: Cardiomyopathies (5m)
<!-- id: sys02-saq-q3 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on the cardiomyopathies.

- **Definition (1m):** cardiomyopathies = **structural and functional myocardial disorders** not explained
  by coronary, valvular, hypertensive or congenital disease; classified as **dilated, hypertrophic,
  restrictive** (and arrhythmogenic). (1m)
- **Dilated (DCM) (1m):** ventricular **dilatation + impaired systolic** function; causes — idiopathic,
  ischaemic, alcohol, peripartum, viral myocarditis, genetic; presents as **heart failure**, arrhythmia,
  emboli; treat as HFrEF. (1m)
- **Hypertrophic (HCM) (1½m):** **asymmetrical LV/septal hypertrophy** (autosomal-dominant sarcomere gene
  mutation) ± **LV outflow obstruction**; exertional dyspnoea, angina, syncope; an **ejection systolic
  murmur that increases on Valsalva/standing**; a leading cause of **sudden cardiac death in the young** —
  avoid dehydration/competitive sport; β-blocker, ICD for high risk. (1½m)
- **Restrictive (RCM) (1m):** **impaired ventricular filling** with preserved systolic function and normal
  cavity size; causes — **amyloidosis**, sarcoidosis, haemochromatosis, endomyocardial fibrosis; presents
  with predominant right-heart failure; must be distinguished from constrictive pericarditis. (1m)
- **Pearl (½m):** echocardiography is the key bedside test that separates the three patterns. (½m)

### Q4. Short note: Cardiogenic shock (5m)
<!-- id: sys02-saq-q4 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on cardiogenic shock.

- **Definition (1m):** cardiogenic shock = **tissue hypoperfusion due to primary cardiac pump failure** —
  sustained **hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg)** with adequate filling, plus end-organ hypoperfusion (oliguria,
  altered mentation, cool peripheries, lactic acidosis). (1m)
- **Causes (1m):** **acute MI with extensive LV failure (commonest)**, mechanical complications (acute MR,
  VSD, free-wall rupture), fulminant myocarditis, end-stage cardiomyopathy, arrhythmia, massive PE,
  tamponade. (1m)
- **Features (1m):** hypotension, tachycardia, **cold clammy mottled skin**, raised JVP, pulmonary oedema,
  oliguria, confusion; haemodynamics — low cardiac output/index, high filling pressures. (1m)
- **Management (1½m):** ICU; **treat the cause — urgent revascularisation (PCI)** in MI; cautious fluids if
  not congested; **inotropes/vasopressors** (noradrenaline ± dobutamine); **mechanical circulatory support**
  (intra-aortic balloon pump / Impella / VA-ECMO) in refractory cases; surgery for mechanical complications;
  treat arrhythmia/correct acidosis. (1½m)
- **Pearl (½m):** mortality is high — early identification, monitoring and **reperfusion** are the priorities. (½m)
