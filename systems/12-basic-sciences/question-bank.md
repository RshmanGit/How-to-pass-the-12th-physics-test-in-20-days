### Q1. Short note: Bilirubin metabolism (5m)
<!-- id: sys12-q1 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on the formation, transport and excretion of bilirubin.

- **Source (1m):** ~80% from breakdown of **senescent RBC haemoglobin** (haem → biliverdin → bilirubin by
  **haem oxygenase** + **biliverdin reductase** in reticuloendothelial system/spleen); rest from
  ineffective erythropoiesis and other haemoproteins. (1m)
- **Transport (1m):** **unconjugated** (indirect) bilirubin is **lipid-soluble, water-insoluble**, carried
  in blood **bound to albumin**; not excreted in urine. (1m)
- **Hepatic conjugation (1½m):** taken up by hepatocytes → **conjugated** with glucuronic acid by
  **UDP-glucuronyl transferase** → **conjugated (direct) bilirubin** is **water-soluble**, excreted in bile. (1½m)
- **Excretion / enterohepatic cycle (1m):** gut bacteria convert it to **urobilinogen** → **stercobilin**
  (faecal colour); some urobilinogen reabsorbed → re-excreted in bile or in urine (**urobilin**). (1m)
- **Clinical pearl (½m):** **unconjugated** hyperbilirubinaemia = haemolysis / Gilbert's / neonatal;
  **conjugated** = hepatocellular or obstructive (bile not reaching gut → **pale stools, dark urine**). (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate:**
- [ ] **Flowchart** — Hb → haem → biliverdin → unconjugated → (albumin) → liver conjugation → bile → urobilinogen — ~1m
- [ ] **Table** — unconjugated vs conjugated (solubility, albumin binding, urine) — ~½m

**Learn it:** Harper's Biochemistry — *Porphyrins & Bile Pigments*. · Harrison's — *Jaundice*. [verify current edition]

### Q2. Short note: Fat-soluble vitamins and their deficiencies (5m)
<!-- id: sys12-q2 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on the fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) and their deficiency states.

- **General principle (1m):** vitamins **A, D, E, K** are **lipid-soluble**, absorbed with dietary fat
  (need bile + pancreatic lipase), **stored** in liver/adipose; **fat malabsorption** (cholestasis,
  pancreatic insufficiency, coeliac) causes combined deficiency, and excess **A/D can be toxic** (stored). (1m)
- **Vitamin A (1m):** retinol; for vision (rhodopsin), epithelial integrity, immunity. **Deficiency:**
  **night blindness → xerophthalmia → Bitot's spots → keratomalacia**; commonest cause of preventable
  childhood blindness. (1m)
- **Vitamin D (1m):** calcium/phosphate homeostasis (gut absorption, bone mineralisation). **Deficiency:**
  **rickets** in children, **osteomalacia** in adults; hypocalcaemia. (1m)
- **Vitamin E (½m):** antioxidant (protects membranes). **Deficiency** (rare): haemolytic anaemia,
  spinocerebellar/peripheral neuropathy. (½m)
- **Vitamin K (1m):** cofactor for **γ-carboxylation** of clotting factors **II, VII, IX, X** + proteins
  C & S. **Deficiency:** bleeding, **prolonged PT/INR**; haemorrhagic disease of newborn (give IM vitamin K
  at birth). (1m)
- **Pearl (½m):** **warfarin** acts by antagonising vitamin K (inhibits epoxide reductase). (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate:**
- [ ] **Table** — vitamin · function · deficiency disease (A/D/E/K rows) — ~1½m

**Learn it:** Harper's Biochemistry — *Micronutrients: Vitamins*. · Harrison's — *Vitamin & Trace-Mineral Deficiency*. [verify]

### Q3. Short note: Mechanism and classes of antihypertensive drugs (5m)
<!-- id: sys12-q3 -->

**Restated:** Classify antihypertensive drugs and state the mechanism of each class.

- **Principle (½m):** BP = cardiac output × peripheral resistance; drugs lower one or both, or reduce
  blood volume. (½m)
- **ACE inhibitors / ARBs (1m):** **enalapril, ramipril / losartan, telmisartan** — block the
  renin-angiotensin system → vasodilation, less aldosterone. First-line, esp. diabetes/CKD/heart failure.
  ACEi → dry cough; **both contraindicated in pregnancy**. (1m)
- **Calcium-channel blockers (1m):** **amlodipine, nifedipine** (dihydropyridine — vasodilator) ·
  **verapamil/diltiazem** (rate-limiting) → relax vascular smooth muscle. Side effect: ankle oedema. (1m)
- **Diuretics (1m):** **thiazides (hydrochlorothiazide, chlortalidone)** reduce volume/peripheral
  resistance; watch **hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia**. (1m)
- **Beta-blockers (½m):** **metoprolol, atenolol** — reduce cardiac output/renin (used with comorbid
  IHD/HF, not first-line for uncomplicated HTN). (½m)
- **Others (1m):** **α-blockers** (prazosin), **central** (clonidine, **methyldopa — safe in pregnancy**),
  **direct vasodilators** (hydralazine), **K-sparing/aldosterone antagonist** (spironolactone — resistant
  HTN). (1m)

**What to draw / tabulate:**
- [ ] **Table** — class · example drug · mechanism · key caution — ~1½m

**Learn it:** KD Tripathi *Essentials of Medical Pharmacology* — *Antihypertensive Drugs*. · Harrison's — *Hypertensive Vascular Disease*. [verify]

### Q4. Short note: Insulin preparations and pharmacology (5m)
<!-- id: sys12-q4 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on insulin — its actions, preparations and clinical use.

- **Action (1m):** anabolic hormone — promotes **glucose uptake** (muscle/fat via GLUT-4), glycogen/
  protein/fat synthesis, **lowers blood glucose**; suppresses gluconeogenesis/lipolysis. (1m)
- **Classification by onset/duration (2m):**
  - **Rapid-acting analogues** — lispro, aspart, glulisine: onset ~15 min, duration 3–5 h (with meals). (½m)
  - **Short-acting (regular)** — onset ~30 min, duration 6–8 h. (½m)
  - **Intermediate (NPH)** — onset 1–2 h, duration 12–18 h. (½m)
  - **Long-acting analogues** — glargine, detemir, degludec: ~24 h, flat profile (basal). (½m)
- **Regimens (1m):** **basal-bolus** (long-acting + premeal rapid), twice-daily premixed, or basal-only;
  **type 1 DM always needs insulin**; route usually **subcutaneous** (IV regular insulin in emergencies/DKA). (1m)
- **Adverse effects / pearl (1m):** **hypoglycaemia** (commonest, dangerous), weight gain, lipodystrophy
  at injection sites (rotate sites); in **DKA** give **IV regular insulin** infusion + fluids + potassium. (1m)

**What to draw / tabulate:**
- [ ] **Table** — preparation · onset · peak · duration — ~1½m

**Learn it:** KD Tripathi — *Insulin & Oral Hypoglycaemics*. · Harrison's — *Diabetes Mellitus: Management*. [verify]

### Q5. Short note: Acid-base physiology (5m)
<!-- id: sys12-q5 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on acid-base balance and the classification of its disorders.

- **Normal values (1m):** arterial **pH 7.35–7.45**, **PaCO₂ 35–45 mmHg**, **HCO₃⁻ 22–26 mmol/L**;
  acidaemia <7.35, alkalaemia >7.45. (1m)
- **Buffering / regulation (1m):** main buffer is **bicarbonate–carbonic acid** (CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌
  H⁺ + HCO₃⁻); **lungs** adjust CO₂ (fast), **kidneys** adjust HCO₃⁻ and excrete H⁺ (slow, days). (1m)
- **Four primary disorders (1½m):**
  - **Metabolic acidosis** — ↓HCO₃⁻ (DKA, lactic acidosis, renal failure, diarrhoea). (½m)
  - **Metabolic alkalosis** — ↑HCO₃⁻ (vomiting, diuretics). (¼m)
  - **Respiratory acidosis** — ↑CO₂ (hypoventilation/COPD). (¼m)
  - **Respiratory alkalosis** — ↓CO₂ (hyperventilation). (½m)
- **Anion gap (1m):** **AG = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻)**, normal **8–12 mmol/L**; **high-AG** metabolic acidosis
  = added acid (lactate, ketones, toxins — "MUDPILES"); **normal-AG** = HCO₃⁻ loss (diarrhoea, RTA). (1m)
- **Compensation pearl (½m):** the body **partially** compensates (lungs for metabolic, kidneys for
  respiratory) but **never overcorrects** past normal pH. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate:**
- [ ] **Table** — disorder · primary change · expected compensation — ~1½m

**Learn it:** Ganong's *Review of Medical Physiology* — *Regulation of ECF pH*. · Harrison's — *Acidosis & Alkalosis*. [verify]

### Q6. Short note: Body-fluid compartments and IV fluid therapy (5m)
<!-- id: sys12-q6 -->

**Restated:** Describe the body-fluid compartments and the principles of intravenous fluid therapy.

- **Compartments (1½m):** total body water ≈ **60%** of body weight, split into **intracellular (⅔, ~40%)**
  and **extracellular (⅓, ~20%)**; ECF = **interstitial (~15%)** + **plasma (~5%)**. Membranes are freely
  permeable to water; Na⁺ is the main ECF cation, K⁺ the main ICF cation. (1½m)
- **Crystalloids (1½m):** small molecules distributing across ECF —
  - **0.9% normal saline** (isotonic; large volumes → hyperchloraemic acidosis). (½m)
  - **Ringer's lactate / Hartmann's** (balanced, near-physiological — preferred for resuscitation). (½m)
  - **5% dextrose** (effectively free water once glucose metabolised; for maintenance/free-water deficit,
    NOT resuscitation). (½m)
- **Colloids (½m):** larger molecules (albumin) staying intravascular longer; limited routine role. (½m)
- **Clinical use (1m):** **resuscitation** — boluses of balanced crystalloid/saline for hypovolaemia/shock;
  **maintenance** — water + Na⁺ + K⁺ to daily needs; reassess clinically (HR, BP, urine output, weight). (1m)
- **Pearl (½m):** over-resuscitation → oedema/pulmonary congestion; correct **Na⁺ slowly** (rapid
  correction of chronic hyponatraemia → osmotic demyelination). (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate:**
- [ ] **Diagram** — body-water compartments (ICF / interstitial / plasma with % of body weight) — ~1m
- [ ] **Table** — fluid · tonicity · main use — ~1m

**Learn it:** Ganong's Physiology — *Body Fluid Compartments*. · Harrison's — *Fluid & Electrolyte Disturbances*. [verify]

### Q7. Short note: Inheritance patterns and single-gene disorders (5m)
<!-- id: sys12-q7 -->

**Restated:** Classify the patterns of single-gene (Mendelian) inheritance with examples.

- **Autosomal dominant (1m):** one mutant allele suffices; **vertical** transmission, each child **50%**
  risk, both sexes affected; e.g. **Marfan, neurofibromatosis, Huntington, ADPKD, familial
  hypercholesterolaemia**. (1m)
- **Autosomal recessive (1m):** both alleles mutant (parents are carriers); **horizontal** pattern, **25%**
  affected, **consanguinity** raises risk; e.g. **cystic fibrosis, sickle-cell anaemia, thalassaemia,
  Wilson's disease**. (1m)
- **X-linked recessive (1m):** gene on X; **males affected**, females usually carriers; **no male-to-male
  transmission**; e.g. **haemophilia A/B, Duchenne muscular dystrophy, G6PD deficiency**. (1m)
- **X-linked dominant (½m):** affected males + heterozygous females affected; e.g. vitamin-D-resistant
  rickets. (½m)
- **Mitochondrial (½m):** **maternal** (matrilineal) inheritance only; e.g. Leber's hereditary optic
  neuropathy, MELAS. (½m)
- **Pearl (1m):** terms — penetrance (proportion expressing), expressivity (severity), anticipation
  (earlier/severe over generations, e.g. trinucleotide-repeat disorders). (1m)

**What to draw / tabulate:**
- [ ] **Table** — pattern · risk to offspring · sexes affected · example — ~1½m
- [ ] **Pedigree sketches** — AD (vertical) vs AR (horizontal) vs XLR — ~½m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Principles of Human Genetics*. · Robbins Basic Pathology — *Genetic Disorders*. [verify]

### Q8. Short note: Oncogenes and tumour-suppressor genes (5m)
<!-- id: sys12-q8 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on oncogenes and tumour-suppressor genes in carcinogenesis.

- **Proto-oncogenes / oncogenes (1½m):** normal genes (**proto-oncogenes**) promoting growth/proliferation;
  a **gain-of-function** mutation converts them to **oncogenes** — acts **dominantly** (one mutated allele
  enough). Mechanisms: point mutation, amplification, translocation. Examples — **RAS** (point mutation),
  **MYC** (amplification), **BCR-ABL** (translocation, CML), **HER2/neu** (amplification, breast). (1½m)
- **Tumour-suppressor genes (1½m):** normally **restrain** the cell cycle / promote apoptosis/repair; a
  **loss-of-function** drives cancer and is usually **recessive** — needs **both alleles inactivated**
  (**Knudson "two-hit" hypothesis**). Examples — **TP53** ("guardian of the genome"), **RB1**
  (retinoblastoma), **APC** (colorectal), **BRCA1/2** (breast/ovarian). (1½m)
- **DNA-repair / "caretaker" genes (1m):** mutations raise mutation rate → cancer predisposition; e.g.
  **mismatch-repair genes** (Lynch syndrome / HNPCC). (1m)
- **Pearl (1m):** cancer is **multistep** — accumulation of activated oncogenes + inactivated suppressors
  (e.g. the adenoma-carcinoma sequence in colon cancer). (1m)

**What to draw / tabulate:**
- [ ] **Table** — oncogene vs tumour-suppressor (function, mutation type, dominant/recessive, example) — ~1½m

**Learn it:** Robbins Basic Pathology — *Neoplasia: Molecular Basis of Cancer*. · Harrison's — *Cancer Genetics*. [verify]

### Q9. Short note: The complement system (5m)
<!-- id: sys12-q9 -->

**Restated:** Write a short note on the complement system — its pathways and functions.

- **Definition (1m):** a cascade of **~30 plasma proteins** (synthesised mainly in liver) that, once
  activated sequentially, amplify the immune response; a key part of **innate immunity** and a bridge to
  adaptive immunity. (1m)
- **Three activation pathways (1½m):** all converge on **C3**:
  - **Classical** — antigen–antibody (IgG/IgM) complexes bind C1. (½m)
  - **Alternative** — direct activation on microbial surfaces (spontaneous C3 tickover). (½m)
  - **Lectin** — mannose-binding lectin binds microbial sugars. (½m)
- **Final common path (1m):** C3 → C3b + C3a; → C5 → **C5b–C9 membrane attack complex (MAC)** punching
  pores → **cell lysis**. (1m)
- **Functions ("the 4 jobs") (1m):** **opsonisation** (C3b), **chemotaxis/inflammation** (anaphylatoxins
  C3a, C5a), **MAC-mediated lysis**, and **clearance of immune complexes**. (1m)
- **Clinical pearl (½m):** deficiencies → recurrent infection (esp. **C5–C9 → Neisseria**); **C1-esterase
  inhibitor deficiency → hereditary angioedema**; low C3/C4 in active SLE / post-strep GN. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate:**
- [ ] **Flowchart** — three pathways → C3 → C5 → MAC (C5b–C9) — ~1½m

**Learn it:** Harrison's — *Introduction to the Immune System (Complement)*. · Abbas *Basic Immunology* — *Complement*. [verify]

### Q10. Short note: Pharmacokinetics basics — half-life, clearance and first-pass metabolism (5m)
<!-- id: sys12-q10 -->

**Restated:** Define the basic pharmacokinetic parameters: half-life, clearance, volume of distribution
and first-pass metabolism.

- **Pharmacokinetics overview (1m):** "what the body does to the drug" — **A**bsorption, **D**istribution,
  **M**etabolism, **E**xcretion (ADME). (1m)
- **Half-life (t½) (1m):** time for **plasma concentration to fall by half**; determines dosing frequency
  and time to **steady state (~4–5 half-lives)** and to clear the drug after stopping. (1m)
- **Clearance (Cl) (1m):** volume of plasma **cleared of drug per unit time** (mL/min); sum of hepatic +
  renal (+ other) clearance; determines the **maintenance dose** at steady state. (1m)
- **Volume of distribution (Vd) (½m):** apparent volume relating total drug in body to plasma
  concentration; high Vd = extensive tissue distribution. Relation: **t½ = 0.693 × Vd / Cl**. (½m)
- **First-pass metabolism (1m):** orally absorbed drug passes via **portal vein → liver** and is partly
  metabolised **before** reaching systemic circulation → reduced **bioavailability** (e.g. GTN, propranolol,
  morphine); bypassed by **sublingual / IV / transdermal / rectal** routes. (1m)
- **Pearl (½m):** **bioavailability (F)** = fraction of an oral dose reaching systemic circulation; IV F = 1.
  Loading dose ∝ Vd; maintenance dose ∝ clearance. (½m)

**What to draw / tabulate:**
- [ ] **Concentration-time curve** — exponential decay marking successive half-lives — ~½m
- [ ] **Equation box** — t½ = 0.693 × Vd / Cl; steady state ≈ 4–5 t½ — ~½m

**Learn it:** KD Tripathi — *Pharmacokinetics: Membrane Transport, Absorption & Distribution / Metabolism & Excretion*. · Rang & Dale's Pharmacology — *Drug Elimination & Pharmacokinetics*. [verify]
