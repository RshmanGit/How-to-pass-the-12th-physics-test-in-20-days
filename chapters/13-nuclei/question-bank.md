# Chapter 13 — Nuclei — Question Bank

> CBSE Class 12 Physics (2025-26). Weightage ~4 marks. Answers written in CBSE marking-scheme
> style: **statement -> formula -> substitution -> result with units**.
>
> Constants used throughout:
> `1 u = 931.5 MeV/c^2` (so a mass defect of 1 u releases `931.5 MeV`);
> `R0 = 1.2 x 10^-15 m`; `m_p = 1.007825 u` (¹H atom), `m_n = 1.008665 u`, `m_e = 0.000549 u`;
> `1 MeV = 1.602 x 10^-13 J`; `N_A = 6.023 x 10^23 /mol`; `ln 2 = 0.693`.
> Masses are quoted as **atomic** masses (electrons included), so electron masses cancel in
> binding-energy and most Q-value calculations.

## MCQ & Assertion-Reason

### Q1. Nuclear density vs mass number (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** As the mass number `A` of a nucleus increases, the nuclear mass density:

(a) increases as `A`  (b) increases as `A^(1/3)`  (c) decreases as `1/A`  (d) is independent of `A`

**Answer:** (d) is independent of `A`.
- `R = R0 A^(1/3)` so volume `V = (4/3) pi R0^3 A`, i.e. `V ∝ A`. Mass `∝ A`. Hence density
  `= mass/volume` is the same (~`2.3 x 10^17 kg/m^3`) for all nuclei. ...1m

### Q2. Binding energy per nucleon peak (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** The binding energy per nucleon is **maximum** (≈ 8.8 MeV) for nuclei near mass number:

(a) 2  (b) 56  (c) 120  (d) 238

**Answer:** (b) 56 (the iron/nickel region). These nuclei are the most stable; this is why both
fission of heavy nuclei and fusion of light nuclei release energy — both move products toward the
peak. ...1m

### Q3. Quantities conserved in nuclear decay (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** In any radioactive decay, which set of quantities is conserved?

(a) charge and mass number only  (b) charge, mass number, energy, momentum
(c) energy only  (d) number of protons only

**Answer:** (b). Atomic number (charge), mass number (nucleons), total energy and linear/angular
momentum are all conserved. The number of protons alone is **not** conserved (it changes in beta
decay). ...1m

### Q4. Nature of nuclear force (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** Nuclear forces are:

(a) long-range and charge-dependent  (b) short-range, charge-independent and attractive at nuclear separations
(c) weaker than gravity  (d) purely electrostatic

**Answer:** (b). Nuclear force acts only up to ~few fm, is charge-independent (n-n = n-p = p-p),
is the strongest of the known forces, and saturates (each nucleon interacts only with neighbours).
...1m

### Q5. Isotopes, isobars, isotones (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** ¹⁴₆C and ¹⁴₇N are examples of:

(a) isotopes  (b) isobars  (c) isotones  (d) isomers

**Answer:** (b) isobars — same mass number `A = 14`, different atomic number `Z`.
(Isotopes: same `Z`; isotones: same neutron number `N`.) ...1m

### Q6. Assertion-Reason — mass of nucleus (1m)

**Type: Assertion-Reason.**
**Assertion (A):** The measured mass of a stable nucleus is always less than the sum of the masses
of its constituent nucleons.
**Reason (R):** The "missing" mass (mass defect) has been converted into the binding energy that
holds the nucleus together.

(a) Both A and R true, R is the correct explanation of A
(b) Both A and R true, R is NOT the correct explanation of A
(c) A true, R false
(d) A false, R true

**Answer:** (a). `Delta m = [Z m_p + (A-Z) m_n] - M_nucleus > 0`; this defect appears as
`E_b = Delta m c^2`, so R correctly explains A. ...1m

### Q7. Assertion-Reason — gamma decay (1m)

**Type: Assertion-Reason.**
**Assertion (A):** In gamma decay the mass number and atomic number of the nucleus are unchanged.
**Reason (R):** A gamma photon carries no charge and no mass number; gamma emission only de-excites
the nucleus from a higher to a lower energy state.

(a) Both true, R correct explanation  (b) Both true, R NOT correct explanation
(c) A true, R false  (d) A false, R true

**Answer:** (a). Gamma emission changes neither `Z` nor `A`; it merely releases the excess energy
of an excited nucleus as a photon, so R correctly explains A. ...1m

### Q8. Assertion-Reason — half-life and activity (1m)

**Type: Assertion-Reason.**
**Assertion (A):** A sample with a shorter half-life has a higher initial activity than an equal
number of nuclei with a longer half-life.
**Reason (R):** Activity `A = lambda N` and `lambda = ln2 / T_(1/2)`, so a smaller half-life means
a larger decay constant.

(a) Both true, R correct explanation  (b) Both true, R NOT correct explanation
(c) A true, R false  (d) A false, R true

**Answer:** (a). For the same `N`, `A = lambda N` and `lambda = 0.693/T_(1/2)`, so smaller
`T_(1/2)` -> larger `lambda` -> larger activity. R correctly explains A. ...1m

## Short Answer (2-3m)

### Q9. Mass defect and binding energy (2m)
<!-- id: ch13-q9 -->

**Q (own words):** Define mass defect and binding energy of a nucleus. Write the relation between them.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Mass defect** `Delta m`: the difference between the total mass of the free constituent nucleons
  and the actual mass of the nucleus:
  `Delta m = [Z m_p + (A-Z) m_n] - M_nucleus`. ...1m
- **Binding energy** `E_b`: the energy equivalent of the mass defect — the energy needed to break
  the nucleus into its free nucleons (or released when they combine):
  `E_b = Delta m c^2` (in MeV, `E_b = Delta m (in u) x 931.5`). ...1m

### Q10. Binding energy per nucleon and stability (2m)

**Q (own words):** What is binding energy per nucleon? How does it indicate nuclear stability?

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Definition:** `E_bn = E_b / A` — average energy needed to remove one nucleon. ...1m
- **Stability:** A larger `E_bn` means a more tightly bound, more stable nucleus. The curve peaks
  (~8.8 MeV) near `A = 56`, so mid-mass nuclei are most stable; very light and very heavy nuclei
  have smaller `E_bn` and are less stable. ...1m

### Q11. Why fission and fusion both release energy (2-3m)
<!-- id: ch13-q11 -->

**Q (own words):** Using the binding-energy-per-nucleon curve, explain why energy is released in
both nuclear fission of heavy nuclei and nuclear fusion of light nuclei.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- The `E_bn` curve **rises** sharply for light nuclei, **peaks** near `A = 56`, then **falls**
  slowly for heavy nuclei. ...1m
- **Fission:** a heavy nucleus (`E_bn ≈ 7.6 MeV`) splits into two mid-mass fragments
  (`E_bn ≈ 8.5 MeV`). Products are more tightly bound -> mass defect -> energy released. ...0.5m
- **Fusion:** light nuclei (very small `E_bn`) combine into a heavier nucleus with larger `E_bn`.
  Again products are more tightly bound -> energy released. ...0.5m
- In both, nucleons move **toward the peak** of higher binding energy per nucleon. ...0.5m

**Diagram — binding energy per nucleon curve** (draw it; do not just describe):
- [ ] Axes: x = mass number `A`, y = `E_b/A` in MeV
- [ ] Curve rises steeply, peaks ~8.8 MeV near `A = 56` (Fe), then falls gently to ~7.6 MeV at `A = 238`
- [ ] Mark the peak region (most stable) and label fusion side (low A) and fission side (high A)
- Earns: ~1m for correctly shaped/labelled curve

### Q12. Nuclear force properties (2-3m)

**Q (own words):** State any three characteristic properties of nuclear forces.

**Marking-scheme answer (any three, ~1m each):**
- **Short-range:** effective only up to ~`2-3 fm`; negligible beyond, but strongly repulsive at
  very small (< 0.7 fm) separations (prevents collapse).
- **Charge-independent:** the force between two protons, two neutrons, or a proton-neutron pair is
  essentially the same (independent of charge).
- **Strongest known force:** much stronger than the electrostatic and gravitational forces at
  nuclear distances.
- **Saturation:** a nucleon interacts only with its immediate neighbours, which is why `E_bn` is
  nearly constant (~8 MeV) over a wide range of `A`.

### Q13. Three types of radioactive decay (3m)
<!-- id: ch13-q13 -->

**Q (own words):** Write the general nuclear equations for alpha, beta-minus and gamma decay, and
state how `Z` and `A` change in each.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Alpha decay:** `_(Z)^(A)X -> _(Z-2)^(A-4)Y + _(2)^(4)He`. `Z` decreases by 2, `A` by 4. ...1m
- **Beta-minus decay:** `_(Z)^(A)X -> _(Z+1)^(A)Y + _(-1)^(0)e + nu-bar` (a neutron -> proton +
  electron + antineutrino). `Z` increases by 1, `A` unchanged. ...1m
- **Gamma decay:** `_(Z)^(A)X* -> _(Z)^(A)X + gamma` (excited nucleus de-excites). Both `Z` and `A`
  unchanged. ...1m

### Q14. Half-life and mean life (2m)
<!-- id: ch13-q14 -->

**Q (own words):** Define half-life and mean (average) life of a radioactive sample and relate both
to the decay constant `lambda`.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Half-life** `T_(1/2)`: time for half the nuclei to decay. `T_(1/2) = ln2/lambda = 0.693/lambda`.
  ...1m
- **Mean life** `tau`: average lifetime of a nucleus. `tau = 1/lambda`. Hence
  `T_(1/2) = 0.693 tau`. ...1m

### Q15. Activity of a sample (2m)

**Q (own words):** Define the activity of a radioactive sample, give its SI unit, and write its
relation to the number of undecayed nuclei.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Activity** `R`: the rate of disintegration, `R = -dN/dt = lambda N`. ...1m
- **SI unit:** becquerel (Bq) = 1 disintegration per second. (Old unit: curie,
  `1 Ci = 3.7 x 10^10 Bq`.) Since `N = N0 e^(-lambda t)`, activity also decays as
  `R = R0 e^(-lambda t)`. ...1m

### Q16. Mass-energy equivalence; meaning of 1 u = 931.5 MeV (2m)

**Q (own words):** State Einstein's mass-energy relation and show why a mass of 1 atomic mass unit
is equivalent to 931.5 MeV.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Relation:** `E = m c^2` — mass and energy are interconvertible. ...1m
- **Conversion:** `1 u = 1.6605 x 10^-27 kg`, `c = 2.998 x 10^8 m/s`.
  `E = (1.6605 x 10^-27)(2.998 x 10^8)^2 = 1.492 x 10^-10 J`.
  Dividing by `1.602 x 10^-13 J/MeV` gives `E ≈ 931.5 MeV`. So `1 u <-> 931.5 MeV`. ...1m

## Derivations & Long Answer

### Q17. Nuclear density is independent of mass number (3m)
<!-- id: ch13-q17 -->

**Q (own words):** Using `R = R0 A^(1/3)`, prove that the density of nuclear matter is the same for
all nuclei (independent of `A`), and estimate its value.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Mass of nucleus:** `M ≈ A m` where `m ≈ 1.66 x 10^-27 kg` is the average nucleon mass. ...0.5m
- **Volume:** `V = (4/3) pi R^3 = (4/3) pi (R0 A^(1/3))^3 = (4/3) pi R0^3 A`. ...1m
- **Density:**
  `rho = M/V = (A m) / ((4/3) pi R0^3 A) = m / ((4/3) pi R0^3)`. ...0.5m
- The factor `A` cancels, so `rho` is **independent of `A`**. ...0.5m
- **Estimate:** with `m = 1.66 x 10^-27 kg`, `R0 = 1.2 x 10^-15 m`:
  `rho = (1.66 x 10^-27)/((4/3) pi (1.2 x 10^-15)^3) ≈ 2.3 x 10^17 kg/m^3`. ...0.5m
- **Boxed:** `rho ≈ 2.3 x 10^17 kg/m^3` (same for every nucleus).

### Q18. Radioactive decay law (3-5m)
<!-- id: ch13-q18 -->

**Q (own words):** State the law of radioactive decay and derive `N = N0 e^(-lambda t)`. Hence
obtain the relation between half-life and decay constant.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Law (statement):** the rate of disintegration is proportional to the number of undecayed
  nuclei present at that instant:
  `-dN/dt ∝ N => -dN/dt = lambda N`, where `lambda` = decay constant. ...1m
- **Set up and integrate:**
  `dN/N = -lambda dt`. Integrating from `N0` (at `t=0`) to `N` (at `t`):
  `integral(N0->N) dN/N = -lambda integral(0->t) dt`
  `=> ln(N/N0) = -lambda t`. ...1.5m
- **Result:** `N = N0 e^(-lambda t)`. ...0.5m
- **Half-life:** set `N = N0/2` at `t = T_(1/2)`:
  `1/2 = e^(-lambda T_(1/2)) => lambda T_(1/2) = ln2`
  `=> T_(1/2) = ln2 / lambda = 0.693/lambda`. ...1m

**Diagram — exponential decay curve** (optional, scores in 5m version):
- [ ] Axes: x = time `t`, y = number of nuclei `N`
- [ ] Smooth decaying exponential from `N0`
- [ ] Mark `N0`, `N0/2` at `t = T_(1/2)`, `N0/4` at `2 T_(1/2)`
- Earns: ~0.5-1m for correctly labelled curve

### Q19. Relate decay constant to half-life and mean life (3m)

**Q (own words):** Starting from `N = N0 e^(-lambda t)`, derive the relations
`T_(1/2) = 0.693/lambda` and `tau = 1/lambda`, and hence `T_(1/2) = 0.693 tau`.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Half-life:** at `t = T_(1/2)`, `N = N0/2`:
  `e^(-lambda T_(1/2)) = 1/2 => T_(1/2) = ln2/lambda = 0.693/lambda`. ...1m
- **Mean life:** `tau = (integral t dN) / (total) = integral(0->inf) lambda t e^(-lambda t) dt
  = 1/lambda`. ...1.5m
- **Combine:** `T_(1/2) = 0.693/lambda = 0.693 tau`. ...0.5m

## Numericals

### Q20. Binding energy of nitrogen-14 (3m)
<!-- id: ch13-q20 -->

**Q (own words):** Find the binding energy of ¹⁴₇N. Given atomic mass `M(¹⁴N) = 14.00307 u`,
`m_H = 1.007825 u`, `m_n = 1.008665 u`.

**Solution:**
- `Z = 7, A-Z = 7`. Mass defect (using atomic masses, electrons cancel):
  `Delta m = 7 m_H + 7 m_n - M(¹⁴N)`
  `= 7(1.007825) + 7(1.008665) - 14.00307`
  `= 7.054775 + 7.060655 - 14.00307 = 0.11236 u`. ...1.5m
- `E_b = Delta m x 931.5 = 0.11236 x 931.5`. ...1m
- **`E_b ≈ 104.7 MeV`** (≈ 7.48 MeV/nucleon). ...0.5m

### Q21. Binding energy per nucleon of iron-56 (3m)
<!-- id: ch13-q21 -->

**Q (own words):** Calculate the binding energy and binding energy per nucleon of ⁵⁶₂₆Fe.
Given `M(⁵⁶Fe) = 55.934939 u`, `m_H = 1.007825 u`, `m_n = 1.008665 u`.

**Solution:**
- `Z = 26, A-Z = 30`.
  `Delta m = 26(1.007825) + 30(1.008665) - 55.934939`
  `= 26.20345 + 30.25995 - 55.934939 = 0.528461 u`. ...1.5m
- `E_b = 0.528461 x 931.5 ≈ 492.3 MeV`. ...1m
- `E_bn = 492.3 / 56 ≈ 8.79 MeV/nucleon`. ...0.5m
- **Answer:** `E_b ≈ 492.3 MeV`, `E_bn ≈ 8.79 MeV/nucleon` (one of the most stable nuclei).

### Q22. Energy released per fission of U-235; energy from 1 kg (3m)
<!-- id: ch13-q22 -->

**Q (own words):** Each fission of ²³⁵U releases about 200 MeV. How much energy (in joules) is
released by the complete fission of 1 kg of ²³⁵U?

**Solution:**
- Number of atoms in 1 kg: `N = (mass/molar mass) x N_A = (1000 g / 235 g) x 6.023 x 10^23`
  `= 2.56 x 10^24` atoms. ...1m
- Total energy `= N x 200 MeV = 2.56 x 10^24 x 200 = 5.12 x 10^26 MeV`. ...1m
- Convert: `x 1.6 x 10^-13 J/MeV = 8.2 x 10^13 J`. ...0.5m
- **Answer:** `≈ 8.2 x 10^13 J` (≈ 5.1 x 10^26 MeV). ...0.5m

### Q23. Fraction remaining after n half-lives (2m)

**Q (own words):** A radioactive sample decays to `3.125%` of its initial amount. Express this as a
fraction and find how many half-lives have elapsed; hence the time in terms of `T_(1/2)`.

**Solution:**
- `3.125% = 3.125/100 = 1/32 = (1/2)^5`. ...1m
- After `n` half-lives, fraction left `= (1/2)^n`. So `n = 5`. ...0.5m
- **Time `= 5 T_(1/2)`.** ...0.5m

### Q24. Activity / disintegration rate of strontium-90 (3m)
<!-- id: ch13-q24 -->

**Q (own words):** Find the disintegration rate (activity) of `15 mg` of ⁹⁰₃₈Sr. Half-life
`T_(1/2) = 28 years`. (`1 year = 3.15 x 10^7 s`.)

**Solution:**
- Number of nuclei: `N = (0.015 g / 90 g/mol) x 6.023 x 10^23 = 1.0 x 10^20` atoms. ...1m
- Decay constant: `lambda = 0.693 / T_(1/2) = 0.693 / (28 x 3.15 x 10^7 s) = 7.85 x 10^-10 /s`.
  ...1m
- Activity: `R = lambda N = (7.85 x 10^-10)(1.0 x 10^20) ≈ 7.85 x 10^10 disintegrations/s`. ...1m
- **Answer:** `R ≈ 7.9 x 10^10 Bq`.

### Q25. Ratio of nuclear radii (2m)
<!-- id: ch13-q25 -->

**Q (own words):** Find the ratio of the nuclear radii of gold ¹⁹⁷₇₉Au to silver ¹⁰⁷₄₇Ag.

**Solution:**
- `R = R0 A^(1/3)`, so `R_Au / R_Ag = (A_Au / A_Ag)^(1/3) = (197/107)^(1/3)`. ...1m
- `= (1.841)^(1/3) ≈ 1.23`. ...0.5m

### Q26. Q-value of alpha decay of radium-226 (3m)
<!-- id: ch13-q26 -->

**Q (own words):** Find the Q-value (energy released) in the alpha decay
`²²⁶₈₈Ra -> ²²²₈₆Rn + ⁴₂He`. Atomic masses: `M(Ra) = 226.02540 u`, `M(Rn) = 222.01758 u`,
`M(He) = 4.00260 u`.

**Solution:**
- `Q = [M(Ra) - M(Rn) - M(He)] x 931.5` (atomic masses; electrons balance). ...1m
- `Delta m = 226.02540 - 222.01758 - 4.00260 = 0.00522 u`. ...1m
- `Q = 0.00522 x 931.5 ≈ 4.86 MeV`. ...0.5m
- Decay is exothermic (`Q > 0`), so it occurs spontaneously. ...0.5m

### Q27. Energy released in D-T fusion (2m)
<!-- id: ch13-q27 -->

**Q (own words):** In the fusion reaction `²₁H + ³₁H -> ⁴₂He + ¹₀n`, find the energy released.
Masses: `M(²H) = 2.014102 u`, `M(³H) = 3.016049 u`, `M(⁴He) = 4.002603 u`, `m_n = 1.008665 u`.

**Solution:**
- `Delta m = [M(²H) + M(³H)] - [M(⁴He) + m_n]`
  `= (2.014102 + 3.016049) - (4.002603 + 1.008665) = 5.030151 - 5.011268 = 0.018883 u`. ...1m
- `Q = 0.018883 x 931.5 ≈ 17.6 MeV`. ...1m
- **Answer:** `≈ 17.6 MeV` released per fusion.

### Q28. Carbon-14 radioactive dating (3m)
<!-- id: ch13-q28 -->

**Q (own words):** A specimen from an old site gives `9` ¹⁴C decays per minute per gram, while a
living sample gives `15` per minute per gram. Half-life of ¹⁴C is `5730 years`. Estimate the age of
the specimen.

**Solution:**
- Activity ratio: `R/R0 = N/N0 = 9/15 = 0.6`. ...1m
- `N = N0 e^(-lambda t) => t = (1/lambda) ln(N0/N) = (T_(1/2)/0.693) ln(15/9)`. ...1m
- `t = (5730/0.693) x ln(1.667) = 8268 x 0.5108 ≈ 4223 years`. ...0.5m
- **Answer:** `t ≈ 4.2 x 10^3 years`. ...0.5m

## Case-Study

### Q29. Case study — nuclear fission and the reactor (4m)
<!-- id: ch13-q29 -->

**Passage (own words):** When a slow neutron is absorbed by a ²³⁵U nucleus it becomes unstable and
splits into two medium-mass fragments, releasing ~200 MeV and 2-3 fast neutrons. These neutrons can
trigger further fissions — a chain reaction. In a power reactor the chain is kept just self-sustaining
(controlled). A typical fission is
`²³⁵₉₂U + ¹₀n -> ¹⁴¹₅₆Ba + ⁹²₃₆Kr + 3 ¹₀n + energy`.

**Q29.1 (1m):** Why are *slow* (thermal) neutrons more effective than fast neutrons in causing the
fission of ²³⁵U?
**Ans:** Slow neutrons have a much higher probability (capture cross-section) of being absorbed by
²³⁵U, so they trigger fission far more readily than fast neutrons. ...1m

**Q29.2 (1m):** What is the role of a **moderator**, and name a common one.
**Ans:** A moderator (e.g. heavy water `D2O` or graphite) slows the fast fission neutrons down to
thermal speeds by repeated elastic collisions, so they can sustain the chain reaction. ...1m

**Q29.3 (1m):** What is the role of **control rods**, and name the material used.
**Ans:** Control rods (e.g. cadmium or boron) absorb excess neutrons, regulating the reaction rate
and keeping the chain reaction just critical (self-sustaining, not runaway). ...1m

**Q29.4 (1m):** Where does the ~200 MeV released per fission mainly come from?
**Ans:** From the increase in binding energy per nucleon — the fragments are more tightly bound than
²³⁵U, so the mass defect `Delta m` appears as energy via `E = Delta m c^2` (mostly as kinetic energy
of the fragments). ...1m
