### Q1. Drift velocity vs current (1m)

A current I flows in a conductor of cross-section A carrying n free electrons per unit volume. If the same current flows in a wire of **half** the cross-section (same material), the drift velocity becomes:

(a) half (b) double (c) unchanged (d) four times

**Answer: (b) double.**
Reason: I = neA v_d ⇒ v_d ∝ 1/A at fixed I. Half the area ⇒ twice the drift velocity.

---

### Q2. Effect of temperature on drift velocity (1m)

When a metallic conductor is heated (voltage across it kept constant), the drift velocity of free electrons:

(a) increases (b) decreases (c) stays constant (d) becomes zero

**Answer: (b) decreases.**
Reason: Heating raises resistance R (lattice vibrations cut relaxation time τ), so at constant V the current I = V/R falls, and v_d ∝ I falls.

---

### Q3. Resistivity and length (1m)

A wire of resistance R is stretched to **twice** its length (volume constant). Its new resistance is:

(a) R (b) 2R (c) 4R (d) R/4

**Answer: (c) 4R.**
Reason: Volume constant ⇒ A halves when length doubles. R = ρl/A ⇒ R ∝ l² ⇒ (2)² = 4R.

---

### Q4. Drift velocity order of magnitude (1m)

The typical drift speed of free electrons in a copper wire carrying ordinary current is of the order:

(a) 10⁸ m/s (b) 10⁶ m/s (c) 10⁻⁴ m/s (d) 10² m/s

**Answer: (c) 10⁻⁴ m/s.**
Reason: Drift speed is tiny (~0.1 mm/s); the *signal* (field) propagates near light speed, not the electrons.

---

### Q5. Temperature coefficient of resistance (1m)

For a typical metallic conductor, the temperature coefficient of resistance α is:

(a) positive (b) negative (c) zero (d) infinite

**Answer: (a) positive.**
Reason: Metal resistance rises with temperature, so α = (R−R₀)/(R₀ΔT) > 0. (Semiconductors have α negative.)

---

### Q6. EMF vs terminal voltage (1m)

The terminal voltage of a cell of EMF ε and internal resistance r, while it **delivers** current I, is:

(a) ε + Ir (b) ε − Ir (c) ε (d) Ir

**Answer: (b) ε − Ir.**
Reason: V = ε − Ir while discharging; terminal voltage is less than EMF because of the internal drop Ir.

---

### Q7. Kirchhoff's junction rule basis (1m)

Kirchhoff's first law (junction rule, ΣI = 0 at a node) is a statement of conservation of:

(a) energy (b) charge (c) momentum (d) potential

**Answer: (b) charge.**
Reason: Charge cannot accumulate at a junction in steady state, so current in = current out.

---

### Q8. Kirchhoff's loop rule basis (1m)

Kirchhoff's second law (loop rule, ΣV = 0 around a closed loop) is a statement of conservation of:

(a) charge (b) momentum (c) energy (d) mass

**Answer: (c) energy.**
Reason: Net change in potential around a closed loop is zero — energy gained from sources equals energy dissipated.

---

### Q9. Wheatstone bridge balance (1m)

A Wheatstone bridge with arms P, Q, R, S is balanced. The galvanometer reads zero when:

(a) P/Q = R/S (b) PQ = RS (c) P/R = S/Q (d) P + Q = R + S

**Answer: (a) P/Q = R/S.**
Reason: Balance ⇒ no current through galvanometer ⇒ ratio condition P/Q = R/S.

---

### Q10. Meter bridge balance length (1m)

In a meter bridge, the null point for an unknown resistance X (with standard Y in the right gap) is at length l (cm) from the left end. Then:

(a) X = Y·l/(100−l) (b) X = Y·(100−l)/l (c) X = Yl (d) X = Y/l

**Answer: (a) X = Y·l/(100−l).**
Reason: X/Y = l/(100−l) since the wire is uniform (resistance ∝ length).

---

### Q11. Potentiometer sensitivity (1m)

The sensitivity of a potentiometer is increased by:

(a) increasing the potential gradient (b) decreasing the potential gradient
(c) using a shorter wire (d) increasing the driver EMF only

**Answer: (b) decreasing the potential gradient.**
Reason: Smaller potential gradient (V/length) ⇒ longer balancing length for a given EMF ⇒ finer reading.

---

### Q12. Why potentiometer beats voltmeter (1m)

A potentiometer measures the EMF of a cell more accurately than a voltmeter because at balance it:

(a) draws maximum current from the cell (b) draws no current from the cell
(c) has very low resistance (d) heats the cell

**Answer: (b) draws no current from the cell.**
Reason: At null point I = 0 through the cell, so there is no Ir drop — it reads true EMF, not terminal voltage.

---

### Q13. Cells in series (1m)

Two cells of EMF ε each and internal resistance r each are joined in **series** to a resistor R. The current is:

(a) 2ε/(R+2r) (b) ε/(R+r) (c) 2ε/(R+r) (d) ε/(R+2r)

**Answer: (a) 2ε/(R+2r).**
Reason: Series: EMFs add (2ε) and internal resistances add (2r).

---

### Q14. Cells in parallel (1m)

Two identical cells (EMF ε, internal resistance r) in **parallel** across R give a current:

(a) 2ε/(R+r) (b) ε/(R+r/2) (c) ε/(R+2r) (d) ε/(2R+r)

**Answer: (b) ε/(R+r/2).**
Reason: Parallel: equivalent EMF = ε, equivalent internal resistance = r/2 ⇒ I = ε/(R + r/2).

---

### Q15. Power transfer (1m)

Maximum power is delivered by a cell of internal resistance r to an external load R when:

(a) R = 0 (b) R = r (c) R ≫ r (d) R = 2r

**Answer: (b) R = r.**
Reason: Maximum power transfer theorem — load matches source internal resistance.

---

### Q16. Conductivity and mobility (1m)

Conductivity σ of a metal in terms of free-electron density n, charge e and mobility μ is:

(a) neμ (b) ne/μ (c) μ/(ne) (d) ne²μ

**Answer: (a) neμ.**
Reason: σ = neμ, where mobility μ = v_d/E = eτ/m.

## Assertion–Reason (Q17–Q22)

> Format: **A** = assertion, **R** = reason. Choose:
> (a) Both A and R true, R correct explanation of A.
> (b) Both A and R true, R NOT the correct explanation.
> (c) A true, R false.
> (d) A false (R may be true/false).

### Q17. Drift velocity and current (1m)

**A:** Even though drift velocity of electrons is only ~10⁻⁴ m/s, a bulb lights up almost instantly when switched on.
**R:** The electric field that drives the electrons is set up through the conductor at nearly the speed of light.

**Answer: (a).** Both true and R explains A — the field, not the slow electrons, reaches the bulb almost instantly.

---

### Q18. Ohm's law (1m)

**A:** A semiconductor diode does not obey Ohm's law.
**R:** Ohm's law states that current is always directly proportional to voltage for every conductor.

**Answer: (c).** A is true (diode is non-ohmic) but R is false — Ohm's law holds only for ohmic conductors at constant physical conditions, not "every conductor."

---

### Q19. Resistance vs temperature (1m)

**A:** The resistance of a metallic wire increases when its temperature rises.
**R:** With rising temperature, the relaxation time of free electrons decreases.

**Answer: (a).** Both true; shorter relaxation time τ raises resistivity ρ = m/(ne²τ), so R rises — R correctly explains A.

---

### Q20. Terminal voltage during charging (1m)

**A:** When a cell is being charged, its terminal voltage is greater than its EMF.
**R:** During charging the current flows into the positive terminal, so V = ε + Ir.

**Answer: (a).** Both true; while charging the Ir drop adds to ε, so V = ε + Ir > ε.

---

### Q21. Wheatstone bridge (1m)

**A:** At balance, the Wheatstone bridge reading is independent of the galvanometer's resistance.
**R:** At balance no current flows through the galvanometer.

**Answer: (a).** Both true; with zero galvanometer current its resistance can't affect the balance — R explains A.

---

### Q22. Kirchhoff's laws (1m)

**A:** Kirchhoff's loop rule can be applied even to circuits that contain capacitors.
**R:** The loop rule is a consequence of conservation of energy (the work done per unit charge around a closed loop is zero).

**Answer: (a).** Both true; the loop rule follows from energy conservation and applies to any closed loop, including ones with capacitors.
