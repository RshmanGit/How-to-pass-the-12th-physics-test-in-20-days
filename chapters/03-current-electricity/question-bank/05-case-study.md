### Q1. Drift, resistivity and temperature (Case 1 — 4 × 1m MCQ)

**Passage.** In a metal, free electrons move randomly with high thermal speeds but zero average velocity. When a potential difference is applied, an electric field E is set up, giving electrons a small **drift velocity** v_d = (eτ/m)E opposite to E, where τ is the relaxation time. The current is I = neAv_d and the resistivity is ρ = m/(ne²τ). For a metal, n is nearly fixed but τ falls as temperature rises, so ρ increases with temperature.

**(i)** The drift velocity of electrons in a wire is of the order of:
(a) 10⁶ m/s (b) 10⁻⁴ m/s (c) 10⁸ m/s (d) 10² m/s
**Answer: (b)** — drift speed is ~0.1 mm/s. …1m

**(ii)** If the current is doubled (same wire), the drift velocity:
(a) halves (b) doubles (c) unchanged (d) quadruples
**Answer: (b)** — v_d = I/(neA) ∝ I. …1m

**(iii)** As the temperature of the metal increases, its resistivity:
(a) decreases (b) increases (c) is unchanged (d) becomes zero
**Answer: (b)** — τ falls, so ρ = m/(ne²τ) rises. …1m

**(iv)** Conductivity σ of the metal equals:
(a) m/(ne²τ) (b) ne²τ/m (c) ne/m (d) neτ
**Answer: (b)** — σ = 1/ρ = ne²τ/m. …1m

### Q2. Cell, terminal voltage and internal resistance (Case 2 — 2 + 1 + 1m)
<!-- id: ch03-cs-q2 -->

**Passage.** A cell of EMF ε maintains a potential difference across its terminals only when no current is drawn. When it drives a current I through an external resistance R, some energy is lost across its internal resistance r, so the terminal voltage drops to V = ε − Ir. A student connects a cell of EMF 1.5 V to a 4 Ω resistor and measures the terminal voltage as 1.2 V.

**(i) (2m)** Find the internal resistance of the cell.
- Current I = V/R = 1.2/4 = 0.3 A. …1m
- r = (ε − V)/I = (1.5 − 1.2)/0.3 = 0.3/0.3 = **1 Ω**. …1m

**(ii) (1m)** What would the terminal voltage be if the cell were on open circuit?
- On open circuit I = 0 ⇒ V = ε = **1.5 V**. …1m

**(iii) (1m)** State one factor that increases a cell's internal resistance.
- Any one: smaller electrode area / larger separation between electrodes / lower electrolyte concentration / lower temperature. …1m

### Q3. Wheatstone bridge & meter bridge in practice (Case 2 — 2 + 1 + 1m)

**Passage.** A meter bridge is a practical form of the Wheatstone bridge that uses a 1 m uniform resistance wire. An unknown resistance X is placed in the left gap and a known resistance Y in the right gap. The jockey is moved until the galvanometer shows no deflection at a balance length l (cm) from the left end, giving X = Y·l/(100−l). For best sensitivity the null point should lie near the middle of the wire.

**(i) (2m)** With Y = 6 Ω the balance point is at 40 cm. Find X.
- X = Y·l/(100−l) = 6 × 40/60. …1m
- X = **4 Ω**. …1m

**(ii) (1m)** Why is the null point kept near the middle (≈ 50 cm) of the wire?
- The bridge is **most sensitive** there — a small change in resistance produces the largest, most easily detected shift in the balance point. …1m

**(iii) (1m)** What is the condition for the bridge to be balanced (galvanometer current zero)?
- The arm ratios are equal: **P/Q = R/S** (for the meter bridge, X/Y = l/(100−l)). …1m
