## MCQ & Assertion-Reason

### Q1. Susceptibility signs and magnitudes (1m)

**Question (own words):** Match each material class to the correct value/sign of magnetic susceptibility χ.

**Answer (MCQ, 1m):**
- Diamagnetic: small **negative** χ (−1 ≤ χ < 0), e.g. χ ≈ −10⁻⁵.
- Paramagnetic: small **positive** χ (0 < χ ≪ 1), e.g. χ ≈ +10⁻⁵ to 10⁻³.
- Ferromagnetic: **large positive** χ (χ ≫ 1, ~10³).

Correct ranking by magnitude: ferromagnetic ≫ paramagnetic ≈ diamagnetic (small), with diamagnetic the only **negative** one. **(1m)**

---

### Q2. Relative permeability classification (1m)

**Question (own words):** A material has relative permeability μᵣ = 0.9983. Classify it.

**Answer (MCQ, 1m):**
- χ = μᵣ − 1 = 0.9983 − 1 = **−1.7 × 10⁻³** (negative) -> **Diamagnetic**.
- (For reference: μᵣ slightly > 1 -> paramagnetic; μᵣ ≫ 1 -> ferromagnetic.) **(1m)**

---

### Q3. Gauss's law for magnetism (1m)

**Question (own words):** What is the net magnetic flux through any closed surface, and what does it tell us?

**Answer (MCQ, 1m):**
- ∮ **B**·d**S** = 0 (Gauss's law in magnetism).
- It means **isolated magnetic poles (monopoles) do not exist** — magnetic field lines are always closed loops. **(1m)**

### Q4. Assertion-Reason — diamagnetic in field (1m)

**Assertion (A):** A diamagnetic material is feebly **repelled** by a strong magnet and tends to move from a stronger to a weaker field region.
**Reason (R):** A diamagnetic material develops a net magnetic moment in the **same** direction as the applied field.

**Answer (Assertion-Reason, 1m):**
- A is **true** (diamagnets are repelled; move to weaker field).
- R is **false** — induced moment is **opposite** (anti-parallel) to the applied field.
- Correct option: **A is true, R is false.** **(1m)**

---

### Q5. Assertion-Reason — Curie's law (1m)

**Assertion (A):** The magnetic susceptibility of a paramagnetic material decreases as temperature increases.
**Reason (R):** By Curie's law χ ∝ 1/T, so higher T gives smaller χ.

**Answer (Assertion-Reason, 1m):**
- A is **true**; R is **true**; and R correctly explains A (thermal agitation randomises dipoles, reducing alignment).
- Correct option: **Both A and R true, and R is the correct explanation of A.** **(1m)**

---

### Q6. Angle of dip at poles and equator (1m)

**Question (own words):** What is the value of the angle of dip (i) at the magnetic equator and (ii) at the magnetic poles?

**Answer (MCQ, 1m):**
- At magnetic **equator**: dip δ = **0°** (field is horizontal).
- At magnetic **poles**: dip δ = **90°** (field is vertical). **(1m)**

### Q7. Distinguish dia-, para- and ferromagnetism (3m)

**Question (own words):** Compare diamagnetic, paramagnetic and ferromagnetic substances on (a) behaviour in a non-uniform field, (b) sign/size of susceptibility, (c) temperature dependence, with one example each.

**Answer (3m):** Tabulated comparison — each correct row ~1m.

| Property | Diamagnetic | Paramagnetic | Ferromagnetic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Behaviour in non-uniform field | Feebly repelled; moves strong→weak | Feebly attracted; moves weak→strong | Strongly attracted; moves weak→strong |
| Susceptibility χ | Small **negative** (≈ −10⁻⁵) | Small **positive** (≈ +10⁻⁵) | **Large positive** (~10³) |
| Temperature dependence | Almost **independent** of T | χ ∝ 1/T (Curie's law) | χ ∝ 1/(T − Tᴄ) above Curie point; loses ferromagnetism above Tᴄ |
| Example | Bismuth, copper, water | Aluminium, sodium, oxygen | Iron, cobalt, nickel |

(b) Net atomic moment: diamagnetic = zero (induced opposite); para/ferro = permanent moments. **(3m total)**

### Q8. Curie's law statement and graph (2m)

**Question (own words):** State Curie's law for a paramagnetic material and sketch the χ–T behaviour.

**Answer (2m):**
- **Statement:** Far from saturation, the magnetisation of a paramagnetic material is directly proportional to the applied field B and inversely proportional to the absolute temperature T:
  M = C·(B/T), so susceptibility **χ = C/T** (χ ∝ 1/T), where C = Curie constant. **(1m)**

- [ ] Smooth **rectangular-hyperbola** curve falling from high χ at low T toward 0 at high T
- [ ] (Optional) a straight line if plotted as χ vs 1/T through origin
- Earns: ~1m for correct shape + labelled axes **(1m)**

### Q9. Permanent magnet vs electromagnet material (3m)

**Question (own words):** What magnetic properties (retentivity, coercivity) should the material of (a) a permanent magnet and (b) the core of an electromagnet/transformer have? Name a suitable material for each.

**Answer (3m):**
- **Permanent magnet** (e.g. **steel / alnico**): needs **high retentivity** (stays magnetised) AND **high coercivity** (not easily demagnetised) -> **fat (wide) hysteresis loop**, large enclosed area. **(1.5m)**
- **Electromagnet / transformer core** (e.g. **soft iron**): needs **high permeability** (strong field for the given current) but **low retentivity AND low coercivity** so it loses magnetism quickly when current stops -> **thin (narrow) hysteresis loop**, small loop area (low energy loss per cycle). **(1.5m)**

### Q10. Why soft iron core increases sensitivity (2m)

**Question (own words):** Why is a soft-iron core used in a moving-coil galvanometer / why does it concentrate field lines?

**Answer (2m):**
- Soft iron has **very high permeability**, so magnetic field lines prefer to pass through it and get **concentrated/crowded** inside the core. **(1m)**
- This makes the field **strong and radial**, increasing the deflecting torque on the coil and hence the galvanometer's **sensitivity**; being soft iron, it loses magnetism instantly when field is removed. **(1m)**

### Q11. Three elements of Earth's magnetism (3m)

**Question (own words):** Define the three quantities used to completely specify Earth's magnetic field at a place: declination, dip (inclination), and horizontal component.

**Answer (3m):** One definition ~1m each.
- **Magnetic declination (D):** the angle between the **geographic meridian** (true north–south plane) and the **magnetic meridian** (vertical plane through the compass needle). **(1m)**
- **Angle of dip / inclination (δ):** the angle the Earth's total field **B** makes with the **horizontal** at that place. **(1m)**
- **Horizontal component (Bₕ):** the component of Earth's total field along the horizontal in the magnetic meridian; Bₕ = B cos δ. (Vertical component Bᵥ = B sin δ.) **(1m)**

- [ ] Horizontal component Bₕ and vertical component Bᵥ as the two legs of a right triangle
- [ ] Angle δ (dip) marked between B and the horizontal
- Earns: ~1m if asked with diagram

### Q12. Relations among B, Bₕ, Bᵥ, δ (2m)

**Question (own words):** Derive the relations connecting the total field B, its horizontal component Bₕ, vertical component Bᵥ, and angle of dip δ.

**Answer (2m):**
- Resolving the total field in the magnetic meridian:
  Bₕ = B cos δ,  Bᵥ = B sin δ. **(1m)**
- Therefore **tan δ = Bᵥ / Bₕ** and **B = √(Bₕ² + Bᵥ²)** (equivalently B = Bₕ / cos δ). **(1m)**

### Q13. Bar magnet vs solenoid analogy (2m)

**Question (own words):** In what way is a current-carrying solenoid equivalent to a bar magnet?

**Answer (2m):**
- Both produce the **same pattern of magnetic field lines** outside (closed loops emerging from one end "N", entering the other "S"); both behave as **magnetic dipoles** with two poles that cannot be isolated. **(1m)**
- A solenoid of N turns, current I, area A has magnetic moment **m = NIA**, exactly like a bar magnet of moment m; field on its axis at large r is B = (μ₀/4π)(2m/r³), identical in form to a bar magnet. **(1m)**

## Derivations & Long Answer

### Q14. Bar magnet as an equivalent solenoid — axial field (5m)

**Question (own words):** Show that a uniformly magnetised bar magnet is equivalent to a solenoid, and derive the magnetic field on the axis of a solenoid at a far point, identifying its magnetic moment.

**Answer (5m):**
- **Setup / principle (1m):** Treat the bar magnet as a solenoid of length 2*l*, radius *a*, with *n* turns per unit length carrying current I. Consider a point P on the axis at distance *r* from the centre (r ≫ *l*, *a*). Each elemental ring (width dx, at distance x from centre) carries current (n dx)I.
- **Field of one element (1m):** The axial field of a circular element at P:
  dB = (μ₀/4π) · [2π a² (n I dx)] / [(r − x)² + a²]^{3/2}.
- **Far-point approximation (1m):** For r ≫ x and r ≫ a, denominator ≈ r³:
  dB ≈ (μ₀/4π) · (2π a² n I dx) / r³.
- **Integrate over the length −*l* to +*l* (1m):**
  B = (μ₀/4π)(2π a² n I / r³) ∫_{−l}^{+l} dx = (μ₀/4π) · (2π a² n I · 2*l*) / r³.
- **Identify the moment & result (1m):** Total turns N = n(2*l*); moment **m = N I A** with A = πa². Then
  **B = (μ₀ / 4π) · (2m / r³)** — identical to the axial field of a bar magnet of moment m. ∎

**Diagram — solenoid as bar magnet (draw it):**
- [ ] Solenoid of length 2*l*, radius *a*, N turns, current I
- [ ] Axis with point P at distance r from centre; element dx at distance x
- [ ] N and S ends marked (current sense -> N pole)
- Earns: ~1m for labelled figure

### Q15. Torque on a magnetic dipole in a uniform field (3m)

**Question (own words):** Derive the expression for the torque on a magnetic dipole (bar magnet) of moment m placed at angle θ to a uniform magnetic field B.

**Answer (3m):**
- **Force pair (1m):** Poles +qₘ and −qₘ separated by 2*l* (m = qₘ·2*l*). Forces +qₘB and −qₘB act in opposite directions -> net force = 0, but they form a **couple**.
- **Torque magnitude (1m):** τ = (force) × (perpendicular distance) = (qₘB)(2*l* sin θ) = (qₘ·2*l*) B sin θ = **m B sin θ**.
- **Vector form & result (1m):** **τ = m × B**, magnitude **τ = mB sin θ**, direction perpendicular to the m–B plane. Torque is **maximum (= mB)** at θ = 90° and **zero** at θ = 0° (stable) or 180° (unstable). ∎

**Diagram — dipole in field (draw it):**
- [ ] Bar magnet (dipole moment m) at angle θ to horizontal field B
- [ ] Force +qₘB on N pole and −qₘB on S pole (equal, opposite, parallel lines)
- [ ] Arc showing θ and the couple's sense of rotation
- Earns: ~1m

### Q16. Potential energy of a dipole in a magnetic field (3m)

**Question (own words):** Derive the potential energy of a magnetic dipole of moment m in a uniform field B, and give the values for stable and unstable equilibrium.

**Answer (3m):**
- **Work done rotating the dipole (1m):** Work to turn the dipole through dθ against the torque: dW = τ dθ = mB sin θ dθ.
- **Integrate (taking U = 0 at θ = 90°) (1m):**
  U(θ) = ∫_{90°}^{θ} mB sin θ′ dθ′ = −mB cos θ. So **U = −m·B = −mB cos θ**.
- **Special cases / result (1m):**
  - θ = 0° (m ∥ B): U = **−mB** -> **stable** equilibrium (minimum energy).
  - θ = 180° (m anti-∥ B): U = **+mB** -> **unstable** equilibrium (maximum energy).
  - θ = 90°: U = 0. ∎

### Q17. Time period of oscillation of a bar magnet (3m)

**Question (own words):** A bar magnet of moment m and moment of inertia I oscillates in a uniform horizontal field B. Derive its time period of oscillation.

**Answer (3m):**
- **Restoring torque (1m):** For small angular displacement θ, τ = −mB sin θ ≈ −mBθ (SHM condition, restoring torque ∝ −θ).
- **Equation of motion (1m):** I(d²θ/dt²) = −mBθ -> d²θ/dt² = −(mB/I)θ, angular frequency ω = √(mB/I).
- **Result (1m):** **T = 2π √(I / mB)**. (Used to measure Bₕ of Earth in a vibration magnetometer.) ∎

## Numericals

### Q18. Magnetic moment of a solenoid (2m)

**Question (own words):** A closely wound solenoid of 800 turns has cross-sectional area 2.5 × 10⁻⁴ m² and carries a current of 3.0 A. Find its magnetic moment.

**Answer (2m):**
- Formula: **m = N I A**. **(0.5m)**
- Substitute: m = 800 × 3.0 × (2.5 × 10⁻⁴). **(1m)**
- Result: **m = 0.60 J T⁻¹** (along the axis, behaves like a bar magnet). **(0.5m)**

### Q19. Magnetic moment from torque (2m)

**Question (own words):** A bar magnet placed at 30° to a uniform field B = 0.25 T experiences a torque of 4.5 × 10⁻² N m. Find its magnetic moment.

**Answer (2m):**
- Formula: τ = m B sin θ -> **m = τ / (B sin θ)**. **(0.5m)**
- Substitute: m = (4.5 × 10⁻²) / (0.25 × sin 30°) = (4.5 × 10⁻²) / (0.25 × 0.5). **(1m)**
- Result: **m = 0.36 J T⁻¹**. **(0.5m)**

### Q20. Torque on a solenoid in a field (2m)

**Question (own words):** The solenoid of Q18 (m = 0.6 J T⁻¹) is suspended freely and its axis makes 30° with a uniform field of 0.25 T. Find the torque on it.

**Answer (2m):**
- Formula: **τ = m B sin θ**. **(0.5m)**
- Substitute: τ = 0.6 × 0.25 × sin 30° = 0.6 × 0.25 × 0.5. **(1m)**
- Result: **τ = 7.5 × 10⁻² N m**. **(0.5m)**

### Q21. Earth's total field from Bₕ and dip (2m)

**Question (own words):** At a place the horizontal component of Earth's field is 0.35 G and the angle of dip is 22°. Find the magnitude of Earth's total magnetic field.

**Answer (2m):**
- Formula: Bₕ = B cos δ -> **B = Bₕ / cos δ**. **(0.5m)**
- Substitute: B = 0.35 / cos 22° = 0.35 / 0.927. **(1m)**
- Result: **B ≈ 0.38 G** (= 0.38 × 10⁻⁴ T). **(0.5m)**

---

### Q22. Vertical component of Earth's field (2m)

**Question (own words):** At a place Bₕ = 0.4 G and angle of dip = 60°. Find the vertical component and the total field.

**Answer (2m):**
- Vertical component: Bᵥ = Bₕ tan δ = 0.4 × tan 60° = 0.4 × 1.732 = **0.69 G**. **(1m)**
- Total field: B = Bₕ / cos δ = 0.4 / cos 60° = 0.4 / 0.5 = **0.80 G**. **(1m)**

### Q23. Curie's law — magnetisation at a new B and T (3m)

**Question (own words):** A paramagnetic sample shows magnetisation M₁ = 6.0 J T⁻¹ when placed in a field B₁ = 0.50 T at temperature T₁ = 300 K. What is its magnetisation M₂ in a field B₂ = 0.20 T at T₂ = 200 K? (Curie-law regime.)

**Answer (3m):**
- Law (Curie): M ∝ B/T, so **M₂/M₁ = (B₂/B₁)(T₁/T₂)**. **(1m)**
- Substitute: M₂ = 6.0 × (0.20/0.50) × (300/200) = 6.0 × 0.4 × 1.5. **(1.5m)**
- Result: **M₂ = 3.6 J T⁻¹**. **(0.5m)**

*(NCERT 5.23 variant: M₁ = 8.0 J/T at B₁ = 0.64 T, T₁ = 4.2 K -> M₂ at B₂ = 0.98 T, T₂ = 2.8 K gives M₂ = 8.0 × (0.98/0.64)(4.2/2.8) ≈ 18.4 J/T.)*

### Q24. Moment of inertia from oscillation period (3m)

**Question (own words):** A short bar magnet makes 2.0 oscillations per second in a uniform field B = 5.0 × 10⁻² T. If its magnetic moment is m = 5.95 × 10⁻¹ J T⁻¹, find its moment of inertia I about the suspension axis.

**Answer (3m):**
- Formula: T = 2π √(I / mB) -> **I = mB / (4π² f²)** (since f = 1/T). **(1m)**
- Substitute (f = 2.0 s⁻¹, m = 0.595 J T⁻¹, B = 0.05 T):
  I = (0.595 × 0.05) / (4π² × 2.0²) = 0.02975 / 157.9. **(1.5m)**
- Result: **I ≈ 1.9 × 10⁻⁴ kg m²** (same method as NCERT 5.9; that problem uses m = NIA = 0.377 J T⁻¹ and gives I ≈ 1.2 × 10⁻⁴ kg m²). **(0.5m)**

### Q25. Susceptibility to relative permeability (2m)

**Question (own words):** A solenoid is filled with a material of magnetic susceptibility χ = 1.9 × 10⁻⁵. Find its relative permeability and classify the material.

**Answer (2m):**
- Formula: **μᵣ = 1 + χ**. **(0.5m)**
- Substitute: μᵣ = 1 + 1.9 × 10⁻⁵ = **1.000019**. **(1m)**
- χ small **positive** -> **paramagnetic** material. **(0.5m)**

## Case-Study

### Q26. Case study — Earth's magnetism (4m)

**Passage (own words):** Earth behaves like a giant bar magnet tilted ~11.3° to its rotation axis, with the magnetic south pole near the geographic north. The field at any place is fixed by three elements: declination D, dip δ, and horizontal component Bₕ. A typical place has Bₕ = 0.26 G and δ = 60°.

**(a) (1m)** Why is the magnetic field horizontal at the equator?
- At the magnetic equator the field lines run parallel to the Earth's surface, so dip δ = 0° and the field has **only a horizontal component** (Bᵥ = B sin 0° = 0).

**(b) (1m)** Find the total field B at the given place.
- B = Bₕ / cos δ = 0.26 / cos 60° = 0.26 / 0.5 = **0.52 G**.

**(c) (1m)** Find the vertical component Bᵥ.
- Bᵥ = Bₕ tan δ = 0.26 × tan 60° = 0.26 × 1.732 = **0.45 G**.

**(d) (1m)** Why does a freely suspended magnet at the magnetic pole stand vertical?
- At the pole δ = 90°, so the field is **entirely vertical** (Bₕ = B cos 90° = 0); the needle aligns with the vertical field and a compass becomes useless for direction there.
