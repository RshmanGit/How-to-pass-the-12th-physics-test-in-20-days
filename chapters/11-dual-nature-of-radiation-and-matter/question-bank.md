> the "Dual Nature of Radiation and Matter" + "Atoms & Nuclei" unit; it usually yields one
> short/derivation + one numerical on the board paper). Answers are written as the
> **mark-earning steps** of the CBSE marking scheme, not as essays.

**Constants used:**
h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s; c = 3 × 10⁸ m·s⁻¹; e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C;
mₑ = 9.1 × 10⁻³¹ kg; 1 eV = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J.
Useful shortcuts: hc = 1240 eV·nm; de Broglie of an electron accelerated through V volts:
λ = 1.227/√V nm (V in volts).

## MCQ & Assertion-Reason

### Q1. Effect of intensity on stopping potential (1m)

For a fixed frequency of incident light (above threshold), if the **intensity** of light is
doubled, the stopping potential:

(a) doubles  (b) halves  (c) is unchanged  (d) becomes zero

**Answer: (c) is unchanged.** Stopping potential depends only on KE_max, which depends on
**frequency**, not intensity. Doubling intensity doubles the number of photoelectrons (and hence
the saturation photocurrent) but not their maximum energy. (1m)

---

### Q2. de Broglie wavelength vs accelerating potential (1m)

An electron is accelerated through a potential difference V. Its de Broglie wavelength λ varies
with V as:

(a) λ ∝ V  (b) λ ∝ √V  (c) λ ∝ 1/√V  (d) λ ∝ 1/V

**Answer: (c) λ ∝ 1/√V.** From λ = h/√(2meV), λ is inversely proportional to √V. So a graph of
λ versus 1/√V is a **straight line through the origin**. (1m)

---

### Q3. Comparing de Broglie wavelengths at equal KE (1m)

A proton and an electron have the **same kinetic energy**. Which has the larger de Broglie
wavelength?

(a) proton  (b) electron  (c) equal  (d) depends on charge

**Answer: (b) electron.** λ = h/√(2mK). For equal K, λ ∝ 1/√m. Since mₑ ≪ m_proton, the
**electron** has the much larger wavelength. (1m)

---

### Q4. Photon momentum (1m)

The momentum of a photon of wavelength λ is:

(a) h/λ  (b) hλ  (c) hc/λ  (d) h/(cλ)

**Answer: (a) h/λ.** A photon has energy E = hν = hc/λ and momentum p = E/c = **h/λ**. (Its rest
mass is zero.) (1m)

---

### Q5. Assertion-Reason — threshold frequency (1m)

**Assertion (A):** No photoelectrons are emitted from a metal if the frequency of incident light
is below the threshold frequency, however intense the light.
**Reason (R):** Photoelectric emission is an instantaneous one-photon, one-electron process; one
photon must carry energy ≥ work function to eject an electron.

(a) Both A and R true, R is the correct explanation of A
(b) Both A and R true, R is NOT the correct explanation
(c) A true, R false
(d) A false, R true

**Answer: (a).** A is true (existence of threshold). R is true and correctly explains it: emission
is a single-photon event, so a photon of energy hν < φ₀ cannot eject an electron no matter how
many such photons arrive (high intensity). (1m)

---

### Q6. Assertion-Reason — matter waves (1m)

**Assertion (A):** A moving cricket ball does not show observable wave behaviour, but a moving
electron does.
**Reason (R):** The de Broglie wavelength is inversely proportional to momentum, so a massive
fast object has an immeasurably small wavelength.

(a) Both A and R true, R is the correct explanation of A
(b) Both A and R true, R is NOT the correct explanation
(c) A true, R false
(d) A false, R true

**Answer: (a).** λ = h/p. For a cricket ball p is enormous, so λ ~ 10⁻³⁴ m — far too small to
detect (no aperture that small exists). For an electron λ ~ 10⁻¹⁰ m, comparable to crystal
spacing, so diffraction is observable. R correctly explains A. (1m)

## Short Answer (2-3m)

### Q7. Define work function, threshold frequency and stopping potential (3m)

**Restated:** Define the three key quantities of the photoelectric effect.

- **Work function φ₀ (1m):** The minimum energy required to free the most loosely bound electron
  from the surface of a metal. Measured in eV; a property of the metal.
- **Threshold frequency ν₀ (1m):** The minimum frequency of incident radiation below which no
  photoelectric emission occurs, whatever the intensity. Related to φ₀ by **φ₀ = hν₀**.
- **Stopping potential V₀ (1m):** The minimum (negative) anode potential at which the photocurrent
  becomes zero, i.e. the value that just stops the most energetic photoelectrons:
  **eV₀ = KE_max = ½mv²_max**. It depends on frequency, not on intensity.

---

### Q8. Three experimental laws of the photoelectric effect (2-3m)

**Restated:** State the main experimental observations (laws) of the photoelectric effect.

1. For a given metal and frequency (> ν₀), the **photocurrent (number of electrons emitted/sec)
   is directly proportional to the intensity** of incident light. (1m)
2. There exists a **threshold frequency ν₀** below which no emission occurs, however intense the
   light; KE_max increases linearly with frequency above ν₀ and is **independent of intensity**.
   (1m)
3. The process is **instantaneous** — emission begins with no measurable time lag (≲ 10⁻⁹ s) once
   light of ν > ν₀ falls on the metal. (1m)

---

### Q9. Why wave theory fails to explain the photoelectric effect (2m)

**Restated:** Give two features of the photoelectric effect that the classical wave theory of
light cannot explain.

- **Threshold frequency (1m):** Wave theory says enough energy can be accumulated at any
  frequency by waiting/raising intensity, so it predicts emission at all frequencies — but a
  threshold ν₀ is observed.
- **Instantaneous emission / no intensity dependence of KE_max (1m):** Wave theory predicts a
  time lag for low intensity and that KE_max should rise with intensity. Experiment shows
  **instantaneous** emission and KE_max depending only on frequency. (Einstein's photon picture
  resolves both.)

---

### Q10. de Broglie hypothesis (2m)

**Restated:** State the de Broglie hypothesis and write the expression for the wavelength of a
matter wave.

- **Statement (1m):** Just as radiation has a dual (wave–particle) nature, every moving material
  particle has an associated wave, the **matter wave**, with wavelength λ = h/p.
- **Expression (1m):** For a particle of mass m moving with speed v,
  **λ = h/p = h/(mv).**
  The larger the momentum, the shorter the wavelength — which is why wave behaviour is observable
  only for light particles (electrons), not macroscopic bodies.

### Q11. Effect of frequency and intensity on photoelectrons (2-3m)

**Restated:** How do (i) increasing the frequency and (ii) increasing the intensity of incident
light affect the photoelectric current and the maximum KE of emitted electrons?

| Change | Photocurrent (saturation) | KE_max / stopping potential |
|--------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| ↑ Frequency (ν > ν₀) | unchanged (same no. of photons) | **increases** (eV₀ = h(ν−ν₀)) |
| ↑ Intensity (ν fixed) | **increases** (more photons → more electrons) | unchanged |

- KE_max depends only on **frequency** (Einstein's equation). (1½m)
- Saturation photocurrent depends only on **intensity** (number of incident photons). (1½m)

## Derivations & Long Answer

### Q12. Einstein's photoelectric equation (3m)

**Restated:** State Einstein's photoelectric equation and explain each term; show how it accounts
for the experimental laws.

- **Photon picture (1m):** Light of frequency ν consists of photons each of energy E = hν. In the
  photoelectric effect **one photon is absorbed by one electron** entirely.
- **Energy balance (1m):** Of the absorbed energy hν, an amount φ₀ (work function) is used to free
  the electron from the surface; the rest appears as kinetic energy. Hence
  **hν = φ₀ + KE_max = φ₀ + ½mv²_max.**
  Since φ₀ = hν₀, this is also hν = hν₀ + KE_max, i.e. **KE_max = h(ν − ν₀)** and
  **eV₀ = h(ν − ν₀)**.
- **Explains the laws (1m):**
  - Threshold: if ν < ν₀, hν < φ₀ ⇒ KE_max < 0 ⇒ no emission.
  - KE_max ∝ ν and independent of intensity (one photon, one electron).
  - Photocurrent ∝ intensity (more photons ⇒ more electrons).
  - Instantaneous: single-photon absorption needs no energy build-up.

### Q13. Stopping potential vs frequency graph; find h, φ₀, ν₀ (3-4m)

**Restated:** Starting from Einstein's equation, obtain the relation between stopping potential
and frequency, sketch the V₀–ν graph, and state how h, ν₀ and φ₀ are read from it.

- **Set up (1m):** eV₀ = KE_max = h(ν − ν₀), so
  **V₀ = (h/e)ν − (hν₀/e) = (h/e)ν − φ₀/e.**
- **Graph shape (1m):** This is a straight line of the form y = mx + c on a V₀ (y) vs ν (x) plot.
- **Reading it (1m):**
  - **Slope = h/e** ⇒ Planck's constant h = e × slope (same slope for all metals).
  - **x-intercept (V₀ = 0) = ν₀**, the threshold frequency.
  - **y-intercept = −φ₀/e**, so work function **φ₀ = e × (magnitude of y-intercept)** = hν₀.
- **Diagram — V₀ vs ν straight line** (draw it; do not just describe):
  - [ ] Axes: stopping potential V₀ (y) vs frequency ν (x)
  - [ ] Straight line meeting x-axis at ν₀ (positive intercept)
  - [ ] Line slope marked h/e; negative y-intercept = −φ₀/e
  - [ ] For two metals: **parallel** lines (same slope h/e), different ν₀
  - Earns: ~1m correct straight line + intercepts, ~1m labels/slope

### Q14. Photocurrent vs collector voltage and vs intensity graphs (3m)

**Restated:** Draw and explain (i) photocurrent vs collector plate potential for two intensities,
and (ii) saturation photocurrent vs intensity.

- **(i) I–V curve (1½m):** As collector (anode) potential is made more positive, photocurrent
  rises to a constant **saturation value** (all emitted electrons collected). For negative anode
  potential the current falls and reaches **zero at the stopping potential V₀**. For higher
  intensity (same frequency) the **saturation current is larger but V₀ is the same**.
- **Diagram — photocurrent vs voltage** (draw it):
  - [ ] x-axis collector potential (negative region on left), y-axis photocurrent
  - [ ] Two curves I₁ < I₂ reaching different saturation currents
  - [ ] Both curves cut the voltage axis at the **same** stopping potential −V₀
  - Earns: ~1m curve shape + saturation, ~½m common V₀
- **(ii) Saturation current vs intensity (1½m):** A **straight line through the origin** —
  saturation current ∝ intensity (number of photons ∝ number of photoelectrons).
  - [ ] Straight line from origin, axes labelled (saturation current vs intensity)

### Q15. de Broglie wavelength of an accelerated charged particle (3m)

**Restated:** Derive the expression for the de Broglie wavelength of a particle of charge q and
mass m accelerated from rest through a potential difference V.

- **Energy gained (1m):** Work done by the field = KE gained:
  qV = ½mv² ⇒ v = √(2qV/m).
- **Momentum (1m):** p = mv = m√(2qV/m) = **√(2mqV)** (also p = √(2mK) with K = qV).
- **Wavelength (1m):** By de Broglie λ = h/p ⇒
  **λ = h/√(2mqV).**
  For an electron (q = e, m = mₑ), substituting constants gives the working shortcut
  **λ = 1.227/√V nm** (V in volts).

---

### Q16. Davisson–Germer experiment (3-4m)

**Restated:** Describe the Davisson–Germer experiment and explain how it confirms the wave nature
of electrons.

- **Aim/principle (1m):** To verify de Broglie's hypothesis by demonstrating **diffraction of
  electrons** by a crystal (electrons behave as waves of λ = h/√(2meV)).
- **Setup (1m):** Electrons from a heated filament are accelerated through a variable potential V,
  collimated into a fine beam, and directed at a **nickel single crystal**. A movable detector
  measures the intensity of electrons scattered at angle φ.
- **Observation (1m):** The scattered intensity shows a **distinct peak at V = 54 V and scattering
  angle φ = 50°**. A peak (constructive interference) is the signature of wave diffraction by the
  regular crystal lattice (Bragg-type interference).
- **Verification (1m):**
  - Experimental wavelength from the crystal geometry ≈ **0.165 nm (1.65 Å)**.
  - Theoretical de Broglie value λ = 1.227/√54 nm ≈ **0.167 nm (1.67 Å)**.
  - Close agreement ⇒ confirms electrons have a wave nature with λ = h/p.
- **Diagram — Davisson–Germer setup** (draw it):
  - [ ] Electron gun (filament + accelerating anode, potential V)
  - [ ] Collimated electron beam striking nickel crystal
  - [ ] Movable detector measuring scattered intensity at angle φ
  - Earns: ~1m labelled setup + ~1m peak description

## Numericals

### Q17. Work function and threshold (sodium) (2-3m)

**Restated:** The work function of sodium is 2.14 eV. Find its threshold frequency ν₀ and the
maximum KE (in eV) of photoelectrons when light of frequency 6.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz falls on it.

- φ₀ = hν₀ ⇒ ν₀ = φ₀/h = (2.14 × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹)/(6.63 × 10⁻³⁴)
  = **5.16 × 10¹⁴ Hz.** (1m)
- KE_max = h(ν − ν₀) = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ × (6.0 − 5.16) × 10¹⁴
  = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ × 0.84 × 10¹⁴ = 5.57 × 10⁻²⁰ J.
- In eV: KE_max = (5.57 × 10⁻²⁰)/(1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹) ≈ **0.35 eV.** (1-2m)

(Equivalently hν = 2.48 eV; KE_max = 2.48 − 2.14 = 0.34 eV — small rounding difference.)

### Q18. Energy and momentum of a He–Ne laser photon (2-3m)

**Restated:** Monochromatic light of wavelength 632.8 nm is produced by a He–Ne laser. Find the
energy and momentum of each photon. (NCERT-type.)

- **Energy:** E = hc/λ = (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ × 3 × 10⁸)/(632.8 × 10⁻⁹)
  = **3.14 × 10⁻¹⁹ J ≈ 1.96 eV.** (1½m)
- **Momentum:** p = h/λ = E/c = (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴)/(632.8 × 10⁻⁹)
  = **1.05 × 10⁻²⁷ kg·m·s⁻¹.** (1½m)

### Q19. Stopping potential from incident frequency (2-3m)

**Restated:** Light of wavelength 412.5 nm (energy ≈ 3.0 eV) is incident on a metal of work
function 2.14 eV. Find (i) the maximum KE of photoelectrons and (ii) the stopping potential.

- Photon energy E = hc/λ = 1240/412.5 nm ≈ **3.0 eV.** (1m)
- KE_max = E − φ₀ = 3.0 − 2.14 = **0.86 eV** (= 1.38 × 10⁻¹⁹ J). (1m)
- Stopping potential: eV₀ = KE_max ⇒ V₀ = KE_max/e = **0.86 V.** (1m)

**Restated:** An electron is accelerated from rest through 56 V. Find its de Broglie wavelength.
(NCERT-type.)

  = **0.164 nm (1.64 Å).** (2m)

### Q21. de Broglie wavelength from kinetic energy (2-3m)

**Restated:** Find the de Broglie wavelength of (i) an electron of kinetic energy 120 eV and
(ii) an electron of kinetic energy 100 eV.

- General: λ = h/√(2mK), with K in joules. Equivalently λ = 1.227/√(K in eV) nm.
- (i) K = 120 eV: λ = 1.227/√120 = 1.227/10.95 = **0.112 nm (1.12 Å).** (1½m)
- (ii) K = 100 eV: λ = 1.227/√100 = 1.227/10 = **0.123 nm (1.23 Å).** (1½m)

### Q22. Comparing wavelengths of a photon and an electron of equal energy (2-3m)

**Restated:** A photon and an electron each have energy 100 eV. Compare their de Broglie /
wavelengths (which is larger?).

- **Photon** (E = hc/λ): λ_photon = hc/E = 1240 eV·nm / 100 eV = **12.4 nm.** (1½m)
- **Electron** (treat 100 eV as kinetic energy): λ_electron = 1.227/√100 = **0.123 nm.** (1½m)
- The **photon's wavelength is ~100× larger.** Reason: for the photon λ ∝ 1/E, but for the
  (non-relativistic) electron λ ∝ 1/√E, and the electron also carries far more momentum for the
  same energy. (½m)

### Q23. Maximum frequency and minimum wavelength of X-rays (2-3m)

**Restated:** Electrons are accelerated through 30 kV in an X-ray tube. Find the maximum frequency
and the minimum wavelength of the X-rays produced. (NCERT-type.)

- All the electron's energy → one photon at the cut-off: eV = hν_max.
- ν_max = eV/h = (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 30 × 10³)/(6.63 × 10⁻³⁴)
  = **7.24 × 10¹⁸ Hz.** (1½m)
- λ_min = c/ν_max = (3 × 10⁸)/(7.24 × 10¹⁸)
  = **4.14 × 10⁻¹¹ m = 0.0414 nm.** (1½m)

### Q24. Threshold frequency from incident frequency and electron speed (3m)

**Restated:** Light of frequency 7.21 × 10¹⁴ Hz falls on a metal and ejects electrons of maximum
speed 6.0 × 10⁵ m·s⁻¹. Find the threshold frequency. (NCERT-type.)

- KE_max = ½mv²_max = ½ × 9.1 × 10⁻³¹ × (6.0 × 10⁵)²
  = ½ × 9.1 × 10⁻³¹ × 3.6 × 10¹¹ = 1.638 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. (1m)
- h(ν − ν₀) = KE_max ⇒ ν₀ = ν − KE_max/h
  = 7.21 × 10¹⁴ − (1.638 × 10⁻¹⁹)/(6.63 × 10⁻³⁴). (1m)
- ν₀ = 7.21 × 10¹⁴ − 2.47 × 10¹⁴ = **4.74 × 10¹⁴ Hz.** (1m)

---

### Q25. Number of photons emitted per second by a source (2-3m)

**Restated:** A monochromatic source of wavelength 632.8 nm radiates power 9.42 mW. How many
photons does it emit per second?

- Energy per photon: E = hc/λ = 3.14 × 10⁻¹⁹ J (from Q18). (1m)
- Number per second: n = P/E = (9.42 × 10⁻³)/(3.14 × 10⁻¹⁹)
  = **3.0 × 10¹⁶ photons·s⁻¹.** (2m)

## Case-Study

### Q26. Photoelectric effect — frequency vs intensity (4m)

**Passage:** A clean metal plate of work function φ₀ = 2.0 eV is illuminated. Light A has photon
energy 2.5 eV at intensity I; light B has photon energy 3.5 eV at intensity 2I. The plate is part
of a photocell whose collector potential can be varied.

**(i)** Which light, if any, fails to cause emission? *(1m)*
**Answer:** Neither fails — both photon energies (2.5 eV, 3.5 eV) exceed φ₀ = 2.0 eV. (Light below
2.0 eV would fail regardless of intensity.) (1m)

**(ii)** Find the maximum KE of photoelectrons for each light. *(1m)*
**Answer:** KE_max = E − φ₀.
- Light A: 2.5 − 2.0 = **0.5 eV.**
- Light B: 3.5 − 2.0 = **1.5 eV.** (1m)

**(iii)** Compare the stopping potentials for A and B. *(1m)*
**Answer:** eV₀ = KE_max ⇒ V₀(A) = 0.5 V, V₀(B) = **1.5 V.** B has the larger stopping potential
(depends on frequency, not on the higher intensity). (1m)

**(iv)** Which light gives the larger saturation photocurrent, and why? *(1m)*
**Answer:** **Light B** — its intensity (2I) is greater, so it delivers more photons per second
and ejects more electrons per second. Saturation current ∝ intensity, independent of frequency.
(1m)
