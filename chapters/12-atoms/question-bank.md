## MCQ & Assertion-Reason

### Q1. Quantisation of angular momentum (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** In Bohr's model, the angular momentum of the electron in the nth orbit is:

(a) `n h`  (b) `n h / (2 pi)`  (c) `h / (2 pi n)`  (d) `2 pi n / h`

**Answer:** (b) `L = n h / (2 pi)`.
- Bohr's quantisation postulate allows only orbits where `L = m v r = n h/(2 pi)`, `n = 1, 2, 3,...` ...1m

### Q2. Dependence of orbit radius on n (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** The radius of the nth Bohr orbit of hydrogen is proportional to:

(a) `n`  (b) `1/n`  (c) `n^2`  (d) `1/n^2`

**Answer:** (c) `n^2`. From `r_n = (e0 h^2 / pi m e^2) n^2`, so `r_n proportional to n^2`
(with `r1 = 0.53 angstrom`). ...1m

### Q3. Energy of the ground state (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** The total energy of the electron in the ground state of hydrogen is:

(a) `+13.6 eV`  (b) `-13.6 eV`  (c) `-3.4 eV`  (d) `-27.2 eV`

**Answer:** (b) `-13.6 eV`. `E_n = -13.6/n^2 eV`; for `n = 1`, `E1 = -13.6 eV`. The negative
sign shows the electron is bound. ...1m

### Q4. Speed in nth orbit (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** The speed of the electron in the nth Bohr orbit varies as:

(a) `n`  (b) `1/n`  (c) `n^2`  (d) `1/n^2`

**Answer:** (b) `1/n`. `v_n = e^2/(2 e0 h) . (1/n)`, so `v_n proportional to 1/n`
(`v1 ≈ 2.19 x 10^6 m/s ≈ c/137`). ...1m

### Q5. Frequency of revolution (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** The frequency of revolution of the electron in the nth orbit is proportional to:

(a) `n`  (b) `1/n^2`  (c) `1/n^3`  (d) `n^3`

**Answer:** (c) `1/n^3`. `f = v_n/(2 pi r_n)`; with `v proportional to 1/n` and `r proportional
to n^2`, `f proportional to 1/n^3`. ...1m

### Q6. Series lying in the UV region (1m)

**Type: MCQ.** Which hydrogen spectral series lies entirely in the ultraviolet region?

(a) Lyman  (b) Balmer  (c) Paschen  (d) Brackett

**Answer:** (a) Lyman (transitions ending at `n1 = 1`). Balmer is in visible, Paschen/Brackett in
infrared. ...1m

### Q7. Assertion-Reason — stationary orbits do not radiate (1m)

**Type: Assertion-Reason.**
**Assertion (A):** An electron revolving in a Bohr stationary orbit does not emit radiation.
**Reason (R):** A stationary orbit is one in which the electron's acceleration is zero.

(a) Both A and R true, R is the correct explanation of A
(b) Both A and R true, R is NOT the correct explanation of A
(c) A true, R false
(d) A false, R true

**Answer:** (c) A is true, R is false.
- A is a Bohr postulate: in a permitted (stationary) orbit the electron does **not** radiate. ...0.5m
- R is false: the electron is in circular motion, so it has non-zero centripetal acceleration.
  Bohr simply **postulated** no radiation, contradicting classical theory; he did not claim zero
  acceleration. ...0.5m

### Q8. Assertion-Reason — Bohr model only for one-electron atoms (1m)

**Type: Assertion-Reason.**
**Assertion (A):** Bohr's model successfully explains the spectrum of hydrogen and singly ionised
helium (He+) but fails for neutral helium.
**Reason (R):** Bohr's model does not account for the electron-electron repulsion present in
multi-electron atoms.

(a) Both true, R correct explanation  (b) Both true, R not correct explanation
(c) A true, R false  (d) A false, R true

**Answer:** (a). Bohr's model works for one-electron (hydrogen-like) systems such as H and He+.
For atoms with two or more electrons the mutual electron-electron repulsion is not included, so
the model fails — R correctly explains A. ...1m

## Short Answer (2-3m)

### Q9. State Bohr's postulates (3m)
<!-- id: ch12-q9 -->

**Q (own words):** State the three postulates Bohr used to build his model of the hydrogen atom.

**Marking-scheme answer:** (1 mark each)
1. **Stationary orbits:** The electron revolves only in certain allowed circular orbits without
   emitting radiation; each orbit corresponds to a fixed (stationary) energy state. ...1m
2. **Quantisation of angular momentum:** Only those orbits are allowed in which the angular
   momentum is an integral multiple of `h/(2 pi)`:
   `L = m v r = n h/(2 pi)`, `n = 1, 2, 3,...` ...1m
3. **Frequency (Bohr's frequency) condition:** Radiation is emitted/absorbed only when the electron
   jumps between two stationary states; the photon energy equals the energy difference:
   `h nu = E_i - E_f`. ...1m

### Q10. Why the classical (Rutherford) atom is unstable (2m)

**Q (own words):** On the basis of classical electromagnetism, explain why Rutherford's nuclear
model predicts an unstable atom.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- The orbiting electron is **accelerated** (centripetal acceleration), and an accelerated charge
  must continuously **radiate electromagnetic energy** (classical EM theory). ...1m
- Losing energy, the electron would spiral into the nucleus in ~`10^-8 s`, and the emitted
  spectrum would be **continuous** — contrary to the observed stable atom and its **discrete line
  spectrum**. Hence the classical model is untenable. ...1m

### Q11. Two limitations of Bohr's model (2m)

**Q (own words):** Give two shortcomings/limitations of Bohr's atomic model.

**Marking-scheme answer:** (any two, 1 mark each)
- Applies only to **hydrogen-like (single-electron)** atoms; fails for multi-electron atoms. ...1m
- Cannot explain the **relative intensities** of spectral lines, nor the **fine structure**
  (splitting of lines), nor splitting in magnetic/electric fields (Zeeman/Stark effects). ...1m
- It is a mixture of classical and quantum ideas (an ad-hoc, semi-classical model) and does not
  treat the electron's wave nature. ...(alt)

### Q12. Ground-state KE and PE of hydrogen (2m)
<!-- id: ch12-q12 -->

**Q (own words):** In the ground state of hydrogen the total energy is `-13.6 eV`. Find the
kinetic energy and potential energy of the electron.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- For a Bohr orbit, `KE = -E` and `PE = 2E` (PE is twice the total energy). ...1m
- `KE = +13.6 eV`; `PE = 2 x (-13.6) = -27.2 eV`. ...1m
- Check: `KE + PE = 13.6 - 27.2 = -13.6 eV = E`. ✓

### Q13. Identify the series and the region (2-3m)
<!-- id: ch12-q13 -->

**Q (own words):** An electron in hydrogen makes a transition from `n = 4` to `n = 2`, and another
from `n = 3` to `n = 1`. Name the series each line belongs to and the spectral region.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- Transition **ending at `n1 = 2`** -> **Balmer series**, lies in the **visible** region. ...1m
- Transition **ending at `n1 = 1`** -> **Lyman series**, lies in the **ultraviolet** region. ...1m
- (General rule: `n1 = 1` Lyman/UV, `n1 = 2` Balmer/visible, `n1 = 3` Paschen/IR.) ...1m

### Q14. Maximum number of spectral lines (2m)

**Q (own words):** Hydrogen atoms are excited to the state `n = 4`. What is the maximum number of
distinct spectral lines that can be emitted as they de-excite?

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- Number of lines `= n(n-1)/2` for de-excitation from level `n`. ...1m
- `= 4(4-1)/2 = 6` lines. ...1m
- (These are 4->3, 4->2, 4->1, 3->2, 3->1, 2->1.)

### Q15. Define distance of closest approach (2m)

**Q (own words):** Define the distance of closest approach in alpha-particle scattering, and write
its expression for a head-on collision.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Definition:** The distance of closest approach `r0` is the minimum distance to which an
  alpha-particle directed head-on towards a nucleus can travel before it momentarily stops and
  reverses, i.e. when its entire kinetic energy is converted into electrostatic potential energy. ...1m
- **Expression:** At closest approach `K = (1/(4 pi e0)) (2 Z e^2 / r0)`, so
  `r0 = (1/(4 pi e0)) (2 Z e^2 / K)`. ...1m

### Q16. Effect of doubling K on closest approach (2m)
<!-- id: ch12-q16 -->

**Q (own words):** An alpha-particle of kinetic energy `K` has distance of closest approach `r`
to a gold nucleus. What is the closest approach if its kinetic energy is doubled to `2K`?

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- `r0 = (1/(4 pi e0)) (2 Z e^2 / K)`, so `r0 proportional to 1/K`. ...1m
- Doubling `K` halves `r0`: new distance `= r/2`. ...1m

### Q17. Impact parameter and scattering angle (2m)

**Q (own words):** Define impact parameter. How does the scattering angle depend on it?

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Impact parameter `b`:** the perpendicular distance of the initial velocity vector of the
  alpha-particle from the centre of the nucleus (had there been no force). ...1m
- **Dependence:** small `b` (near head-on) -> large scattering angle (up to 180 deg for a direct
  hit, `b = 0`); large `b` -> small scattering angle. So the rare large-angle scattering proves
  the positive charge is concentrated in a tiny nucleus. ...1m

## Derivations & Long Answer

### Q18. Radius of nth orbit (r proportional to n^2) (3m)
<!-- id: ch12-q18 -->

**Q (own words):** Using Bohr's postulates, derive the expression for the radius of the nth orbit
of the hydrogen atom and show that `r_n proportional to n^2`.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Coulomb = centripetal force** for the orbiting electron:
  `(1/(4 pi e0)) (e^2 / r^2) = m v^2 / r`  =>  `m v^2 = e^2/(4 pi e0 r)`. ...(i) ...1m
- **Bohr quantisation:** `m v r = n h/(2 pi)`  =>  `v = n h/(2 pi m r)`. ...(ii) ...0.5m
- Substitute (ii) into (i) and solve for `r`:
  `r_n = (e0 h^2 / (pi m e^2)) n^2`. ...1m
- Hence **`r_n proportional to n^2`**; numerically `r1 = 0.53 x 10^-10 m` (Bohr radius), so
  `r_n = 0.53 n^2 angstrom`. ...0.5m

**Boxed result:** `r_n = (e0 h^2 / pi m e^2) n^2 = 0.53 n^2 angstrom`.

### Q19. Total energy of electron (E_n = -13.6/n^2 eV) (5m)
<!-- id: ch12-q19 -->

**Q (own words):** Derive the expression for the total energy of the electron in the nth orbit of
hydrogen and show that `E_n = -13.6/n^2 eV`. Explain the significance of the negative sign.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Kinetic energy:** from `m v^2 = e^2/(4 pi e0 r)`,
  `KE = (1/2) m v^2 = e^2/(8 pi e0 r)`. ...1m
- **Potential energy** (electron-nucleus, attractive):
  `PE = -e^2/(4 pi e0 r)`. ...1m
- **Total energy:** `E = KE + PE = e^2/(8 pi e0 r) - e^2/(4 pi e0 r) = -e^2/(8 pi e0 r)`. ...1m
- Substitute the Bohr radius `r_n = (e0 h^2 / pi m e^2) n^2`:
  `E_n = - (m e^4 / (8 e0^2 h^2)) (1/n^2)`. ...1m
- Evaluating the constant: `E_n = -13.6/n^2 eV`. **Negative sign** => the electron is **bound**
  to the nucleus; energy must be supplied to free it (ionisation). ...1m

**Boxed result:** `E_n = - (m e^4 / 8 e0^2 h^2) (1/n^2) = -13.6/n^2 eV`.

### Q20. Bohr quantisation from de Broglie (3m)
<!-- id: ch12-q20 -->

**Q (own words):** Using de Broglie's hypothesis, show that the angular momentum of the electron
in a Bohr orbit is quantised, `L = n h/(2 pi)`.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- For a **stable (standing) electron wave** around the orbit, the circumference must contain a
  whole number of de Broglie wavelengths: `2 pi r = n lambda`, `n = 1, 2, 3,...` ...1m
- de Broglie wavelength: `lambda = h/(m v)`. ...1m
- Substitute: `2 pi r = n h/(m v)`  =>  `m v r = n h/(2 pi)`. This is exactly Bohr's
  quantisation condition. ...1m

### Q21. Rydberg formula for the spectral series (3m)
<!-- id: ch12-q21 -->

**Q (own words):** Using `E_n = -13.6/n^2 eV`, obtain the formula for the wavelength of a hydrogen
spectral line and write the Rydberg relation. Name the series for `n1 = 1, 2, 3`.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- Photon emitted in transition `n2 -> n1`: `h c/lambda = E_{n2} - E_{n1}`. ...1m
- `h c/lambda = 13.6 eV (1/n1^2 - 1/n2^2)`, which gives:
  `1/lambda = R (1/n1^2 - 1/n2^2)`, with `R = 1.097 x 10^7 m^-1` (Rydberg constant). ...1m
- **Series:** `n1 = 1` Lyman (UV); `n1 = 2` Balmer (visible); `n1 = 3` Paschen (IR);
  `n1 = 4` Brackett; `n1 = 5` Pfund (all IR). ...1m

### Q22. Rutherford alpha-scattering experiment (5m)
<!-- id: ch12-q22 -->

**Q (own words):** Describe the Geiger-Marsden (Rutherford) alpha-particle scattering experiment.
State the main observations and the conclusions Rutherford drew about atomic structure.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- **Setup:** A narrow beam of energetic alpha-particles (from a radioactive source) is fired at a
  thin gold foil; a movable ZnS detector (scintillation screen) counts particles scattered at
  various angles. ...1m
- **Observations:** (i) Most alpha-particles pass nearly undeviated; (ii) a small fraction are
  deflected by large angles; (iii) about 1 in 8000 is scattered by more than 90 deg (some bounce
  straight back). ...1.5m
- **Conclusions:** (i) The atom is **mostly empty space** (most pass through). (ii) The entire
  positive charge and almost all the mass are concentrated in a tiny, dense **nucleus**
  (only a near head-on Coulomb repulsion can cause large-angle scattering / back-scattering).
  (iii) Electrons revolve around the nucleus; nuclear size `~10^-15 m << atom ~10^-10 m`. ...1.5m
- **Trajectory note:** the path of an alpha-particle is a **hyperbola** with the nucleus at the
  focus; small impact parameter -> large deflection. ...1m

- [ ] ZnS / scintillation detector that can rotate about the foil; angle `theta` marked
- [ ] Beam paths: most go straight; a few deflected at angle `theta`; one back-scattered (~180 deg)
- [ ] On a separate sketch: nucleus (+Ze) with several alpha trajectories as hyperbolae, impact
      parameter `b` labelled for one of them
- Earns: ~1m apparatus + ~1m trajectory/impact-parameter labels.

### Q24. Radius and energy at n = 2 (2m)

**Q (own words):** The ground-state Bohr radius of hydrogen is `0.53 x 10^-10 m` and `E1 = -13.6
eV`. Find the radius and total energy of the `n = 2` orbit.

### Q25. Distance of closest approach — 5.5 MeV alpha on gold (3m)
<!-- id: ch12-q25 -->

**Q (own words):** A 5.5 MeV alpha-particle is fired head-on at a gold nucleus (`Z = 79`).
Estimate the distance of closest approach.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- At closest approach all KE becomes PE:
  `r0 = (1/(4 pi e0)) (2 Z e^2 / K)`. ...1m
- `K = 5.5 x 10^6 x 1.6 x 10^-19 = 8.8 x 10^-13 J`. ...0.5m
- `r0 = (9 x 10^9)(2)(79)(1.6 x 10^-19)^2 / (8.8 x 10^-13)`. ...0.5m
- `r0 ≈ 4.13 x 10^-14 m ≈ 4.0 x 10^-14 m`. So the gold nucleus is smaller than this. ...1m

### Q26. Distance of closest approach — Z = 80, K = 8 MeV (3m)

**Q (own words):** In a Geiger-Marsden experiment an alpha-particle of kinetic energy `8 MeV`
approaches a nucleus with `Z = 80`. Calculate the distance of closest approach. What happens to it
if `K` is doubled?

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- `r0 = (1/(4 pi e0)) (2 Z e^2 / K)`. ...1m
- `K = 8 x 10^6 x 1.6 x 10^-19 = 1.28 x 10^-12 J`;
  `r0 = (9 x 10^9)(2)(80)(1.6 x 10^-19)^2 / (1.28 x 10^-12)`. ...1m
- `r0 ≈ 2.88 x 10^-14 m ≈ 2.9 x 10^-14 m`. Doubling `K` -> `r0` halves to `≈ 1.44 x 10^-14 m`
  (since `r0 proportional to 1/K`). ...1m

### Q27. Shortest wavelength of the Balmer series (2m)

**Q (own words):** Find the shortest wavelength (series limit) of the Balmer series.
Take `R = 1.097 x 10^7 m^-1`.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- `1/lambda = R (1/n1^2 - 1/n2^2)`; Balmer `n1 = 2`, shortest wavelength is `n2 = infinity`:
  `1/lambda = R (1/4 - 0) = R/4`. ...1m
- `lambda = 4/R = 4/(1.097 x 10^7) = 3.65 x 10^-7 m = 365 nm` (near-UV edge of visible). ...1m

### Q28. Longest wavelength (H-alpha) of the Balmer series (2m)

**Q (own words):** Find the longest wavelength of the Balmer series (the H-alpha line).
Take `R = 1.097 x 10^7 m^-1`.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- Longest wavelength = smallest energy gap = `n2 = 3 -> n1 = 2`:
  `1/lambda = R (1/4 - 1/9) = R (5/36)`. ...1m
- `lambda = 36/(5 R) = 36/(5 x 1.097 x 10^7) = 6.56 x 10^-7 m = 656 nm` (red H-alpha). ...1m

### Q29. Wavelength of the first Lyman line (2m)

**Q (own words):** Calculate the wavelength of the photon emitted when a hydrogen electron jumps
from `n = 2` to `n = 1`. Take `R = 1.097 x 10^7 m^-1`.

**Marking-scheme answer:**
- `1/lambda = R (1/1^2 - 1/2^2) = R (1 - 1/4) = (3/4) R`. ...1m
- `lambda = 4/(3 R) = 4/(3 x 1.097 x 10^7) = 1.215 x 10^-7 m = 121.5 nm` (UV, Lyman-alpha). ...1m

### Q30. Frequency of revolution in the ground state (3m)

**Q (own words):** For the ground-state hydrogen electron (`r1 = 0.53 x 10^-10 m`,

- `f = (2.19 x 10^6)/(2 pi x 0.53 x 10^-10) = 6.6 x 10^15 Hz`. ...1m

### Q31. de Broglie wavelength of the orbiting electron (2m)

**Q (own words):** Show that the de Broglie wavelength of the electron in the ground-state Bohr

### Q32. Energy and frequency to excite n = 1 -> n = 4 (2-3m)
<!-- id: ch12-q32 -->

**Q (own words):** A photon excites a hydrogen atom from the ground state to `n = 4`. Find the

## Case-Study

### Q33. Energy-level transitions of hydrogen (4m)
<!-- id: ch12-q33 -->

**Case:** The energy levels of the hydrogen atom are `E_n = -13.6/n^2 eV`, giving
`E1 = -13.6, E2 = -3.4, E3 = -1.51, E4 = -0.85 eV`, and `0 eV` at `n = infinity`. Atoms de-excite
by emitting photons whose energy equals the gap between levels; the resulting lines form the
Lyman (`-> n=1`), Balmer (`-> n=2`) and Paschen (`-> n=3`) series. Read the passage and answer.

**(i) (1m)** What is the ionisation energy of hydrogen from the ground state?
- `= 0 - E1 = 0 - (-13.6) = 13.6 eV`. ...1m

**(ii) (1m)** A hydrogen atom is in the `n = 3` state. What is the maximum number of spectral lines
it can emit on de-excitation?
- `n(n-1)/2 = 3 x 2/2 = 3` lines (3->2, 3->1, 2->1). ...1m

**(iii) (1m)** Which transition among 4->2, 3->2, 4->3 emits the photon of **largest** wavelength?
- Largest wavelength = smallest energy gap. Gaps: 4->2 = 2.55 eV, 3->2 = 1.89 eV, 4->3 = 0.66 eV.
  Smallest is **4->3** -> largest wavelength. ...1m

**(iv) (1m)** A 12.09 eV photon is absorbed by a ground-state atom. To which level is it excited?
- Need `E_n - E1 = 12.09` => `E_n = -1.51 eV` => `n = 3`. (Matches `E3 = -1.51 eV`.) ...1m
