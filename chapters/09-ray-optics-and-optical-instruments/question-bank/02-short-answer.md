### Q1. State Snell's law and define refractive index (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section B**

State the law that governs the bending of light at a surface and define the refractive index of a medium.

**Answer:**
- **Snell's law:** For light passing from medium 1 to medium 2, the ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction is constant: n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r. …(1m)
- **Refractive index:** n = (speed of light in vacuum)/(speed of light in medium) = c/v. It measures how much a medium slows light and bends a ray; for a vacuum n = 1, and n > 1 for all material media. …(1m)

### Q2. Conditions for total internal reflection (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section B**

State the two conditions necessary for total internal reflection to take place.

**Answer:**
- (i) Light must travel from an **optically denser medium to an optically rarer medium** (e.g. glass → air). …(1m)
- (ii) The **angle of incidence in the denser medium must exceed the critical angle** (i > C) for that pair of media. …(1m)

When both hold, no light is refracted; all of it is reflected back into the denser medium.

### Q3. Optical fibre and its working (3m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section C**

Explain how an optical fibre transmits light over long distances, and name two of its uses.

**Answer:**
- **Structure:** A thin core of high refractive index is surrounded by a cladding of lower refractive index. …(1m)
- **Principle — total internal reflection:** Light entering the core strikes the core-cladding boundary at an angle greater than the critical angle, so it undergoes repeated total internal reflection and stays trapped, travelling down the fibre with negligible loss even around bends. …(1m)
- **Uses:** (i) Optical communication / high-speed data and telephone signals; (ii) medical endoscopy to view internal organs. …(1m)

### Q4. Why a diamond sparkles (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section B**

Explain, in terms of total internal reflection, why a cut diamond sparkles brilliantly.

**Answer:**
- Diamond has a very high refractive index (n ≈ 2.42), giving a **very small critical angle** (C ≈ 24.4°). …(1m)
- Light entering the diamond strikes most internal faces at angles greater than this small critical angle, so it is **totally internally reflected many times** before finally emerging, producing the brilliant sparkle. Skilful faceting maximises these internal reflections. …(1m)

### Q5. Two uses of total internal reflection in prisms (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section B**

Give two applications in which a totally reflecting prism is used and state the deviation produced.

**Answer:**
- A 45°-45°-90° glass prism (critical angle of glass ≈ 42° < 45°) totally reflects light internally. …(1m)
- (i) To **bend a ray by 90°** (periscope, binoculars) — one TIR at the hypotenuse; (ii) to **invert/erect an image (bend by 180°)** — two TIRs (used in binoculars/erecting systems). They are preferred over mirrors as they reflect ~100% of light with no silvering to tarnish. …(1m)

### Q6. Lens immersed in a liquid (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section B**

A convex lens of glass (n = 1.5) is placed in a liquid. What happens to its focal length when the liquid's refractive index is (i) 1.0, (ii) 1.5, (iii) 1.6?

**Answer:**
Using 1/f = (n_glass/n_liquid − 1)(1/R₁ − 1/R₂):
- (i) n_liquid = 1.0: behaves as a normal converging lens (its usual positive f). …(½m)
- (ii) n_liquid = 1.5 = n_glass: bracket = 0, so f → ∞; the lens **disappears optically** and does nothing to the light. …(1m)
- (iii) n_liquid = 1.6 > n_glass: (n_glass/n_liquid − 1) is negative, so f becomes **negative — the convex lens now behaves as a diverging lens**. …(½m)

### Q7. Power of a lens — definition and unit (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section B**

Define the power of a lens, give its SI unit, and state how powers combine when two thin lenses are in contact.

**Answer:**
- **Power** P = 1/f (f in metres); it measures the lens's ability to converge or diverge light. SI unit: **dioptre (D)**, where 1 D = 1 m⁻¹. …(1m)
- For two thin lenses in contact, P = P₁ + P₂ (powers add algebraically; a diverging lens contributes negative power). …(1m)

### Q8. Refraction through a glass slab — lateral shift (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section B**

A ray passes obliquely through a parallel-sided glass slab. Explain why the emergent ray is parallel to the incident ray, and name the effect produced.

**Answer:**
- At the two parallel faces the ray bends toward the normal entering and away from the normal leaving by equal amounts, so the **emergent ray is parallel to the incident ray** (only the direction is restored). …(1m)
- It is, however, displaced sideways — this is the **lateral shift (lateral displacement)**, which increases with slab thickness and angle of incidence. …(1m)

### Q9. Magnifying power vs resolving power (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section C**

Distinguish between the magnifying power and the resolving power of a telescope.

**Answer:**
- **Magnifying power** = ratio of the angle subtended at the eye by the image to the angle subtended by the object; for a telescope M = f_o/f_e. It makes the object look larger. …(1m)
- **Resolving power** = ability to show two close objects as separate; it depends on the objective aperture D (resolving power ∝ D/λ), not on focal lengths. A larger objective resolves finer detail. …(1m)

---

### Q10. Why objective of a telescope has large aperture (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section B**

State two reasons why the objective of an astronomical telescope is made of large aperture (diameter).

**Answer:**
- (i) A large aperture **gathers more light**, so faint distant stars appear brighter. …(1m)
- (ii) A large aperture gives **higher resolving power** (∝ D/λ), letting the telescope separate two close stars. …(1m)

### Q11. Compound microscope vs simple microscope (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section B**

Why does a compound microscope give much higher magnification than a simple microscope (magnifying glass)?

**Answer:**
- A simple microscope magnifies once: M = 1 + D/f. …(½m)
- A compound microscope magnifies in **two stages** — the objective forms a real, magnified image, which the eyepiece then magnifies again — so the magnifications multiply: M = m_o × m_e = (L/f_o)(D/f_e). Two short-focus lenses in series give far higher overall magnification. …(1½m)

### Q12. Mirror vs lens sign of focal length (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section B**

State the sign of the focal length for: (i) concave mirror, (ii) convex mirror, (iii) convex lens, (iv) concave lens, under the New Cartesian sign convention.

**Answer:**
- (i) Concave mirror: f **negative**. (ii) Convex mirror: f **positive**. …(1m)
- (iii) Convex (converging) lens: f **positive**. (iv) Concave (diverging) lens: f **negative**. …(1m)

---

### Q13. Refraction at a spherical surface — what the formula relates (2m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section C**

Write the equation for refraction at a single spherical surface and state what each symbol means.

**Answer:**
- **Equation:** n₂/v − n₁/u = (n₂ − n₁)/R. …(1m)
- Here n₁ = refractive index of the medium of the incident ray, n₂ = that of the medium of the refracted ray, u = object distance, v = image distance, R = radius of curvature of the surface, all measured from the pole with the New Cartesian sign convention. …(1m)

### Q14. Behaviour of a concave mirror at different object positions (3m)

**Type:** Short Answer · **Section C**

State the nature, position and size of the image formed by a concave mirror when the object is (i) at infinity, (ii) between C and F, (iii) between F and the pole.

**Answer:**
- (i) Object at infinity → image **at F**, real, inverted, highly diminished (point image). …(1m)
- (ii) Object between C and F → image **beyond C**, real, inverted, magnified. …(1m)
- (iii) Object between F and pole → image **behind the mirror**, virtual, erect, magnified (this is the shaving/make-up mirror use). …(1m)
