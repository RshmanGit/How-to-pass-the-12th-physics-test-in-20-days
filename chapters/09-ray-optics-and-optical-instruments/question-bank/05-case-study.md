### Q1. Total internal reflection and the optical fibre (4m)
<!-- id: ch09-cs-q1 -->

**Type:** Case-Based · **Section D**

**Passage:** When light travels from a denser medium to a rarer medium and the angle of incidence exceeds a certain value, the ray is reflected entirely back into the denser medium instead of refracting out — this is total internal reflection (TIR). The least angle of incidence for which this happens is the critical angle C, given by sin C = 1/n where n is the refractive index of the denser medium relative to the rarer one. TIR is the principle behind optical fibres, which carry light (and hence data) over long distances by repeated internal reflection inside a high-index core surrounded by a low-index cladding. The same effect makes diamonds sparkle and lets totally-reflecting prisms replace mirrors in binoculars.

**(a)** State the two conditions required for total internal reflection. **(1m)**

**Answer:** (i) Light must go from a denser to a rarer medium; (ii) the angle of incidence must exceed the critical angle (i > C). …(1m)

**(b)** The refractive index of the core glass is 1.5. Calculate its critical angle. **(1m)**

**Answer:** sin C = 1/n = 1/1.5 = 0.667 ⇒ **C = 41.8° ≈ 42°**. …(1m)

**(c)** Why is the cladding of an optical fibre made of a material of lower refractive index than the core? **(1m)**

**Answer:** A lower-index cladding ensures the light meeting the core-cladding boundary does so from the denser side at angles above the critical angle, so TIR occurs and light stays trapped in the core with minimal loss. …(1m)

**(d)** Why is a 45°-45°-90° glass prism preferred over a plane mirror to turn a beam through 90° in binoculars? **(1m)**

**Answer:** The critical angle of glass (≈ 42°) is less than 45°, so the prism gives total internal reflection — reflecting essentially 100% of the light, with no silvering to tarnish or absorb, unlike a mirror. …(1m)

### Q2. Lenses, power and the human eye / instruments (5m)

**Type:** Case-Based · **Section D**

**Passage:** A lens refracts light at its two spherical surfaces. The lens-maker's formula, 1/f = (n − 1)(1/R₁ − 1/R₂), shows that focal length depends on the glass index and the surface curvatures. The power of a lens, P = 1/f (in dioptres, with f in metres), measures its converging or diverging strength; for lenses in contact, powers simply add: P = P₁ + P₂. Opticians use this additive rule to design corrective spectacles and to combine the objective and eyepiece of microscopes and telescopes — though in compound instruments the *magnifications* multiply rather than add.

**(a)** Define one dioptre. **(1m)**

**Answer:** One dioptre is the power of a lens whose focal length is 1 metre (1 D = 1 m⁻¹). …(1m)

**(b)** A convex lens of focal length 25 cm and a concave lens of focal length 50 cm are placed in contact. Find the power and focal length of the combination. **(2m)**

**Answer:**
- P₁ = 1/0.25 = +4 D; P₂ = 1/(−0.50) = −2 D. …(1m)
- P = P₁ + P₂ = 4 − 2 = **+2 D**, so f = 1/2 = **0.5 m = 50 cm** (converging). …(1m)

**(c)** Glass of index 1.5 is moulded into a double-convex lens with both radii 20 cm. Find its focal length. **(1m)**

**Answer:** 1/f = (n − 1)(1/R₁ − 1/R₂) = (0.5)(1/20 − (−1/20)) = 0.5 × (2/20) = 0.05 cm⁻¹ ⇒ **f = 20 cm**. …(1m)

**(d)** In a compound microscope the objective magnifies 5× and the eyepiece 10×. What is the total magnifying power, and why is it a product not a sum? **(1m)**

**Answer:** M = m_o × m_e = 5 × 10 = **50×**. The real image made by the objective becomes the object for the eyepiece, so the second stage magnifies the already-magnified image — the two magnifications multiply. …(1m)

### Q3. Prism, dispersion and minimum deviation (4m)

**Type:** Case-Based · **Section D**

**Passage:** When a ray passes through a triangular prism it deviates toward the base by an angle δ that first decreases, reaches a minimum value Dm, then increases as the angle of incidence is varied. At minimum deviation the ray passes symmetrically (i = e, r₁ = r₂) and the refractive index is n = sin[(A + Dm)/2] / sin(A/2). Because n is slightly larger for violet than for red light, a prism also spreads white light into its colours (dispersion).

**(a)** What is the relation between the two internal refraction angles and the prism angle A? **(1m)**

**Answer:** r₁ + r₂ = A. …(1m)

**(b)** Write the condition satisfied by the angles at minimum deviation. **(1m)**

**Answer:** At minimum deviation the path is symmetric: i = e and r₁ = r₂ (= A/2). …(1m)

**(c)** An equilateral prism (A = 60°) gives minimum deviation 40°. Find its refractive index. **(2m)**

**Answer:**
- n = sin[(A + Dm)/2] / sin(A/2) = sin[(60 + 40)/2] / sin 30° = sin 50° / sin 30°. …(1m)
- = 0.766 / 0.5 = **1.53**. …(1m)
