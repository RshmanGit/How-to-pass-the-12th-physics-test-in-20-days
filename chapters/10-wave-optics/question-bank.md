# Chapter 10 — Wave Optics · Question Bank

> CBSE Class 12 Physics · ~4 marks on the board paper. Answers are written in **marking-scheme
> step style** — each line is a mark-earning step, not an essay. Diagrams are given as

**Core formulas (memorise):**
- Fringe width (YDSE): β = λD/d
- Bright fringe (constructive): path diff = nλ ; position yₙ = nλD/d
- Dark fringe (destructive): path diff = (n − ½)λ ; position yₙ = (2n−1)λD/2d
- Resultant intensity (two coherent sources): I = 4I₀cos²(φ/2)
- Single slit, minima: a sinθ = nλ ; half-angular width of central max θ = λ/a
- Width of central maximum (linear): 2λD/a
- Malus's law: I = I₀cos²θ
- Brewster's law: μ = tan iₚ ; at iₚ, reflected ⟂ refracted (iₚ + r = 90°)
- Speed of light in a medium: v = c/μ ; in a medium λ_medium = λ_vacuum/μ (ν unchanged)

## MCQ & Assertion-Reason

### Q1. Nature of secondary wavelets (1m)

**Question (own words):** In Huygens' construction, what shape does each point on a wavefront act as?

- (A) A point absorber of light
- (B) A fresh source of secondary spherical wavelets
- (C) A reflector only
- (D) A source of straight-line rays

**Answer (1m):** **(B)** — every point on a wavefront is a source of secondary wavelets that
spread out as spheres with the speed of light in the medium; their common forward tangent
(envelope) is the new wavefront. The backward wavelets are discarded.

---

### Q2. Effect of dipping YDSE apparatus in water (1m)

**Question (own words):** A Young's double-slit set-up giving fringe width β in air is fully
immersed in water (μ = 4/3). The new fringe width is:

- (A) β (B) 4β/3 (C) 3β/4 (D) 0

**Answer (1m):** **(C) 3β/4.** In water the wavelength becomes λ/μ, so
β' = (λ/μ)D/d = β/μ = β/(4/3) = **3β/4**. Fringes get closer together.

### Q3. Condition for sustained interference (1m)

**Question (own words):** Sustained (stable) interference fringes are observed only when the two
sources are:

- (A) of different frequencies
- (B) coherent (same frequency, constant phase difference)
- (C) of large and unequal intensities
- (D) two independent bulbs

**Answer (1m):** **(B)** — coherent sources keep a **constant phase difference** so the fringe
pattern stays fixed in time. Two independent bulbs emit randomly-phased wavetrains, so the
pattern washes out.

### Q4. Width of central diffraction maximum vs slit width (1m)

**Question (own words):** In single-slit diffraction, if the slit width a is **doubled**, the
width of the central maximum becomes:

- (A) doubled (B) halved (C) unchanged (D) four times

**Answer (1m):** **(B) halved.** Central-max width = 2λD/a, which is inversely proportional to a.
(Its peak intensity also rises ≈4×, since more light passes through.)

### Q5. Malus's law — crossed polaroids (1m)

**Question (own words):** Unpolarised light of intensity I₀ passes through two polaroids whose
axes are crossed (90° apart). The transmitted intensity is:

- (A) I₀/2 (B) I₀/4 (C) zero (D) I₀

**Answer (1m):** **(C) zero.** First polaroid transmits I₀/2 (polarised along its axis); the
second is at 90°, so I = (I₀/2)cos²90° = **0**.

### Q6. Brewster's angle direction (1m)

**Question (own words):** Light is incident at the polarising (Brewster) angle on a glass slab.
The angle between the reflected and refracted rays is:

- (A) 0° (B) 45° (C) 90° (D) 180°

**Answer (1m):** **(C) 90°.** At Brewster's angle the **reflected ray ⟂ refracted ray**, so
iₚ + r = 90°; the reflected light is completely plane-polarised.

### Q7. Assertion–Reason: coherent sources (1m)

**Assertion (A):** Two independent sodium lamps cannot produce an interference pattern.
**Reason (R):** The two lamps emit light of the same wavelength.

Options: (a) Both A and R true, R is the correct explanation of A. (b) Both true, R **not** the
correct explanation. (c) A true, R false. (d) A false, R true.

**Answer (1m):** **(b).** A is true and R is true (both are sodium, ~589 nm), **but** R is not the
reason — interference fails because the lamps have **no constant phase relation** (incoherent),
not because of wavelength. Same wavelength alone is not enough.

### Q8. Assertion–Reason: diffraction and wavelength (1m)

**Assertion (A):** Sound waves diffract around an open door easily, but light does not.
**Reason (R):** Diffraction is appreciable only when the obstacle/aperture size is comparable to
the wavelength.

Options as in Q7.

**Answer (1m):** **(a).** Both true and R explains A. Sound's wavelength (~1 m) is comparable to a
door (~1 m), so it bends; light's wavelength (~500 nm) is far smaller than the door, so its
diffraction is negligible. R is the correct explanation.

## Short Answer (2-3m)

### Q9. Wavefront and Huygens' principle (2m)

**Question (own words):** Define a wavefront. State Huygens' principle.

**Answer (2m):**
- **Wavefront:** the locus of all points of a medium that are vibrating **in the same phase** at a
  given instant. The direction of propagation (ray) is always **perpendicular** to the wavefront. …½m
- A point source gives **spherical** wavefronts; a source at infinity (or a distant point) gives
  **plane** wavefronts; a line source gives **cylindrical** wavefronts. …½m
- **Huygens' principle:** (i) every point on a wavefront is a source of secondary wavelets
  spreading out with the wave speed of the medium; (ii) the new wavefront at a later instant is the
  **forward envelope (common tangent)** of all these secondary wavelets. …1m

**Diagram — wavefront types** (draw):
- [ ] Spherical wavefront: concentric arcs around a point source, ray arrows radially outward
- [ ] Plane wavefront: parallel straight lines, ray arrows ⟂ to them
- Earns: ½m for correct shapes + perpendicular rays labelled

### Q10. Coherent sources — definition and conditions (2m)

**Question (own words):** What are coherent sources? State the conditions two sources must satisfy
to produce a stable interference pattern.

**Answer (2m):**
- **Coherent sources:** two sources that emit light waves of the **same frequency (wavelength)**
  with a **constant (time-independent) phase difference**. …1m
- Conditions for a sustained pattern: (i) same wavelength/frequency; (ii) constant phase
  difference; (iii) (for good contrast) equal or comparable amplitudes and same state of
  polarisation. …1m
- In practice both beams are derived from a **single source** (e.g. the two slits in YDSE) so the
  phase relation is automatically fixed.

### Q11. Bright and dark fringe conditions in YDSE (2m)

**Question (own words):** Write the conditions (path difference and fringe position) for bright and
dark fringes in Young's double-slit experiment.

**Answer (2m):**
- Path difference at point P on screen: Δ = d sinθ ≈ d·y/D. …½m
- **Bright fringe (constructive):** Δ = nλ ⇒ yₙ = nλD/d, n = 0, 1, 2, … …½m
- **Dark fringe (destructive):** Δ = (n − ½)λ = (2n−1)λ/2 ⇒ yₙ = (2n−1)λD/2d, n = 1, 2, … …½m
- **Fringe width** (spacing between consecutive bright OR dark fringes): β = λD/d — same for both.
  …½m

### Q12. Two differences: interference vs diffraction (2m)

**Question (own words):** Distinguish between interference and diffraction of light (any two
points).

**Answer (2m):** (any two, ~1m each)

| Interference | Diffraction |
|---|---|
| Superposition of waves from **two (or more) separate coherent sources** | Superposition of secondary wavelets from **different parts of the same wavefront** |
| All bright fringes are of **equal width and (nearly) equal intensity** | Central maximum is **widest and brightest**; secondary maxima fall off rapidly |
| Fringes are **equally spaced** | Successive maxima get closer and dimmer |
| Minima are usually perfectly dark | Minima are not perfectly dark |

### Q13. Polarised vs unpolarised light; how to tell them apart (2m)

**Question (own words):** Distinguish polarised from unpolarised light. How can a polaroid be used
to detect plane-polarised light?

**Answer (2m):**
- **Unpolarised light:** electric-field vibrations occur in **all directions** ⟂ to the direction
  of propagation, with no preferred plane. …½m
- **Plane- (linearly-) polarised light:** vibrations confined to **a single plane** ⟂ to
  propagation. …½m
- **Detection:** view the light through a rotating polaroid (analyser). If the transmitted
  intensity **varies between a maximum and zero** as you rotate, the light is plane-polarised
  (Malus's law); if it stays constant, it is unpolarised; partial variation ⇒ partially polarised.
  …1m

---

### Q14. Brewster's law and the polarising angle (2m)

**Question (own words):** State Brewster's law. Show that at the polarising angle the reflected and
refracted rays are perpendicular.

**Answer (2m):**
- **Brewster's law:** when unpolarised light is incident on a transparent medium at the polarising
  angle iₚ, the reflected light is completely plane-polarised and **μ = tan iₚ**. …1m
- **Proof of perpendicularity:** Snell's law gives μ = sin iₚ/sin r. Brewster: μ = tan iₚ =
  sin iₚ/cos iₚ. Equate: sin iₚ/sin r = sin iₚ/cos iₚ ⇒ sin r = cos iₚ = sin(90° − iₚ) ⇒
  r = 90° − iₚ ⇒ **iₚ + r = 90°**, i.e. reflected ray ⟂ refracted ray. …1m

---

### Q15. Why does a single slit give a pattern, not a sharp shadow? (2m)

**Question (own words):** Using Huygens' idea, explain qualitatively why a narrow single slit
produces a broad central bright band with dark fringes on either side.

**Answer (2m):**
- Each point of the wavefront in the slit acts as a secondary source; wavelets from all points
  **superpose** on the screen. …½m
- At the centre (θ = 0) all wavelets travel equal paths and arrive **in phase** ⇒ central
  **maximum**. …½m
- At angle θ with a sinθ = λ, the slit can be split into two halves whose corresponding points
  differ by λ/2, cancelling in pairs ⇒ **first minimum**. Generally a sinθ = nλ gives the n-th
  minimum. …½m
- Between minima lie weak secondary maxima; intensity falls off rapidly with order ⇒ pattern, not a
  sharp geometric shadow. …½m

## Derivations & Long Answer

### Q16. Huygens' principle: laws of reflection (3m)

**Question (own words):** Using Huygens' wave theory, prove the laws of reflection (angle of
incidence = angle of reflection, and incident ray, reflected ray, normal are coplanar).

**Answer (3m):**
- **Set-up:** a plane wavefront AB strikes a plane mirror MM′; the incident ray makes angle i with
  the normal. Treat A and B as secondary sources. …½m
- While the wavelet from B travels BC to reach the mirror, the wavelet from A travels a sphere of
  the **same radius** AD (same speed, same time t): so **BC = AD = c·t**. …1m
- In right triangles ABC and ADC: AC common, BC = AD, and both have a right angle ⇒ the triangles
  are congruent ⇒ ∠BAC = ∠DCA, i.e. **angle of incidence i = angle of reflection r**. …1m
- The incident wavefront, reflected wavefront and normal all lie in the plane of the page ⇒
  incident ray, reflected ray and normal are **coplanar**. …½m
- **Result:** ∠i = ∠r and all three coplanar — the laws of reflection. ✅

**Diagram — reflection by Huygens** (draw):
- [ ] Mirror MM′ as a horizontal line
- [ ] Incident plane wavefront AB approaching at angle i
- [ ] Reflected wavefront CD leaving at angle r
- [ ] Secondary wavelet (arc) of radius AD = BC drawn from A
- [ ] Normals and angles i, r marked equal
- Earns: ~1m for a correct labelled construction

### Q17. Huygens' principle: laws of refraction (Snell's law) (3m)

**Question (own words):** Using Huygens' principle, derive Snell's law of refraction
(μ = sin i/sin r) and hence relate the refractive index to the speeds of light in the two media.

**Answer (3m):**
- **Set-up:** plane wavefront AB in medium 1 (speed v₁) meets boundary XY at angle i; refracts into
  medium 2 (speed v₂) at angle r. …½m
- Time for wavelet at B to reach the surface at C: t = BC/v₁. In the same time the wavelet from A
  advances into medium 2 a distance AE = v₂·t. …½m
- From the geometry: BC = AC·sin i and AE = AC·sin r. …1m
- So sin i/sin r = BC/AE = (v₁t)/(v₂t) = **v₁/v₂** = constant = ₁μ₂. This is **Snell's law:
  sin i/sin r = v₁/v₂ = μ₂/μ₁**. …½m
- Since v = c/μ, the wave bends toward the normal entering a denser medium (v decreases); frequency
  is unchanged, so **wavelength decreases** in the denser medium (λ₂ = λ₁·v₂/v₁). …½m
- **Result:** sin i/sin r = v₁/v₂ = constant. ✅

**Diagram — refraction by Huygens** (draw):
- [ ] Boundary line XY (medium 1 above, medium 2 below)
- [ ] Incident wavefront AB at angle i; refracted wavefront EC at angle r
- [ ] Secondary wavelet of radius AE = v₂t drawn into medium 2
- [ ] Angles i and r, speeds v₁, v₂ labelled
- Earns: ~1m for the labelled geometry

### Q18. Young's double-slit experiment: fringe width derivation (5m)

**Question (own words):** Describe Young's double-slit experiment. Derive the expression for fringe
width β = λD/d, and state the conditions for bright and dark fringes.

**Answer (5m):**
- **Principle:** Light from a single monochromatic source passes through two narrow, closely-spaced
  slits S₁ and S₂ (separation d) which act as **coherent sources**; the overlapping beams interfere
  on a screen a distance D away (D ≫ d). …1m
- **Path difference:** for a point P at distance y from the centre O,
  S₂P − S₁P ≈ d sinθ ≈ d·(y/D) (small-angle). …1m
- **Bright fringe (constructive):** path difference = nλ
  ⇒ d·yₙ/D = nλ ⇒ **yₙ = nλD/d** (n = 0, 1, 2, …). …½m
- **Dark fringe (destructive):** path difference = (2n−1)λ/2
  ⇒ **yₙ = (2n−1)λD/2d** (n = 1, 2, …). …½m
- **Fringe width** = spacing between two consecutive bright (or dark) fringes:
  β = yₙ₊₁ − yₙ = (n+1)λD/d − nλD/d. …½m
- **Result:** **β = λD/d** — fringes are equally spaced; β ∝ λ, ∝ D, ∝ 1/d. …½m
- Intensity distribution: I = 4I₀cos²(φ/2) where φ = (2π/λ)·(path difference); maxima 4I₀, minima 0.
  …½m

**Diagram — YDSE set-up** (draw):
- [ ] Source → single slit S → two slits S₁, S₂ separated by d
- [ ] Screen at distance D; central point O on the axis, point P at height y
- [ ] Path lines S₁P and S₂P, with the small path-difference triangle (d sinθ) marked
- [ ] Fringe pattern at the side: equally-spaced bright/dark bands
- Earns: ~1m for labelled geometry (d, D, y) + ~½m for the fringe pattern

### Q19. Single-slit diffraction and width of central maximum (5m)

**Question (own words):** Explain diffraction at a single slit. Obtain the condition for minima and
derive the width of the central maximum. Sketch the intensity distribution.

**Answer (5m):**
- **Set-up:** parallel monochromatic light (plane wavefront) of wavelength λ falls on a slit of
  width a; the diffracted light is focused on a screen at distance D. Each point in the slit is a
  secondary source (Huygens). …1m
- **Central maximum:** at θ = 0 all secondary wavelets travel equal paths and arrive in phase ⇒
  strong central bright band. …½m
- **Minima:** divide the slit into pairs of halves. The path difference between the edges is a sinθ.
  When **a sinθ = nλ** (n = 1, 2, …) wavelets cancel in pairs ⇒ **dark fringes (minima)**. …1m
- **Secondary maxima:** roughly where a sinθ = (n + ½)λ; their intensity falls off rapidly. …½m
- **First minimum** on either side: a sinθ = λ ⇒ sinθ ≈ θ = λ/a. …½m
- **Width of central maximum:** angular full width = 2θ = **2λ/a**; linear width on the screen =
  2D·tanθ ≈ **2λD/a**. It is **twice** as wide as any secondary maximum. …1m
- **Result:** central max is the brightest and widest; width ∝ λ, ∝ 1/a — narrower slit ⇒ broader
  pattern. ✅ …½m

**Diagram — single-slit diffraction + intensity** (draw):
- [ ] Plane wavefront → slit of width a → screen at distance D
- [ ] Central bright band with first minima at angle θ = λ/a on each side
- [ ] Intensity plot: tall central peak, then small symmetric secondary maxima decreasing outward
- [ ] Minima marked at a sinθ = λ, 2λ, 3λ …
- Earns: ~1m for the labelled geometry + ~1m for a correct intensity graph

### Q20. Polarisation by reflection; Brewster's angle (long) (5m)

**Question (own words):** What is plane-polarised light? Explain polarisation by reflection, state
Brewster's law, and show the reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular at the polarising
angle. State one use of polaroids.

**Answer (5m):**
- **Plane-polarised light:** light whose E-field vibrations are confined to **one plane** ⟂ to the
  direction of propagation (unpolarised light vibrates in all such directions). Light is a
  **transverse** wave, which is why it can be polarised (sound cannot). …1m
- **Polarisation by reflection:** when unpolarised light reflects off a transparent surface
  (glass, water), the reflected beam is **partially polarised**; at one special angle of incidence
  it becomes **completely plane-polarised** (vibrations ⟂ plane of incidence). …1m
- **Brewster's law:** this special angle is the polarising angle iₚ, and **μ = tan iₚ**. …1m
- **Perpendicularity:** Snell μ = sin iₚ/sin r; Brewster μ = tan iₚ = sin iₚ/cos iₚ. Equate ⇒
  sin r = cos iₚ ⇒ r = 90° − iₚ ⇒ **iₚ + r = 90°** ⇒ reflected ray ⟂ refracted ray. …1m
- **Use of polaroids:** in sunglasses/camera filters to cut glare; in LCD displays; to control
  light intensity (crossed polaroids); in stress analysis (photoelasticity). …½m + ½m diagram

- [ ] Reflected ray (dots ⊙ = vibrations ⟂ to page = plane-polarised) and refracted ray (arrows ↕ + dots)
- [ ] Normal drawn; angle between reflected and refracted rays marked **90°**
- [ ] μ = tan iₚ written beside

## Numericals

### Q21. Fringe width of YDSE (2m)

**Question (own words):** In a YDSE, the slits are 1 mm apart and the screen is 1 m away. Red light
of wavelength 6500 Å is used. Find the fringe width (spacing between adjacent bright fringes).

**Answer (2m):**
- Formula: β = λD/d. …½m
- Substitute: λ = 6500 Å = 6.5×10⁻⁷ m, D = 1 m, d = 1 mm = 1×10⁻³ m. …½m
- β = (6.5×10⁻⁷ × 1)/(1×10⁻³). …½m
- **β = 6.5×10⁻⁴ m = 0.65 mm.** …½m

### Q22. Wavelength from measured fringe width (2m)

**Question (own words):** In a double-slit experiment d = 0.24 mm and D = 1.2 m. The measured
fringe width is 0.03 cm. Find the wavelength of the light used.

**Answer (2m):**
- Rearranged formula: λ = βd/D. …½m
- Substitute: β = 0.03 cm = 3×10⁻⁴ m, d = 0.24 mm = 2.4×10⁻⁴ m, D = 1.2 m. …½m
- λ = (3×10⁻⁴ × 2.4×10⁻⁴)/1.2 = (7.2×10⁻⁸)/1.2. …½m
- **λ = 6×10⁻⁷ m = 600 nm** (orange-red). …½m

### Q23. YDSE — two wavelengths overlapping (NCERT) (3m)

**Question (own words):** A YDSE uses light containing two wavelengths, 650 nm and 520 nm.
(a) Find the distance of the 3rd bright fringe of 650 nm from the centre.
(b) Find the least distance from the centre where bright fringes of the two wavelengths coincide.
(Take slit separation d and screen distance D as symbols.)

**Answer (3m):**
- **(a)** Bright fringe position yₙ = nλD/d. For n = 3, λ = 650 nm:
  y₃ = 3 × 650 × (D/d) = **1950 (D/d) nm** = 1.95×10⁻⁶ (D/d) m. …1m
- **(b)** Coincidence needs n₁λ₁ = n₂λ₂ ⇒ n₁(650) = n₂(520) ⇒ n₁/n₂ = 520/650 = 4/5.
  Smallest integers: n₁ = 4, n₂ = 5. …1m
- Least coincidence distance = 4λ₁(D/d) = 4 × 650 × (D/d) = **2600 (D/d) nm = 2.6×10⁻⁶ (D/d) m**
  (same as 5 × 520). …1m

### Q24. YDSE — find wavelength from nth bright fringe (NCERT) (2m)

**Question (own words):** In a YDSE the slits are 0.28 mm apart and the screen is 1.4 m away. The
4th bright fringe is 1.2 cm from the central fringe. Find the wavelength.

**Answer (2m):**
- Position of nth bright fringe: yₙ = nλD/d ⇒ λ = yₙd/(nD). …½m
- Substitute: yₙ = 1.2 cm = 1.2×10⁻² m, d = 0.28 mm = 2.8×10⁻⁴ m, n = 4, D = 1.4 m. …½m
- λ = (1.2×10⁻² × 2.8×10⁻⁴)/(4 × 1.4) = (3.36×10⁻⁶)/5.6. …½m
- **λ = 6×10⁻⁷ m = 600 nm.** …½m

### Q25. Single-slit diffraction — width of central maximum (3m)

**Question (own words):** Monochromatic light of wavelength 600 nm falls on a single slit of width

dark fringe from the centre and (b) the width of the central maximum.

- Substitute: λ = 6×10⁻⁷ m, a = 1×10⁻³ m, D = 2 m. …½m

### Q26. Brewster's angle for glass (2m)

**Question (own words):** Find the Brewster (polarising) angle for light passing from air into
glass of refractive index 1.5. Also find the angle of refraction at that incidence.

**Answer (2m):**
- Brewster's law: μ = tan iₚ ⇒ iₚ = tan⁻¹(μ). …½m
- iₚ = tan⁻¹(1.5) = **56.3°.** …1m
- Since iₚ + r = 90°, r = 90° − 56.3° = **33.7°.** …½m

### Q27. Malus's law — intensity through two polaroids (2m)

**Question (own words):** Unpolarised light of intensity I₀ is incident on a pair of polaroids
whose transmission axes make 30° with each other. Find the intensity of the emerging light.

**Answer (2m):**
- After the **first** polaroid (polariser): I₁ = I₀/2 (half of unpolarised light passes). …½m
- After the **second** polaroid (analyser), Malus's law: I₂ = I₁cos²θ = (I₀/2)cos²30°. …1m
- cos30° = √3/2 ⇒ cos²30° = 3/4 ⇒ I₂ = (I₀/2)(3/4) = **3I₀/8 = 0.375 I₀.** …½m

### Q28. Speed and wavelength of light in a medium (2m)

**Question (own words):** Light of wavelength 589 nm (in vacuum) enters water of refractive index
1.33. Find its speed and wavelength in water. (c = 3×10⁸ m/s.)

**Answer (2m):**
- Speed: v = c/μ = (3×10⁸)/1.33 = **2.26×10⁸ m/s.** …1m
- Frequency is unchanged, so wavelength in water: λ_w = λ/μ = 589/1.33 = **443 nm.**
  …1m
- (Colour is set by frequency, so it looks the same colour even though λ shortens.)

### Q29. Intensity ratio from amplitude/intensity ratio (3m)

**Question (own words):** Two coherent sources have intensities in the ratio I₁ : I₂ = 81 : 1.
Find the ratio of maximum to minimum intensity in their interference pattern.

**Answer (3m):**
- Amplitude ratio: a₁/a₂ = √(I₁/I₂) = √81 = 9, so a₁ = 9k, a₂ = k. …1m
- I_max ∝ (a₁ + a₂)² = (9 + 1)² = 100 ; I_min ∝ (a₁ − a₂)² = (9 − 1)² = 64. …1m
- **I_max : I_min = 100 : 64 = 25 : 16.** …1m

## Case-Study

### Q30. Polaroid sunglasses and glare (4m)

**Read:** Light reflected from a horizontal surface such as a road, water, or car bonnet is
partially (and at Brewster's angle fully) **plane-polarised**, with vibrations mainly **horizontal**.
This bright reflected light is what we call glare. Polaroid sunglasses contain a polaroid whose
transmission axis is oriented **vertically**, so it blocks the horizontally-vibrating reflected
light while still passing useful (vertically-polarised) light from the scene. Photographers use the
same idea with a rotatable polarising filter.

**Q30.1 (1m):** Why is light reflected from a road surface partially polarised?
**Answer:** Reflection at a dielectric surface partially polarises light; at Brewster's angle
(μ = tan iₚ) it becomes completely plane-polarised with vibrations perpendicular to the plane of
incidence (here, horizontal).

**Q30.2 (1m):** In which direction is the transmission axis of polaroid sunglasses set, and why?
**Answer:** **Vertical** — the glare is horizontally polarised, so a vertical axis blocks it
(Malus's law, θ = 90° ⇒ I = 0) while passing the rest of the scene.

**Q30.3 (1m):** Unpolarised light of intensity I₀ falls on the sunglass polaroid. What intensity of
the **useful** (non-glare, unpolarised) light passes through?
**Answer:** A single polaroid transmits half of unpolarised light ⇒ **I = I₀/2.**

**Q30.4 (1m):** A photographer rotates a polarising filter and the glare from a pond varies from
bright to nearly zero. What does this prove about the reflected light?
**Answer:** That the reflected light is (largely) **plane-polarised** — Malus's law gives
I = I₀cos²θ, so a polarised beam goes to zero when the analyser axis is ⟂ to it; an unpolarised
beam would not change with rotation.
