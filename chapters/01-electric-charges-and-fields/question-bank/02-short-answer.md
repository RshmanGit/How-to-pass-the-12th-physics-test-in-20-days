# Chapter 1 — Electric Charges and Fields: Short Answer (Sections B/C, 2-3m)

> Conceptual 2-3 mark questions. Answers are written as the mark-earning points a CBSE examiner looks for — statement, reason, formula where relevant.

---

### Q1. Define electric dipole moment and state its SI unit (2m)

**Definition (1m):** The electric dipole moment p of a dipole is the product of the magnitude of either charge (q) and the distance (2a) between the two charges.
**Formula + direction (½m):** p = q × 2a; it is a vector directed from the negative charge to the positive charge.
**SI unit (½m):** coulomb-metre (C m).

---

### Q2. Why can two electric field lines never intersect? (2m)

- If two field lines crossed, then at the point of intersection two tangents could be drawn. (1m)
- The tangent to a field line gives the direction of E at that point, so the field would have two directions at one point. (½m)
- A single point can have only one resultant field direction → field lines cannot intersect. (½m)

---

### Q3. State Coulomb's law and write its vector form (2m)

**Statement (1m):** The electrostatic force between two stationary point charges is directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them; it acts along the line joining them.
**Scalar form (½m):** F = (1/4πε₀)·(q₁q₂/r²).
**Vector form (½m):** **F**₁₂ = (1/4πε₀)·(q₁q₂/r²)·r̂₁₂, where r̂₁₂ is the unit vector from charge 2 to charge 1.

---

### Q4. State the principle of superposition of electric forces (2m)

- The total force on a charge due to several other charges is the **vector sum** of the individual forces exerted by each charge taken one at a time. (1m)
- Each pairwise force is unaffected by the presence of the other charges (forces are independent / additive). (½m)
- **F**_net = **F**₁ + **F**₂ + **F**₃ + … (½m)

---

### Q5. Define electric field intensity and give its SI unit (2m)

**Definition (1m):** Electric field intensity at a point is the force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed at that point: **E** = **F**/q₀ (in the limit q₀ → 0, so the test charge does not disturb the source).
**SI unit (½m):** N C⁻¹ (equivalently V m⁻¹).
**Nature (½m):** It is a vector quantity directed along the force on a positive charge.

---

### Q6. Define electric flux. Is it a scalar or vector? (2m)

**Definition (1m):** Electric flux through a surface is the total number of electric field lines passing normally through that surface: φ = ∮ **E**·d**S** = E·S·cosθ.
**Nature (½m):** Scalar quantity.
**SI unit (½m):** N m² C⁻¹ (= V m).

---

### Q7. State Gauss's law in electrostatics (2m)
<!-- id: ch01-sa-q7 -->

**Statement (1½m):** The net electric flux through any closed surface equals 1/ε₀ times the total charge enclosed by that surface.
**Formula (½m):** ∮ **E**·d**S** = q_enclosed/ε₀.
Note: only the enclosed charge contributes to the net flux; external charges contribute zero net flux.

---

### Q8. Why is the electric field zero inside a charged conductor? (2m)

- A conductor has free electrons that move under any internal field. (½m)
- In electrostatic equilibrium charges redistribute on the surface until they cancel the applied field inside. (1m)
- Hence the net field at every interior point is zero; consequently all excess charge resides on the outer surface. (½m)

---

### Q9. Show that the field just outside a charged conductor is σ/ε₀ (2m)

- Take a small pillbox Gaussian surface straddling the surface, flat faces parallel to it. (½m)
- Field inside the conductor = 0, and field outside is normal to the surface, so flux only through the outer face: φ = E·ΔS. (½m)
- Charge enclosed = σ·ΔS. By Gauss's law E·ΔS = σΔS/ε₀. (½m)
- **E = σ/ε₀**, directed normal to the surface. (½m)

---

### Q10. Two charges of dielectric medium: how does force change? (2m)

- In vacuum: F₀ = (1/4πε₀)·(q₁q₂/r²). (½m)
- In a medium of dielectric constant K: F = (1/4πε₀K)·(q₁q₂/r²). (1m)
- Therefore F = F₀/K — the medium reduces the force by a factor K. (½m)

### Q11. Sketch field lines for (i) a single +q, (ii) a dipole (2m)

- [ ] Dipole (+q and −q): curved lines starting on +q, ending on −q; symmetric about the dipole axis; denser between the charges.
- [ ] Arrows on every line showing direction (out of +, into −).
- Earns: ~1m each diagram. Key marks: arrows present and pointing correctly; lines never crossing.

### Q12. Distinguish stable and unstable equilibrium of a dipole (2m)

- **Stable (1m):** θ = 0° (p parallel to E). U = −pE is minimum; a small displacement produces a restoring torque.
- **Unstable (1m):** θ = 180° (p antiparallel to E). U = +pE is maximum; a small displacement produces a torque that turns the dipole further away.

---

### Q13. Effect of doubling the Gaussian surface radius on flux (2m)

- Flux depends only on enclosed charge: φ = q_enclosed/ε₀. (1m)
- Doubling the radius (no new charge enclosed) leaves q_enclosed unchanged. (½m)
- ∴ The flux **remains unchanged**. (The field E at the surface does change as 1/r², but the larger area compensates exactly.) (½m)

---

### Q14. What is the net flux through a surface enclosing a dipole? Explain. (2m)
<!-- id: ch01-sa-q14 -->

- A dipole consists of +q and −q, so the total charge enclosed = +q + (−q) = 0. (1m)
- By Gauss's law φ = q_enclosed/ε₀ = 0. (½m)
- The number of field lines entering equals the number leaving → net flux is **zero**. (½m)

---

### Q15. Why does charge reside only on the outer surface of a hollow conductor? (3m)

- The field inside the conductor material is zero in equilibrium. (1m)
- Draw a Gaussian surface within the conducting material enclosing the cavity: since E = 0 there, the flux is zero, so the enclosed charge is zero. (1m)
- Hence no charge can stay on the inner surface (if cavity is empty) and all excess charge must lie on the **outer surface**. (1m)

---

### Q16. Two large parallel plates with charges +σ and −σ: find the field in the three regions (3m)
<!-- id: ch01-sa-q16 -->

- Each sheet alone produces E = σ/2ε₀. (½m)
- **Outside the plates (left of + / right of −):** the two fields are equal and opposite → **E = 0**. (1m)
- **Between the plates:** the two fields add up → **E = σ/ε₀**, directed from the + plate to the − plate. (1m)
- This uniform field is the basis of a parallel-plate capacitor. (½m)

**Diagram (draw it):**
- [ ] Two vertical plates marked +σ and −σ
- [ ] Region labels: E = 0 outside, E = σ/ε₀ between (arrow + → −)

---

### Q17. A point charge q is at one corner of a cube. What is the flux through the cube? (2m)

- A charge at a corner is shared by 8 cubes meeting there, so the cube "sees" only 1/8 of the total flux. (1m)
- Total flux of the charge = q/ε₀, hence flux through this cube = **q/8ε₀**. (1m)

---

### Q18. State two properties of electric field lines (2m)

Any two of the following (1m each):
- They start on positive charges and end on negative charges (or at infinity); they are continuous, never broken in a charge-free region.
- They never intersect (the field has a unique direction at each point).
- The tangent at any point gives the direction of E; closer spacing means stronger field.
- They are always normal to the surface of a conductor.
- Electrostatic field lines do not form closed loops.
