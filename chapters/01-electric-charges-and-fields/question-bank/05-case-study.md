### Q1. Case study — Gauss's law and the spherical shell (4m)

**Passage:** Gauss's law states that the net electric flux through a closed surface is q_enclosed/ε₀ and is independent of the size or shape of the surface. It is most powerful for charge distributions with symmetry. A uniformly charged thin spherical shell of radius R carries total charge Q. The field it produces is the same as that of a point charge at the centre for all external points, while inside the shell the field is zero.

**(i) The electric field at a point inside the shell (r < R) is: (1m)**
(A) Q/4πε₀r² (B) Q/4πε₀R² (C) zero (D) σ/ε₀
**Answer: (C) zero** — the enclosed charge for any interior Gaussian sphere is zero.

**(ii) Just outside the shell (r = R), the field magnitude equals: (1m)**
(A) σ/2ε₀ (B) σ/ε₀ (C) zero (D) 2σ/ε₀
**Answer: (B) σ/ε₀**, where σ = Q/4πR². (Same as Q/4πε₀R².)

**(iii) If the radius of the Gaussian surface (drawn outside, r > R) is doubled, the flux through it: (1m)**
(A) doubles (B) halves (C) is unchanged (D) becomes one-fourth
**Answer: (C) unchanged** — flux = Q/ε₀ depends only on enclosed charge.

**(iv) Sketch and describe how E varies with r for the shell. (1m)**
- E = 0 for r < R; E = σ/ε₀ (maximum) at r = R; E ∝ 1/r² for r > R.
- **Graph to draw:** flat zero line up to R, a jump to the peak at R, then a 1/r² decay; axes labelled E vs r.

### Q2. Case study — Electric dipole in a uniform field (4m)

**Passage:** An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges +q and −q separated by a small distance 2a, with dipole moment p = q(2a) directed from −q to +q. When placed in a uniform external field E at an angle θ to the field, the dipole experiences no net force but a torque τ = pE sinθ tends to align it with the field. The potential energy is U = −pE cosθ.

**(i) The net force on the dipole in a uniform field is: (1m)**
(A) pE (B) 2qE (C) zero (D) pE sinθ
**Answer: (C) zero** — the +qE and −qE forces are equal and opposite.

**(ii) The torque is maximum when θ equals: (1m)**
(A) 0° (B) 45° (C) 90° (D) 180°
**Answer: (C) 90°** — τ = pE sinθ is greatest at sinθ = 1.

**(iii) The dipole is in stable equilibrium when: (1m)**
(A) θ = 0° (B) θ = 90° (C) θ = 180° (D) θ = 45°
**Answer: (A) θ = 0°** — U = −pE is minimum, so any displacement gives a restoring torque.

**(iv) Find the work done to rotate the dipole from stable to unstable equilibrium. (1m)**
W = pE(cosθ₁ − cosθ₂) = pE(cos0° − cos180°) = pE(1 − (−1)) = **2pE.**

### Q3. Case study — Coulomb's law and the dielectric medium (4m)

**Passage:** Coulomb's law gives the force between two point charges as F = q₁q₂/4πε₀r² in vacuum. When the charges are immersed in a medium, the force is reduced by a factor equal to the dielectric constant (relative permittivity) K of the medium: F_medium = F_vacuum/K. Water has a high K (≈ 80), which is why ionic solids dissolve readily in it. Charge is also quantised: any charge is an integer multiple of e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C.

**(i) Two charges separated by r in a medium of K = 4 feel a force F. In vacuum (same r), the force would be: (1m)**
(A) F/4 (B) 4F (C) F (D) 16F
**Answer: (B) 4F** — vacuum force = K × medium force = 4F.

**(ii) Which of these cannot be the charge on a body? (1m)**
(A) 3.2×10⁻¹⁹ C (B) 4.8×10⁻¹⁹ C (C) 2.0×10⁻¹⁹ C (D) 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C
**Answer: (C) 2.0×10⁻¹⁹ C** — it is 1.25e, not an integer multiple of e.

**(iii) Coulomb force between two charges obeys Newton's third law because: (1m)**
(A) the forces are unequal (B) the forces are equal, opposite, along the line joining them (C) one charge is larger (D) the medium absorbs force
**Answer: (B)** — equal-and-opposite collinear forces form an action-reaction pair.

**(iv) Two point charges 2×10⁻⁷ C and 3×10⁻⁷ C are 30 cm apart in vacuum. Find the force. (1m)**
F = k q₁q₂/r² = (9×10⁹)(2×10⁻⁷)(3×10⁻⁷)/(0.30)² = **6×10⁻³ N (repulsive).**
