# Chapter 2 — Electrostatic Potential & Capacitance: Case-Based (Section D)

> Section D case studies (4m each: a short passage + 4 sub-parts of 1m, last part often a choice). Read the passage, then answer each sub-part with formula → substitution → result.

### Q1. Case study — Parallel-plate capacitor and dielectric (4m)
**Passage:** A parallel-plate capacitor consists of two conducting plates of area A separated by distance d, holding charges +Q and −Q. The capacitance in vacuum is C₀ = ε₀A/d. When a dielectric slab of constant K is fully inserted, the bound charges polarise and reduce the net field between the plates, raising the capacitance to C = Kε₀A/d. Whether the battery stays connected or is removed before insertion changes which quantity is held fixed.

**(i) On what factors does the capacitance of a parallel-plate capacitor depend? (1m)**
- C = Kε₀A/d ⇒ depends on plate area A (↑), separation d (↓), and dielectric constant K (↑). It does NOT depend on Q or V.

**(ii) A 10 pF air capacitor is filled with a dielectric K = 4. What is its new capacitance? (1m)**
- C = K·C₀ = 4 × 10 pF = **40 pF.**

**(iii) The capacitor is charged to 200 V then DISCONNECTED, and the dielectric is inserted. What happens to the voltage? (1m)**
- Q fixed; C → KC, so V = Q/C falls to V₀/K = 200/4 = **50 V** (field also drops to E₀/4).

**(iv) If instead the battery had stayed CONNECTED during insertion, what happens to the stored energy? (1m)**
- V fixed; U = ½CV² rises by factor K. New U = K·U₀ = **4× the original energy** (extra energy supplied by the battery).

> Mark note: 1m per sub-part; sub-parts (iii) and (iv) test the "battery connected vs disconnected" distinction — the examiner's favourite trap.

### Q2. Case study — Electric potential, equipotential surfaces and E = −dV/dr (4m)
**Passage:** Electric potential V at a point is the work done per unit charge to bring a positive test charge from infinity to that point. It is a scalar, so potentials from several charges add algebraically. Surfaces of constant potential are called equipotential surfaces; the field is always perpendicular to them and points toward decreasing potential, expressed as E = −dV/dr. No work is done in moving a charge along an equipotential surface.

**(i) Why is no work done in moving a charge over an equipotential surface? (1m)**
- W = q(V_A − V_B); on the surface V_A = V_B so W = 0. (Equivalently, E ⟂ surface ⇒ force ⟂ displacement.)

**(ii) Two point charges +q and −q form a dipole. What is the potential at any point on the equatorial line? (1m)**
- The two charges are equidistant from any equatorial point, so their scalar potentials cancel: **V = 0.**

**(iii) In a region V = 4x (volts, x in m). Find the electric field. (1m)**
- E = −dV/dx = −d(4x)/dx = **−4 V/m** (4 V/m directed along −x).

**(iv) The potential at a point due to a point charge is 90 V at 2 m. Find the charge. (1m)**
- V = kq/r ⇒ q = Vr/k = (90 × 2)/(9×10⁹) = **2×10⁻⁸ C.**
